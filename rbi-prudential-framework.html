Part I

Genesis: a framework born from a quashed circular

The 2019 Framework cannot be read without its predecessor. It is, in substance, the Reserve Bank's second attempt at the same idea after the Supreme Court struck the first attempt down. The story explains both the Framework's design and its unusually careful drafting.

By the middle of the last decade, the Reserve Bank had cycled through a long succession of restructuring schemes, each one a partial fix layered over the failures of the last. Corporate Debt Restructuring, the Joint Lenders' Forum, Strategic Debt Restructuring, the Scheme for Sustainable Structuring of Stressed Assets. The pattern was always the same: a scheme would offer regulatory forbearance, lenders would use that forbearance to evergreen rather than resolve, and the stock of hidden stress would grow.

2001 TO 2017
The age of schemes

CDR, JLF, Flexible Structuring, SDR, S4A and Change in Ownership outside SDR proliferated. Each carried its own asset-classification dispensations. The cumulative effect was a regulatory architecture that rewarded delay and disguised the true scale of non-performing assets.

12 FEB 2018
The 12 February Circular

The Reserve Bank swept away every prior scheme in a single stroke and replaced them with one harsh rule: recognise default on day one, resolve within 180 days, and on failure refer the borrower to the IBC. It was issued under sections 35A, 35AA and 35AB of the Banking Regulation Act, 1949.

2 APR 2019
Dharani Sugars and Chemicals v Union of India

The Supreme Court held the 12 February Circular ultra vires. The Reserve Bank may direct banks to initiate insolvency against a specific borrower only with prior Central Government authorisation under section 35AA, and only borrower by borrower. A blanket direction covering all defaulters above a threshold was beyond power. Every insolvency proceeding founded on the Circular was declared non est.

4 APR 2019
The Governor's statement

Within two days the Reserve Bank announced it would issue a revised circular to fill the void, retaining the discipline of the 12 February Circular while curing its constitutional defect.

7 JUN 2019
The Prudential Framework

The revised circular issued. The crucial design change: the mandatory IBC reference was dropped. Reference to the IBC became one resolution option among several, not a compulsion. The Framework instead disciplines behaviour through provisioning, not through a forced insolvency trigger.

Why the redesign matters

The 12 February Circular failed because it tried to compel insolvency without the borrower-specific authorisation that section 35AA requires. The 2019 Framework achieves nearly the same resolution discipline by a different lever. It makes inaction expensive. A lender that does not implement a resolution plan within the timelines must hold escalating additional provisions. That is a direction on prudential treatment, squarely within the Reserve Bank's settled regulatory power, and so it is immune from the Dharani Sugars objection.

Part II

Object and architecture: recognise, resolve, provision

The Framework is best read as three sequential machines acting on a single timeline. The first forces early recognition. The second prescribes how a resolution plan is built and approved. The third supplies the disincentive that makes the first two work.

Machine 1 Early recognition

The instant a borrower defaults, the lender must classify the account into a special mention account sub-category and report it. Stress is no longer a state a bank can quietly carry; it is an event that must be flagged on the day it occurs and reported to a central repository. This is the surveillance layer.

Machine 2 Time-bound resolution

Once default is reported, a thirty-day review period opens. Lenders decide a resolution strategy, sign an inter-creditor agreement, and, for large exposures, must implement a resolution plan within 180 days of the end of the review period. The resolution plan may be a restructuring, a change in ownership, a sale of the exposure, a recovery action, or a reference to the IBC. The Framework is route-neutral on which path, but route-strict on the clock.

Machine 3 The provisioning lever

If a viable resolution plan is not implemented within the timeline, additional provisions of twenty per cent, then a further fifteen per cent, must be made over and above normal provisioning. This is the engine of the whole instrument. It does not order a lender to do anything; it makes delay so capital-expensive that resolution becomes the rational choice.

"These directions are issued with a view to providing a framework for early recognition, reporting and time bound resolution of stressed assets." Paragraph 4, the stated purpose
Practitioner's note: what kind of instrument is this?

The Framework is a set of regulatory directions to lenders, not a statute creating rights between creditor and debtor. It does not give a bank a power to seize anything; SARFAESI does that. It does not create a collective process; the IBC does that. It governs the lender's internal conduct and capital treatment. The borrower is the object of the Framework, not a party to it. A practitioner advising a borrower watches the Framework to anticipate lender behaviour and the timing of pressure; a practitioner advising a lender treats it as a compliance and provisioning rulebook with hard deadlines.

Part III

Applicability and the reference-date regime

Two questions decide whether the Framework bites with full force: which lender is involved, and how large the exposure is. The reporting obligation reaches widely; the hard 180-day resolution clock reaches only the large exposures.

Para 3 Covered lenders

The Framework applies to scheduled commercial banks other than regional rural banks, the all-India term financial institutions (NABARD, NHB, EXIM Bank and SIDBI), small finance banks, and the larger non-banking financial companies, meaning systemically important non-deposit-taking NBFCs and deposit-taking NBFCs. Wherever the Framework refers to "lenders", it means this set unless a provision says otherwise.

The asymmetry that catches people out

The thirty-day review period and the inter-creditor mechanics are framed around defaults reported "by any of the lenders mentioned at 3(a), 3(b) and 3(c)", that is, banks, term financial institutions and small finance banks. The large NBFCs in 3(d) are covered lenders for reporting and provisioning, but the mandatory review-period trigger keys off the first three categories. Read the operative paragraph against the definition of "lender" each time; the drafting deliberately distinguishes the two.

Para 6 CRILC reporting threshold

Credit information, including special mention account classification, must be reported to the Central Repository of Information on Large Credits for every borrower with aggregate exposure of fifty million rupees and above. The main report is monthly; in addition, a weekly default report is due by the close of business every Friday. This reporting net is wide and shallow, catching mid-sized exposures well below the resolution-timeline thresholds.

Paras 11 to 12 The reference dates

The hard 180-day implementation clock applies only above an aggregate-exposure threshold, phased in by reference date. The reference date is the point from which the obligation to implement a resolution plan within 180 days of the end of the review period begins to run.

Reading the threshold correctly

The bottom tier, below 15 billion rupees, was left "to be announced" and was never separately notified as a date for the mandatory 180-day clock. The practical consequence is layered. For these smaller exposures the binding 180-day resolution-implementation timeline does not switch on automatically, but the rest of the Framework still applies in full: the day-one default recognition, the SMA classification, the CRILC reporting at fifty million and above, and the board-approved resolution policy obligation. The Framework is therefore not "off" for mid-sized accounts; only its sharpest deadline is.

Part IV · Footnotes and operative paragraphs

The defined terms: where the timeline is actually set

The Framework hides its most important definitions in footnotes and short clauses. Default starts every clock; liquidation value protects the dissenter; financial difficulty is the gateway to the restructuring norms. Master these before reading the operative machinery.

Default
Footnote 2

Non-payment of a debt, as defined under the IBC, when the whole or any instalment has become due and payable and is not paid. For revolving facilities like cash credit, it also means the balance staying continuously over the limit or drawing power for more than thirty days. This single event starts the SMA clock and the review period.

Lenders
Para 3, footnote 1

The covered set: SCBs (excluding RRBs), the four AIFIs, small finance banks, and systemically important non-deposit-taking and deposit-taking NBFCs. "Unless specified otherwise" is the operative caveat; several paragraphs narrow it to the first three categories.

Special Mention Account
Paras 6 to 7

The early-stress flag. SMA-0 (overdue 1 to 30 days), SMA-1 (31 to 60), SMA-2 (61 to 90). For revolving credit only SMA-1 and SMA-2 apply. Classification is automatic on the ageing of the overdue, not discretionary.

Review Period
Para 9

The thirty-day window opening on the first reported default, within which lenders undertake a prima facie review and decide the resolution strategy. The 180-day implementation clock runs from the end of this period.

Resolution Plan (RP)
Para 13

Any action, plan or reorganisation, including regularisation by payment of overdues, sale of the exposure, change in ownership, or restructuring. It must be clearly documented even where no term changes. The RP is route-neutral; the Framework cares that one exists and is implemented on time.

Inter-Creditor Agreement (ICA)
Para 10

The contract binding multiple lenders to a common resolution. A decision by lenders holding 75 per cent by value and 60 per cent by number binds all. Dissenting lenders must receive not less than liquidation value. ARCs with exposure must also sign.

Liquidation value
Footnote 6

The estimated realisable value of the borrower's assets if it were liquidated as on the date the review period commenced. It is the statutory floor for what a dissenting lender must be paid under an RP, and the conceptual bridge to the IBC's own liquidation-value protections.

Restructuring
Footnote 7, Annex 1

A concession granted for economic or legal reasons relating to the borrower's financial difficulty: altered tenor, rate or instalments, rollover, additional credit to cure default, or a compromise settlement where the payment period exceeds three months. Restructuring is the trigger for the Annex 1 prudential norms.

Financial difficulty
Annex 1, paras 2 to 3

The gateway concept for restructuring, drawn from the Basel non-performing-exposure and forbearance guidelines. A non-exhaustive list of indicators, of which actual default is only one. A borrower can be in financial difficulty even with no arrears, on cash-flow and rating signals.

Independent credit evaluation (ICE)
Para 14, Annex 2

An opinion on the residual debt by an RBI-authorised credit rating agency, graded RP1 (highest safety) to RP7. An RP involving restructuring or change of ownership needs an opinion of RP4 or better to be implemented; two ICEs for exposures of five billion and above, one otherwise.

Part V · Section I-A

Stage 1: early identification and reporting of stress

The Framework's surveillance layer is mechanical and unforgiving. The moment a payment ages past due, the account must be slotted into a special mention sub-category and reported. There is no room for managerial judgement at this stage; the only inputs are the amount overdue and the number of days.

Para 6 The SMA grid for term facilities

SMA sub-categoryPrincipal, interest or any amount overdue between
SMA-01 to 30 days
SMA-131 to 60 days
SMA-261 to 90 days

At 90 days the account leaves the SMA grid altogether and becomes a non-performing asset under the income-recognition and asset-classification norms. The SMA sub-categories are therefore a pre-NPA early-warning ladder, not a parallel classification.

Para 7 Revolving facilities

For cash credit and similar revolving facilities, there is no SMA-0. The account is SMA-1 once the outstanding balance stays continuously over the sanctioned limit or drawing power, whichever is lower, for 31 to 60 days, and SMA-2 from 61 to 90 days. The footnote-2 definition of default for these facilities is correspondingly the balance remaining over the limit for more than thirty days.

Para 8 CRILC reporting

For every borrower with aggregate exposure of fifty million rupees and above, the lender files the CRILC-Main Report monthly and, additionally, a weekly report of all defaults by the close of business each Friday (or the preceding working day if Friday is a holiday). The aggregate exposure is computed across all fund-based and non-fund-based exposure, including investment exposure.

Practitioner's note: the reporting net is the real enforcement

The weekly default report is what makes the Framework hard to game. Because every covered lender reports defaults to a shared repository, a borrower's default with one lender is visible to all the others within days. That visibility is what allows the inter-creditor mechanics to engage promptly and what makes selective evergreening across a consortium far harder to sustain. In advising a stressed borrower, assume that a default known to one lender is, within a week, known to the entire lending group.

Part VI · Section I-B and I-C

Stage 2: building and implementing the resolution plan

This is the operative core, and the part a practitioner choreographs. From the first reported default, a sequence of fixed steps and majorities runs to a single deadline. Miss it, and Stage 3 begins.

POLICY
Step 0 · Board-approved policy in place para 9

Every lender must already hold a board-approved stressed-assets policy with resolution timelines before any default arises. Lenders are expected to begin resolution even ahead of a formal default, since default is a lagging indicator of stress.

DAY 0
Step 1 · Default reported, review period opens para 9

Once any covered lender reports the borrower in default, a thirty-day prima facie review period begins. Within it the lenders settle the resolution strategy, including whether to restructure, change ownership, sell, recover, or refer to the IBC.

ICA
Step 2 · Sign the inter-creditor agreement para 10

Where an RP is to be implemented across multiple lenders, all must sign an ICA during the review period. A decision by lenders holding 75 per cent by value and 60 per cent by number binds every lender. Dissenters must be paid not less than liquidation value. ARCs with exposure sign too.

ICE
Step 3 · Independent credit evaluation para 14

For exposures of one billion and above, an RP involving restructuring or change of ownership needs an ICE of the residual debt. Two ICEs at five billion and above, one below. Only an opinion of RP4 or better lets the RP proceed. If more ICEs are obtained than required, all must be RP4 or better.

180
Step 4 · Implement within 180 days para 11

For exposures above the reference-date threshold, the RP must be implemented within 180 days of the end of the review period. "Implemented" has a precise meaning in para 15: documentation executed and security perfected, the new structure reflected in the books of all lenders and the borrower, and the borrower not in default.

EXIT
Step 5 · Or exit by assignment or recovery para 16

Where the RP is an exit, by assigning the exposure to a third party or by recovery action, it is deemed implemented only when the exposure to the borrower is fully extinguished. A half-completed sale does not stop the clock.

Para 15 What "implemented" means

The Framework draws a sharp line between an RP that involves restructuring or change of ownership and one that does not. A non-restructuring RP is implemented only if the borrower is not in default with any lender on the 180th day from the end of the review period; any later default is a fresh default triggering a fresh review. A restructuring or change-of-ownership RP is implemented only when documentation, security perfection, the new capital structure in everyone's books, and the absence of default all coincide.

The 75 and 60 majority, in practice

The dual majority is the single most negotiated number in any consortium workout. Seventy-five per cent by value gives the large lenders effective control; sixty per cent by number prevents a single dominant lender from steamrolling a fragmented tail. The dissenting-lender floor of liquidation value is the safety valve: a minority lender forced into a plan it voted against cannot be left worse off than it would be in a liquidation. In drafting an ICA, the contested clauses are almost always the treatment of differential security, the priority in cash flows, and the exit rights of a dissenter who takes the liquidation-value payout.

Part VII · Section I-D

Stage 3: the provisioning discipline that makes it all work

If Stage 2 is the carrot of an orderly workout, Stage 3 is the stick. The Framework never orders a lender to resolve. It simply makes delay progressively more expensive in capital terms until resolution becomes the only sensible course.

Para 17 The additional provisioning ladder

If a viable RP is not implemented withinAdditional provision (of total outstanding)
180 days from the end of the review period20 per cent
365 days from the commencement of the review periodA further 15 per cent (35 per cent in total)

These additional provisions are over and above the higher of the provisions already held or those required by the account's asset classification, subject to a cap of 100 per cent of total outstanding. Every lender with exposure must make them. Crucially, they are required even where recovery proceedings have begun, unless those proceedings are fully completed.

Practitioner's note: why this lever is constitutionally safe

This is the paragraph that distinguishes the 2019 Framework from the quashed 12 February Circular. The Reserve Bank does not direct any bank to file under the IBC. It prescribes how much capital a bank must set aside if it lets stress drift unresolved. Provisioning treatment is the heartland of prudential regulation, well within sections 21 and 35A of the Banking Regulation Act, and was never in doubt after Dharani Sugars. The behavioural result is similar to a forced resolution, but the legal mechanism is unimpeachable.

Para 21 Reversal of the additional provisions

The additional provisions are not a permanent penalty; they reverse as resolution progresses, and the reversal schedule itself reveals which routes the Reserve Bank treats as genuine resolution.

  • Where the RP is only payment of overdues by the borrower: reversed if the borrower is not in default for six months after clearing the overdues with all lenders.
  • Where the RP is restructuring or change of ownership outside the IBC: reversed upon implementation of the RP.
  • Where resolution is pursued under the IBC: half on filing the insolvency application, and the remaining half on admission of the borrower into the resolution process.
  • Where assignment of debt or recovery proceedings are initiated: reversed upon completion of the assignment or recovery.
Read the reversal schedule as a signal

That the Framework lets a lender reverse half its additional provisions merely on filing an IBC application is a deliberate nudge. It rewards the lender for invoking the collective process early, without compelling it. This is the soft version of what the 12 February Circular tried to mandate. The Framework cannot force the IBC, so it prices it attractively instead.

Part VIII · Annex 1

The restructuring norms: classification, upgrade, valuation, ownership

Annex 1 is where the Framework's prudential teeth are sharpest, and where most of the operational detail lives. It applies to all resolution plans, including those under the IBC. The governing principle is that restructuring is forbearance, and forbearance must be paid for in classification and provisioning.

Para 4 Restructuring downgrades the account

On restructuring, a standard account is immediately downgraded to non-performing, beginning as sub-standard. An account already an NPA keeps its existing classification. In both cases the ageing criteria of the ordinary asset-classification norms continue to run. This is the single most consequential change from the old scheme era, which had allowed restructured accounts to retain standard status. The dispensation is gone.

Paras 5 to 6 Conditions for upgrade

A restructured NPA may be upgraded to standard only after satisfactory performance, meaning no default at any point, through a monitoring period running until at least ten per cent of the outstanding principal and capitalised interest is repaid. No upgrade is possible before one year from the first payment under the longest-moratorium facility. For exposures of one billion and above, upgrade additionally requires an investment-grade rating (BBB- or better), with two ratings at five billion and above.

Para 9 The specified period and re-default

After upgrade, the account remains in a "specified period" until at least twenty per cent of the outstanding principal and capitalised interest is repaid. Any default during the specified period requires a fresh RP and a fresh fifteen per cent additional provision. The Framework thus keeps a recovered account on probation well past the formal upgrade.

Paras 17 to 23 Conversion and valuation

Where restructuring converts principal or unpaid interest into funded interest term loans, debt or equity, those instruments take the same classification as the restructured advance, and their provisioning is the higher of the classification-based provision or the fair-valuation-based provision. The Annex prescribes conservative valuation rules: zero or low coupon bonds where the sinking fund is not built up are collectively valued at one rupee; unquoted equity where no audited balance sheet exists is valued at one rupee per company; the discount factor for the equity discounted-cash-flow method carries a floor of fourteen per cent. The overarching command is conservative cash-flow assessment, with statutory auditors directed to test whether valuations reflect the real risk of loss.

Para 24 Change in ownership and the section 29A bar

Credit facilities may be upgraded to standard on a change in ownership, whether implemented under the IBC or under the Framework, subject to conditions. The acquirer must not be a person disqualified under section 29A of the IBC, must not belong to the existing promoter group, must acquire at least twenty-six per cent of paid-up equity and voting rights as the single largest shareholder, and must be in control as defined under the Companies Act and SEBI regulations.

Practitioner's note: section 29A travels outside the IBC

The most important cross-statutory move in the Annex is that it imports the IBC's section 29A disqualification into a non-IBC restructuring. A defaulting promoter cannot regain a standard-classified company through the back door of an out-of-court change in ownership any more than through a resolution plan in the National Company Law Tribunal. The Framework deliberately closes the arbitrage between the two routes. When advising on a change of ownership outside insolvency, run the full section 29A screen exactly as you would inside it.

Paras 27 to 28 Sale-and-leaseback and refinancing as restructuring

The Annex treats certain disguised restructurings as restructuring for classification purposes. A sale-and-leaseback of a stressed borrower's assets is restructuring where the seller is in financial difficulty, more than half the buyer's revenue from the asset depends on the seller's cash flows, and a quarter or more of the buyer's acquisition funding comes from lenders already exposed to the seller. Borrowings or export advances raised to repay or refinance existing loans, with support from the Indian banking system, are likewise treated as restructuring where the borrower is in financial difficulty.

Para 34 Fraud and wilful default

Borrowers who have committed fraud, malfeasance or wilful default remain ineligible for restructuring. The narrow exception is where existing promoters are wholly replaced and the company is entirely delinked from them; even then, restructuring is without prejudice to continuing criminal action against the former management.

Part IX · Sections II, III, IV

Supervision, disclosure and the carve-outs

The Framework closes with the apparatus that polices it and the categories it deliberately leaves alone. The anti-evergreening provision is its conscience; the carve-outs are where a practitioner checks whether the Framework even applies before doing anything else.

Para 23 The anti-evergreening rule

Any action by a lender intended to conceal the true status of an account or to evergreen stressed accounts attracts stringent supervisory and enforcement action, including higher provisioning and monetary penalties, potentially in addition to a direction to file under the IBC. This is the provision that converts the Framework from a set of timelines into a supervised regime; the deadlines mean little without a credible penalty for disguising the underlying stress.

Para 24 Disclosure

Lenders must disclose implemented resolution plans in the notes on accounts of their financial statements. Resolution is not a private matter between lender and borrower; it surfaces in audited disclosure.

Paras 25 to 28 The carve-outs

  • Restructuring of projects under implementation involving deferment of the date of commencement of commercial operations remains governed by the separate income-recognition and asset-classification master circular, not the Framework's resolution timelines.
  • The resolution-plan implementation provisions do not apply to revival and rehabilitation of MSMEs, which follow their own dedicated instructions.
  • Restructuring after a natural calamity continues to follow extant instructions.
  • The Framework is unavailable where the Reserve Bank has issued a specific section 35AA direction to refer a particular borrower to the IBC; such cases follow that direction.
Check the carve-out before the clock

The project-finance and DCCO carve-out is the one most often overlooked, and it has since become a regime of its own. A construction-stage project loan does not run on the Framework's default-driven 180-day clock; it runs on the project-finance directions, where the trigger is a "credit event" rather than a "default". Confirm the borrower's category, project or non-project, before assuming the Framework's timeline governs. Applying the wrong clock to a project account is a common and costly error.

Part X · Section V and Annex 3

What the Framework swept away

The Framework did not sit alongside the old schemes; it abolished them. Understanding what it replaced explains why its drafting is so suspicious of forbearance and so insistent on hard classification.

With immediate effect, the Framework withdrew the entire prior architecture of stressed-asset schemes: the Framework for Revitalising Distressed Assets, the Corporate Debt Restructuring Scheme, Flexible Structuring of Existing Long Term Project Loans, the Strategic Debt Restructuring Scheme, Change in Ownership outside SDR, and the Scheme for Sustainable Structuring of Stressed Assets. With them went the Joint Lenders' Forum as a mandatory institutional mechanism. Annex 3 lists twenty-eight individual circulars stretching back to 2001 that stood repealed.

The conceptual break

Each repealed scheme carried its own asset-classification dispensation, a regulatory permission to keep a restructured account standard. The Framework's defining choice was to abolish those dispensations and require that restructuring downgrade the account. Where the schemes had treated forbearance as a tool of recovery, the Framework treats it as a recognised loss that must be classified and provisioned. The inter-creditor agreement also replaced the JLF: a contractual majority mechanism in place of a mandatory regulatory forum.

Part XI · The reader's specific doubt

The "Withdrawn" watermark, decoded

The copy of the Framework on the Reserve Bank's website now carries a diagonal "Withdrawn" stamp on every page. This causes understandable alarm, because the Framework is plainly still the live regime for resolution of stressed assets. Both things are true at once, and the reconciliation is a point about RBI's filing practice, not about the law.

Withdrawn

The short answer

"Withdrawn" on an RBI circular does not mean the instruction is dead. It means the standalone circular is no longer the source-of-record document, because its contents have been consolidated into a Master Direction that is now the operative text. The norms continue in force; only their address has changed.

28 Nov 2025 The great consolidation

On 28 November 2025 the Reserve Bank completed a fundamental reorganisation of the instructions administered by its Department of Regulation. It consolidated more than nine thousand circulars and guidelines into a single structured library of function-wise Master Directions, and formally withdrew the 9,445 standalone circulars whose contents had been folded in. The Reserve Bank was explicit that the consolidation collates the instructions on an as-is basis, without changing any existing regulatory requirement, and that the Master Directions now serve as the sole library of regulations administered by the department.

The 7 June 2019 Prudential Framework was one of those standalone circulars. Its substance now lives in the consolidated Master Direction on resolution of stressed assets for commercial banks, issued on the same date as part of the same exercise, which expressly consolidates and harmonises the rules on restructuring, compromise settlements, technical write-offs, DCCO changes for project loans and prudential treatment of stress, in alignment with the IBC. The standalone 2019 circular therefore bears the "Withdrawn" stamp: it is no longer the document you cite, but its rules are intact in their new home.

The three reasons this is not repeal

First, the Reserve Bank stated the consolidation made no change to substantive requirements; it was a filing exercise, not a policy one. Second, the live regime expressly preserves the Framework's machinery: the day-one default recognition, the SMA grid, the review period, the inter-creditor agreement, and the provisioning ladder all survive in the consolidated Direction. Third, the Reserve Bank confirmed that actions and proceedings already initiated under a withdrawn circular continue to be governed by that earlier instruction. A repealed instruction with no successor would do none of these things.

Earlier layer The 2025 project-finance carve-out

There is a second, narrower reason the 2019 text reads as partly superseded even before the November consolidation. In June 2025 the Reserve Bank issued the Project Finance Directions, 2025, which carved the construction-stage project-loan and DCCO terrain out of the Framework and gave it a dedicated regime keyed to a "credit event" rather than a "default". For project finance, the resolution machinery of the Framework is now invoked through those Directions. For everything else, the Framework's own machinery governs, as consolidated.

Practitioner's note: how to cite it now

Treat the 7 June 2019 circular as the historical and conceptual source, and cite it for the design and original wording of the regime. For a current professional opinion or a court filing, cite the operative consolidated Master Direction on resolution of stressed assets as the live instrument, and, for project loans, the Project Finance Directions, 2025. The substance you advise on is unchanged; the authority you point to has moved. Never tell a client the Framework "has been withdrawn" without that explanation, because the unqualified statement is misleading.

Part XII

The interplay: IBC, SARFAESI and the RDB Act

The single most common confusion about the Framework is to treat it as a fourth recovery tool alongside SARFAESI, the RDB Act and the IBC. It is not. It sits at a different layer entirely. It governs the lender's conduct; the others govern the realisation of the debt.

InstrumentWhat it isWhat it does
Prudential FrameworkRBI regulatory direction to lendersForces early recognition, time-bound resolution and escalating provisioning. Creates no creditor remedy against the borrower. The conduct layer.
SARFAESI 2002Self-help enforcement statuteLets a single secured creditor seize and sell collateral without a court. An individual realisation tool. One permitted resolution route within the Framework.
RDB Act 1993Adjudicatory recovery statuteLets a bank prove its claim before a Debts Recovery Tribunal and obtain a recovery certificate. The adjudicated individual-recovery route.
IBC 2016Collective insolvency codeA class-wide, time-bound resolution or liquidation of the whole debtor. The collective route the Framework prices attractively but cannot compel.

Above The Framework as the conduct layer

The Framework tells a lender that, having recognised default, it must choose a resolution route and execute it on time or pay in provisions. The routes it lists in paragraph 13 include sale of the exposure, recovery action and restructuring. SARFAESI enforcement and an RDB Act recovery action are both "recovery actions" the lender may select; a reference to the IBC is another. The Framework is the regime that compels the lender to pick one and move, and that determines the capital cost of dithering.

Across SARFAESI and the RDB Act as chosen routes

When a lender invokes SARFAESI to enforce its security, or files before a Debts Recovery Tribunal, it is acting within the Framework, not outside it. The Framework's only demands are that the recovery be a genuine resolution and that, until the exposure is fully extinguished, the additional provisions continue to be held (paragraph 20). A half-finished SARFAESI auction does not stop the provisioning clock; full extinguishment of the exposure does. The Framework therefore disciplines the timing and seriousness of SARFAESI and RDB action without altering their mechanics.

Below The IBC and the section 35AA boundary

The relationship with the IBC is the most delicate, because it is where the 12 February Circular came to grief. The Framework treats an IBC reference as a resolution option and rewards it through provisioning reversal: half on filing, half on admission. What the Framework cannot do, after Dharani Sugars, is compel a reference. The Reserve Bank may direct a bank to file under the IBC only against a specific borrower and only with prior Central Government authorisation under section 35AA, which is why paragraph 28 carves out borrowers already subject to such a direction. Where an IBC moratorium is in force, it overrides individual SARFAESI and RDB action; the Framework's provisioning machinery accommodates this by letting the additional provisions reverse as the collective process advances.

The cleanest way to hold the four together

Picture a stack. The IBC is the collective destination at the bottom. SARFAESI and the RDB Act are the individual realisation tools in the middle. The Prudential Framework is the layer on top that decides, for each defaulting borrower, how quickly a lender must descend into one of those tools and what it costs to hesitate. SARFAESI asks "can I seize this asset?" The RDB Act asks "can I prove and recover this debt?" The IBC asks "shall we collectively resolve this company?" The Framework asks the prior, regulatory question: "lender, why have you not yet done any of these, and have you provisioned for the delay?"

Part XIII

When to invoke the Framework, and how to run it

The honest answer to "when do I go for the Prudential Framework?" is that a covered lender does not choose to; the Framework engages automatically on default. The real choice is which resolution route to run inside it, and how to sequence the clock. This is the practitioner's operating sequence.

CHECK
Step 1 · Is the borrower inside the Framework at all? paras 3, 25 to 28

Confirm the lender is a covered entity and the borrower is not in a carve-out. Project loans in construction with a DCCO issue run on the Project Finance Directions, not the Framework. MSME revival, natural-calamity restructuring and borrowers under a section 35AA direction sit outside the resolution-timeline machinery.

SIZE
Step 2 · Which clock applies? paras 11 to 12

Above the reference-date threshold, the hard 180-day implementation clock runs and the provisioning ladder is live. Below it, recognition and reporting still apply, but the binding deadline is softer. Size the aggregate exposure across all covered lenders before promising the borrower any timeline.

ALIGN
Step 3 · Build the inter-creditor majority para 10

Map the lending group by value and by number. Identify whether 75 per cent by value and 60 per cent by number is achievable, and which lenders are likely dissenters. Draft the ICA around the contested clauses: differential security, cash-flow priority, and the dissenter's liquidation-value exit.

ROUTE
Step 4 · Choose the resolution route para 13

Restructure where the business is viable and the promoter is clean; change ownership where viability needs a new promoter and section 29A is satisfied; sell or assign to an ARC where the lender wants out; enforce under SARFAESI or the RDB Act where the security is good and speed matters; refer to the IBC where collective resolution or the provisioning reversal on filing is the better course.

COST
Step 5 · Price the delay paras 17 to 21

Model the additional provisions: 20 per cent at the 180-day mark, a further 15 per cent at 365 days from the review-period start. Weigh that capital cost against the value uplift of waiting for a better resolution. The reversal schedule favours early IBC filing, which often tilts the analysis.

CLOSE
Step 6 · Implement to the precise standard paras 15 to 16

Implementation is not signature; it is documentation executed, security perfected, the new structure in everyone's books, and no subsisting default, or, for an exit, full extinguishment of the exposure. Anything short of that leaves the clock running and the provisions in place.

The lender-side pre-flight checklist

  1. Is there a current board-approved stressed-assets policy with resolution timelines in place (para 9)?
  2. Has the default been classified into the correct SMA sub-category and reported to CRILC, including the weekly default report (paras 6 to 8)?
  3. Has the thirty-day review period been correctly dated from the first reported default (para 9)?
  4. Is the ICA signed by all lenders, including any exposed ARC, with the dual majority and the dissenter floor recorded (para 10)?
  5. For exposures of one billion and above, has the ICE of RP4 or better been obtained, with two ICEs above five billion (para 14)?
  6. On a change of ownership, has the acquirer been screened against section 29A of the IBC and the promoter-group test (para 24)?
  7. Has the additional provisioning been correctly computed and held, and is the reversal trigger documented (paras 17 to 21)?
  8. Is the resolution being implemented to the precise paragraph 15 standard, not merely agreed in principle?

Part XIV

Glossary of working abbreviations

SMA

Special Mention Account. The pre-NPA early-warning ladder (SMA-0, 1, 2) keyed to days overdue, classified automatically on ageing.

CRILC

Central Repository of Information on Large Credits. The shared repository to which covered lenders report exposures of fifty million and above, monthly, plus a weekly default report.

RP

Resolution Plan. Any documented action resolving stress: restructuring, change of ownership, sale, recovery, or IBC reference.

ICA

Inter-Creditor Agreement. The contract binding multiple lenders to a common RP; a 75 per cent value and 60 per cent number majority binds all.

ICE

Independent Credit Evaluation. A rating-agency opinion (RP1 to RP7) on the residual debt; RP4 or better is required for an RP to be implemented.

DCCO

Date of Commencement of Commercial Operations. The project-finance milestone whose deferment is carved out of the Framework and governed by separate directions.

FITL

Funded Interest Term Loan. An instrument created by converting unpaid interest, taking the same classification as the restructured advance.

Section 29A

The IBC's promoter-disqualification provision, imported by the Framework into non-IBC changes of ownership to close the arbitrage between routes.

Section 35AA

The Banking Regulation Act power letting RBI direct a bank to refer a specific borrower to the IBC, only with Central Government authorisation. The provision Dharani Sugars turned on.

Master Direction

The consolidated, function-wise instrument that, since the November 2025 consolidation, houses the live text of the resolution-of-stressed-assets regime.
