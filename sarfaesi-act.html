Part I

Genesis: why a self-help statute had to exist

Before 2002, a bank that wanted to realise its mortgage had only one honest route: file a civil suit, obtain a decree, and then chase execution. Recovery routinely took a decade or more. SARFAESI was Parliament's answer to a banking system drowning in non-performing assets it could not lawfully touch.

The Indian recovery architecture evolved in three distinct waves. Each was a response to the failure of the one before it. Understanding this lineage is essential, because SARFAESI is not a standalone idea; it sits on top of the Recovery of Debts due to Banks and Financial Institutions Act, 1993 (the RDB Act, which created the Debts Recovery Tribunals) and below the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016 (the IBC).

PRE-1993
The civil-suit era

Secured creditors enforced mortgages through ordinary suits under the Transfer of Property Act and the Code of Civil Procedure. Decrees were slow, execution slower, and the cost of money locked in litigation crippled the banking system.

1991
Narasimham Committee I

The Committee on the Financial System recommended specialised tribunals for debt recovery, leading directly to the RDB Act and the Debts Recovery Tribunals (DRTs).

1993
The RDB Act & the DRTs

Specialised tribunals were created to adjudicate bank claims faster than civil courts. They helped, but they were still adjudicatory: a bank had to prove its claim and obtain a recovery certificate before it could touch the asset.

1998
Narasimham Committee II & the Andhyarujina Committee

Both bodies recommended that banks be empowered to enforce security without the intervention of courts, drawing on practice in other jurisdictions where a secured creditor can take possession on default. This is the conceptual seed of SARFAESI: the move from adjudication to self-help.

2002
The Ordinance, then the Act

The mechanism was first introduced by Ordinance (twice), then enacted as Act 54 of 2002. It received assent on 17 December 2002 but, by deeming fiction in section 1(3), is treated as having come into force on 21 June 2002. Section 42 repealed the Second Ordinance of 2002 while saving everything done under it.

2004–2013
Early amendments after Mardia

Act 30 of 2004 inserted the borrower's representation right (s.13(3A)) and recast the borrower's remedy after the Supreme Court's intervention in Mardia Chemicals. Act 1 of 2013 brought multi-state cooperative banks within the fold, reduced the joint-financing consent threshold, and added s.13(5A)–(5C) and s.31A.

2016
The structural overhaul — Act 44 of 2016

The single most consequential amendment. It renamed securitisation/reconstruction companies as asset reconstruction companies (ARCs), created a pan-India Central Registry regime, inserted Chapter IVA giving registered secured creditors statutory priority over Government dues, tightened the magistrate's timeline under s.14, and added a penalty code (s.30A–30D). It also simultaneously amended the RDB Act, the Indian Stamp Act and the Depositories Act.

◈ Amendment watch

Throughout this treatise, "the principal Act" means SARFAESI 2002 as it originally stood; "post-2016" refers to the text as substituted by the Enforcement of Security Interest and Recovery of Debts Laws and Miscellaneous Provisions (Amendment) Act, 2016 (Act 44 of 2016), most provisions of which took effect 1 September 2016, with the Central Registry provisions effective 24 January 2020.

Part II

Object & architecture: three machines in one statute

SARFAESI is best understood not as a single remedy but as three connected machines. Two of them deal with moving bad loans off bank balance sheets; the third deals with realising the underlying collateral.

Machine 1 Securitisation

The packaging of financial assets (loans, receivables) into tradeable instruments. An ARC acquires a pool of financial assets and funds the acquisition by issuing security receipts to qualified buyers, each receipt representing an undivided interest in the pool. The cash flows from the underlying loans service the receipts.

Machine 2 Asset reconstruction

The acquisition of a bank's right or interest in a loan by an ARC for the purpose of realising it. Once acquired, the ARC may reschedule the debt, settle it, take over the borrower's management, sell the business, or convert debt to equity. This is recovery by restructuring, run by a specialist entity rather than the originating bank.

Machine 3 Enforcement of security interest

The headline power, and the part of the Act that practitioners live and breathe. Section 13 lets any secured creditor (a bank, financial institution, ARC, or qualifying debenture trustee) enforce its security without the intervention of court or tribunal, by issuing a demand notice and, on default continuing past 60 days, taking possession of and selling the collateral.

"Any security interest created in favour of any secured creditor may be enforced, without the intervention of court or tribunal, by such creditor in accordance with the provisions of this Act." Section 13(1), the engine of the Act
▣ Practitioner's note — the non-obstante spine

Three overriding clauses give the Act its teeth. Section 13(1) overrides ss. 69 and 69A of the Transfer of Property Act. Section 34 bars the civil court from entertaining any matter the DRT is empowered to decide, and forbids injunctions against SARFAESI action. Section 35 gives the Act overriding effect over any inconsistent law. Read together, they shut the borrower out of the civil court and channel every grievance to the DRT under section 17.

Part III

Applicability, extent & the carve-outs that bite

The Act extends to the whole of India and is deemed in force from 21 June 2002. But its reach is policed by two filters: a set of subject-matter exclusions in section 31, and monetary thresholds below which a creditor simply cannot invoke it.

s. 1 Short title, extent & commencement

It applies across India. The deeming of commencement to 21 June 2002 matters because it predates the assent date, giving retrospective coverage to action taken under the preceding Ordinances.

s. 31 Where the Act does not apply

Section 31 is a practitioner's first checklist. If the security falls in any of these baskets, SARFAESI is unavailable and the creditor must fall back on the RDB Act or a civil suit:

  • A lien on goods, money or security under the Contract Act or the Sale of Goods Act.
  • A pledge of movables within section 172 of the Contract Act.
  • Security in an aircraft (Aircraft Act, 1934) or a vessel (Merchant Shipping Act, 1958).
  • Clause (e) — omitted by the 2016 Amendment, because hire-purchase and lease of tangible assets were drawn into the Act's definition of security interest.
  • Any rights of an unpaid seller under section 47 of the Sale of Goods Act.
  • Properties not liable to attachment under the first proviso to s. 60(1) CPC (with the carve-back for property specifically charged).
  • Security interest securing a financial asset not exceeding ₹1 lakh.
  • Any security interest created in agricultural land.
  • Any case where the amount due is less than 20% of the principal plus interest.
⚠ Pitfall — the agricultural-land trap

The agricultural-land exclusion is litigated relentlessly. Borrowers routinely claim possession proceedings are void because the secured asset is "agricultural." Courts look to actual use, not merely revenue records: land classified as agricultural but used for non-agricultural purposes, or converted, may still fall within SARFAESI. Verify use, classification and conversion status before issuing the s.13(2) notice, because a successful agricultural-land plea unwinds the entire enforcement.

s. 31A Power to exempt classes of banks/FIs

Inserted by Act 1 of 2013. The Central Government may, in the public interest, exempt a class of banks or financial institutions from any provision, or apply it with modifications. Post-2016, every such notification must be laid in draft before both Houses of Parliament for 30 days, giving the legislature a veto.

Part IV · Section 2

The defined terms: where most disputes are actually won

Definitions are not preliminaries to skim. In SARFAESI litigation, the threshold fight is almost always whether a thing is a financial asset, whether an account is an NPA, or whether a party is a secured creditor. Master these and the operative sections fall into place.

Borrower
s.2(1)(f)

Anyone granted financial assistance, who has given a guarantee, or who has created a mortgage or pledge as security. Critically, this includes guarantors and third-party mortgagors. Post-2016 it also covers a person who has raised funds through debt securities; from 2021 it extends to a pooled investment vehicle.

Default
s.2(1)(j)

Non-payment of any debt by the borrower upon which the account is classified as an NPA in the secured creditor's books. A second limb (2016) covers default on debt securities after a 90-day notice by the debenture trustee. NPA classification is the condition precedent to a s.13(2) notice.

Non-performing asset (NPA)
s.2(1)(o)

An account classified as sub-standard, doubtful or loss. If the lender is governed by a statutory authority, classification follows that authority's norms; otherwise it follows RBI directions. The classification must be valid, or the whole enforcement is vulnerable.

Financial asset
s.2(1)(l)

Debt or receivables, and a wide list of secured/unsecured claims, beneficial interests, and (post-2016) interests in tangible/intangible assets given on hire, financial lease or conditional sale. The breadth here is what lets ARCs buy almost any loan exposure.

Financial assistance
s.2(1)(k)

Any loan, advance, debenture, bond, guarantee, letter of credit or other credit facility. Post-2016 it expressly includes funds for acquiring assets on hire/lease and the purchase of debt securities.

Security interest
s.2(1)(zf)

Any right, title or interest of any kind (other than those in s.31) created upon property in favour of a secured creditor: mortgage, charge, hypothecation, assignment. Post-2016 it expressly captures interests retained over tangible/intangible assets on hire, lease or conditional sale.

Secured creditor
s.2(1)(zd)

A bank or FI (or a consortium), a debenture trustee, an ARC (whether acting as such or managing a securitisation/reconstruction trust), a Board-registered debenture trustee for secured debt securities, or any trustee holding securities on behalf of a bank/FI, in whose favour security interest is created.

Asset reconstruction
s.2(1)(b)

Acquisition by an ARC of any right or interest of a bank/FI in financial assistance, for the purpose of realisation of that assistance.

Asset reconstruction company (ARC)
s.2(1)(ba)

A company registered with the RBI under section 3 to carry on the business of asset reconstruction or securitisation, or both. Post-2016 nomenclature; previously "securitisation company / reconstruction company."

Securitisation
s.2(1)(z)

Acquisition of financial assets by an ARC from an originator, whether by raising funds from qualified buyers through issue of security receipts representing an undivided interest, or otherwise.

Qualified buyer
s.2(1)(u)

An FI, insurer, bank, state financial/industrial corporation, trustee, ARC, asset management company, FII, or any RBI-specified non-institutional investor. Post-2016 renamed from "qualified institutional buyer." Only QBs may subscribe to security receipts.

Security receipt
s.2(1)(zg)

A receipt or security issued by an ARC to a qualified buyer under a scheme, evidencing an undivided right, title or interest in the financial asset involved in securitisation.

Obligor
s.2(1)(q)

A person liable to the originator to pay a financial asset or discharge an obligation in respect of it. Includes the borrower. Relevant to the s.6 notice machinery.

Originator
s.2(1)(r)

The owner of a financial asset which is acquired by an ARC for securitisation or reconstruction. Typically the lending bank.

Hypothecation
s.2(1)(n)

A charge upon movable property, present or future, created without delivery of possession, as security; includes a floating charge and its crystallisation into a fixed charge.

Debt
s.2(1)(ha)

As in s.2(g) of the RDB Act, expanded post-2016 to include the unpaid purchase price of tangible assets on hire/lease/conditional sale and rights over intangible assets securing such obligations.

Debt securities
s.2(1)(ia)

Debt securities listed under SEBI regulations. Inserted in 2016 to bring debenture trustees and listed debt within the enforcement architecture.

Financial lease
s.2(1)(ma)

A lease of a tangible asset (not a negotiable instrument/document) where the lessee becomes owner at the end of the term or on paying the residual. Inserted in 2016.

▣ Practitioner's note — s.2(2): the borrowed dictionary

Section 2(2) imports definitions from the Contract Act 1872, the Transfer of Property Act 1882, the Companies Act and the SEBI Act for any word not defined here. So "mortgage," "charge," "pledge" and "company" carry their meanings from those parent statutes. Never read a SARFAESI definition in isolation.

Part V · Chapter II (ss. 3–12B)

Regulation of ARCs: licensing, acquisition & the RBI's grip

Chapter II builds and polices the asset reconstruction industry. It is a licensing and prudential regime: who may run an ARC, on what conditions, how they acquire assets, and the wide supervisory powers the RBI holds over them.

s. 3 Registration of ARCs

No company may carry on securitisation or asset reconstruction without an RBI certificate of registration and a minimum net owned fund (NOF). The conditions the RBI must be satisfied of include: no losses in the three preceding financial years; adequate arrangements to realise assets and pay/redeem qualified buyers; directors with adequate experience in finance, securitisation and reconstruction; no director convicted of an offence involving moral turpitude; a sponsor who is "fit and proper"; and compliance with prudential norms and RBI guidelines. Any substantial change in management (including appointing a director, MD or CEO), change of registered office, or change of name needs prior RBI approval.

◈ Amendment watch — the ₹2 crore vs ₹100 crore puzzle

Your class notes say "₹100 crore"; the bare Act says "₹2 crore." Both are right, at different layers. The 2016 Amendment substituted s.3(1)(b) to read "net owned fund of not less than two crore rupees or such other higher amount as the Reserve Bank may, by notification, specify." The RBI then exercised that power and, by notification, raised the minimum NOF for ARCs to ₹100 crore. So ₹2 crore is the statutory floor; ₹100 crore is the operative regulatory requirement. In an exam or a brief, cite both layers — the floor and the RBI uplift.

s. 4 Cancellation of certificate of registration

The RBI may cancel registration where the ARC ceases the business, ceases to hold qualified-buyer investment, fails the s.3 conditions, defaults on RBI directions, fails to maintain accounts, refuses inspection, or fails to obtain a required prior approval. An opportunity of being heard is mandatory before cancellation. An appeal lies to the Central Government within 30 days. Crucially, even after cancellation, the company is deemed to remain an ARC until it repays the entire investment held from qualified buyers, and may take no fresh investment in the interim.

s. 5 Acquisition of rights or interest in financial assets

An ARC acquires a bank's financial assets either by issuing a debenture/bond on agreed terms, or by agreement for transfer. On acquisition, the ARC is deemed to be the lender and all the bank's rights vest in it. Post-2016, the document of transfer is exempt from stamp duty (via the inserted s.8F of the Indian Stamp Act), provided the acquisition is for reconstruction or securitisation. Pending suits do not abate; the ARC may, with the originator's consent, apply to substitute its name in any pending suit, appeal or proceeding (s.5(5)).

s. 5A Transfer of pending applications to one DRT

Where an acquired asset comprises secured debts of more than one bank with applications pending before different DRTs, the ARC may apply to the relevant Appellate Tribunal (DRAT) to consolidate all pending applications before a single DRT. The DRAT's order binds all the DRTs concerned. Inserted by Act 30 of 2004 to prevent fragmented recovery litigation.

s. 6 Notice to obligor & discharge

On acquisition, the bank may (discretionary) notify the obligor, any concerned person and the registering authority (including the Registrar of Companies). Once notified, the obligor must (mandatory) pay the ARC, and such payment fully discharges the obligor. If no notice is given and the bank later receives money, it holds it in trust for the ARC.

s. 7 Issue of security by raising receipts/funds

An ARC may, after acquisition, offer security receipts to qualified buyers (post-2016, also to RBI-specified non-institutional investors). Funds are held in separate, scheme-wise accounts, and realisations from a scheme's assets service that scheme's receipts. A scheme may be structured as a trust managed by the ARC (s.7(2A)). If assets are not realised, QBs holding not less than 75% of the value of the receipts under a scheme may call a binding meeting (s.7(3)).

s. 8 Exemption from registration of security receipt

A security receipt (and any transfer of it) does not require compulsory registration under s.17 of the Registration Act, 1908, unless it actually creates an interest in immovable property beyond an undivided interest already covered by a registered instrument.

s. 9 Measures for asset reconstruction

Substituted entirely in 2016. An ARC may, for reconstruction, deploy any one or more of: proper management of the borrower's business by change/takeover of management; sale or lease of part or whole of the business; rescheduling debt; enforcement of security interest; settlement of dues; taking possession of secured assets; and conversion of debt into shares (with a deeming proviso that any such past conversion is always valid). The RBI sets policy and the ARC must act within it.

s. 10 Other functions of an ARC

An ARC may act as an agent for a bank/FI to recover dues, as a manager of secured assets, or as a receiver if appointed by a court or tribunal, on mutually agreed fees. It may not act as a manager if doing so creates a pecuniary liability. Without prior RBI approval it may carry on no business other than securitisation or asset reconstruction; any pre-existing other business must cease within one year of commencement. An ARC's subsidiary is excluded from the definition.

s. 11 Resolution of disputes

Disputes about securitisation, reconstruction or amounts due, arising among a bank/FI, ARC and qualified buyer, are to be settled by conciliation or arbitration under the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996 — as if the parties had consented in writing.

ss. 12, 12A, 12B The RBI's supervisory arsenal

s.12 empowers the RBI to determine policy and issue binding directions on income recognition, accounting standards, provisioning, capital adequacy and deployment of funds, and on acquisition procedure, aggregate value of assets, fees, and transfer of security receipts. s.12A lets the RBI call for statements and information. s.12B (inserted 2016) gives the RBI audit and inspection powers; where it finds an ARC being run against the public interest or investor interest, it may remove the chairman/director, appoint additional directors, or post an observer (subject to a hearing before removal).

Part VI · Chapter III (ss. 13–19) · The core

Enforcement of security interest: the section 13 machine

This is the chapter that matters at the coalface. Section 13 sets out the entire enforcement sequence; sections 14 to 19 supply the supporting cast — the magistrate's help in taking possession, the rules on management takeover, and the borrower's one real remedy.

s. 13 Enforcement of security interest — clause by clause

13(1) — the override

Notwithstanding ss. 69/69A of the Transfer of Property Act, any security interest may be enforced by the secured creditor without court or tribunal intervention. This is the legal basis for the entire self-help remedy.

13(2) — the demand notice (the 60-day notice)

The condition precedent. Where a borrower defaults and the account is classified as NPA, the secured creditor must serve a written notice requiring the borrower to discharge the full liability within 60 days, failing which the creditor may exercise the s.13(4) measures. Post-2016, the NPA-classification requirement is relaxed for borrowers who raised funds through debt securities, where the debenture trustee enforces.

13(3) & 13(3A) — particulars & the borrower's representation

The notice must give details of the amount due and the secured assets intended to be enforced (13(3)). If the borrower makes a representation or objection, the creditor must consider it and, if rejecting, communicate reasons within 15 days (13(3A), inserted by Act 30 of 2004 in the wake of Mardia). The reasons so communicated do not, however, give the borrower a right to rush to the DRT — that comes only after a s.13(4) measure is taken.

⚠ Pitfall — the "15 days" vs "1 week" trap

The original 13(3A) said reasons must be communicated "within one week." Act 1 of 2013 changed this to "within fifteen days." Older judgments and stale precedents still quote one week. Always cite the current 15-day period, and note that failure to consider and reply to a genuine representation is a recurring ground on which DRTs set aside enforcement.

13(4) — the measures available on continued default

If the borrower does not pay within the 60-day period, the secured creditor may take recourse to one or more of:

  1. Take possession of the secured assets, including the right to transfer by lease, assignment or sale.
  2. Take over management of the borrower's business (only where a substantial part of the business is held as security), with the right to transfer it.
  3. Appoint a manager to manage secured assets whose possession has been taken.
  4. Require any debtor of the borrower (a person who owes money to the borrower) to pay the creditor directly; such payment validly discharges that debtor (13(5)).

13(5A)–(5C) — the reserve-price acquisition (2013)

Where an immovable property does not sell because no bid meets the reserve price, an authorised officer of the secured creditor may bid for and acquire the property at the reserve price at a subsequent sale; the purchase price is adjusted against the claim.

13(8) — the borrower's redemption window (recast 2016)

If the borrower tenders the full dues, costs, charges and expenses before the date of publication of the notice for public auction (or before inviting quotations/tender), the secured assets shall not be transferred, and any steps already taken toward transfer must stop. This is the borrower's last clear chance to redeem.

⚖ Case law — when does the right of redemption die?

Pre-2016, the right of redemption survived until the sale was complete. The 2016 recast of s.13(8) advanced the cut-off to the publication of the auction notice. In Celir LLP v. Bafna Motors (2023), the Supreme Court read the amended provision strictly: once the auction notice is published and the sale confirmed, the borrower cannot defeat the auction purchaser by belatedly tendering dues. Diarise the publication date — it is now a hard deadline.

13(9) — joint financing & the 60% rule

Where a financial asset is financed by more than one secured creditor, no creditor may act under 13(4) unless creditors representing not less than 60% in value of the amount outstanding agree; that decision then binds all. (The original three-fourths threshold was reduced to 60% by Act 1 of 2013.) Provisos preserve the workmen's-dues priority under s.529A of the old Companies Act in a winding-up, and the whole sub-section is now expressly "subject to" the IBC.

13(10) & 13(11) — the shortfall & the guarantors

If the sale proceeds do not satisfy the debt, the creditor may apply to the DRT for the balance (13(10)). And the creditor may proceed against guarantors or pledged assets without first exhausting the s.13(4) measures (13(11)) — guarantor liability is co-extensive and independent.

13(13) — the freeze on the borrower

After receiving the 13(2) notice, the borrower may not transfer (by sale, lease or otherwise, outside the ordinary course of business) any secured asset named in the notice without the creditor's prior written consent.

s. 14 CMM / DM assistance in taking possession

If the borrower resists, the secured creditor need not break the peace itself. It may request the Chief Metropolitan Magistrate or District Magistrate to take possession of the secured asset and hand it over. The application must be supported by a detailed affidavit (the nine declarations introduced in 2013). Post-2016, the magistrate must pass the order within 30 days, extendable for recorded reasons up to an aggregate of 60 days. The CMM/DM may delegate to a subordinate officer (s.14(1A)).

⚖ Case law — the s.14 function is ministerial

The magistrate under s.14 performs an executive, ministerial act, not an adjudicatory one. He verifies the affidavit's contents and assists possession; he does not decide title or the borrower's grievances (Standard Chartered Bank v. V. Noble Kumar, 2013, and the line of authority following it). A borrower aggrieved by a s.14 order is relegated to the DRT under s.17, not to a writ. In Hindon Forge v. State of U.P. (2018), the Court clarified that a s.17 application is maintainable even at the s.14 stage.

s. 15 Manner & effect of takeover of management

On taking over management, the secured creditor publishes a notice in an English and a vernacular newspaper and appoints persons as directors (if the borrower is a company) or as an administrator (in any other case). On publication, sitting directors vacate office, existing management contracts terminate, and the new directors/administrator alone exercise control. While the takeover subsists, shareholders may not nominate directors, no shareholder resolution takes effect without the creditor's approval, and no winding-up may be initiated without the creditor's consent.

s. 16 No compensation for loss of office

A director or manager displaced by a management takeover gets no compensation for loss of office or premature termination, though he may recover money otherwise due to him. The creditor must restore management once the debt is realised in full (s.15(4)) — but post-2016, if the creditor has converted debt into shares and thereby acquired controlling interest, it is not obliged to restore management.

s. 17 Application against measures — the borrower's remedy

This is the borrower's principal weapon. Any person, including the borrower, aggrieved by a 13(4) measure may apply to the DRT within 45 days of the measure. Post-2016 the marginal heading was changed from "Right to appeal" to "Application against measures to recover secured debts" — confirming it is an original application, not an appeal. The DRT examines whether the measures conform to the Act and rules; if not, it may declare them invalid and restore possession or management to the borrower, and pass consequential directions (recast s.17(3), 2016). New jurisdictional rules (s.17(1A)) and a tenancy-rights jurisdiction (s.17(4A)) were also added in 2016.

▣ Practitioner's note — the s.17 jurisdiction & tenancy

Post-2016, a s.17 application is filed where the cause of action arises, where the secured asset is located, or where the lending branch maintains the account (s.17(1A)). Section 17(4A) lets the DRT decide whether a claimed tenancy or lease on the secured asset has expired, is contrary to s.65A TPA, contradicts the mortgage, or was created after the s.13(2) notice — codifying Harshad Govardhan Sondagar v. International Assets Reconstruction Co. (2014), which protected genuine lessees with valid, pre-mortgage leases.

s. 18 Appeal to the DRAT — the deposit gateway

An appeal against the DRT's s.17 order lies to the DRAT within 30 days. The gateway: no appeal is entertained unless the borrower deposits 50% of the debt due (as claimed by the creditor or determined by the DRT, whichever is less). The DRAT may, for recorded reasons, reduce this to not less than 25%, but cannot waive it entirely.

⚖ Case law — Mardia and the death of the 75% pre-deposit

In Mardia Chemicals Ltd v. Union of India (2004), the Supreme Court upheld the constitutional validity of SARFAESI as a whole but struck down the then-condition that a borrower deposit 75% of the claim before approaching the DRT, holding it "onerous and oppressive" and a denial of an effective remedy. Parliament responded with Act 30 of 2004: it removed the pre-deposit at the s.17 stage and relocated a milder, reducible deposit to the appeal stage under s.18. This Mardia–2004 dialogue is the single most exam-worthy episode in the Act's history.

s. 18C Right to lodge a caveat

Inserted by Act 1 of 2013. A secured creditor (or any person claiming a right to be heard) may lodge a caveat before the DRT/DRAT in anticipation of a s.17/s.18 proceeding. The caveator must serve notice on the expected applicant; once an application is filed, the tribunal must notify the caveator and the applicant must furnish copies. A caveat lapses after 90 days unless the proceeding is filed within that period.

s. 19 Compensation & costs to the borrower

If the DRT/DRAT finds that possession was taken not in accordance with the Act, it directs the creditor to return the asset and the borrower (or other aggrieved person) is entitled to compensation and costs as determined by the tribunal. This is the borrower's restitutionary safety net against wrongful enforcement.

✦

Note on ss. 17A & 18B: these provided for applications to the Court of District Judge and appeals to the High Court in areas without DRTs (chiefly the erstwhile J&K). They have been omitted following the J&K and Ladakh reorganisation, with proceedings transferred to the DRT/DRAT framework.

Part VII · Chapters IV & IVA (ss. 20–26E)

The Central Registry & the priority revolution

If section 13 is the Act's engine, Chapter IVA is its quiet game-changer. The Central Registry (CERSAI) was meant to defeat fraudulent multiple financing on the same property; the 2016 amendment then attached to registration a sweeping statutory priority that ranks secured creditors above the tax collector.

ss. 20–22 The Central Registry (CERSAI)

Section 20 lets the Central Government set up the Central Registry of Securitisation Asset Reconstruction and Security Interest of India (CERSAI) to register securitisation, reconstruction and security-interest transactions. The registry is additional to, not in derogation of, registration under the Companies Act, Registration Act, Motor Vehicles Act and others (s.20(4)). A Central Register is maintained, increasingly in electronic form.

ss. 20A, 20B Integration & delegation (2016)

The Government may integrate records of other registration systems (Companies Act, Registration Act, Merchant Shipping Act, Motor Vehicles Act, Patents Act, Designs Act) with CERSAI to build a single pan-India database of security interests. It may delegate its registry powers to the RBI.

ss. 23–26A Filing, modification, satisfaction & inspection

Particulars of every securitisation, reconstruction or creation of security interest are filed with the Central Registrar (s.23). Modifications (s.24) and satisfactions (s.25) are reported; any person may inspect the register on payment of a fee (s.26). Section 26A lets the Central Government condone delay or rectify accidental omissions.

Chapter IVA · ss. 26B–26E Registration by creditors & priority

The heart of the 2016 reform, effective 24 January 2020:

  • s.26B extends the registry to all creditors, not just secured creditors; even tax authorities must file attachment orders. But a non-secured creditor who files gains no enforcement right under the Act.
  • s.26C makes registration a public notice from the date and time of filing, and gives a registered claim priority over any subsequent security interest or transfer (except transactions in the ordinary course of business).
  • s.26D is a hard gate: no secured creditor may enforce under Chapter III unless its security interest is registered with the Central Registry. Registration is now a precondition to enforcement.
  • s.26E is the priority crown jewel: after registration, debts due to a secured creditor are paid in priority over all other debts and all revenues, taxes, cesses and rates payable to the Central or State Government or local authority — subject to the IBC where insolvency proceedings are pending.
⚖ Case law — s.26E vs the State's tax dues

Section 26E reverses the old common-law primacy of Crown/State dues. A run of High Court decisions (and the Supreme Court's broad approach in Punjab National Bank v. Union of India, 2022, on a parallel customs question) confirms that a registered secured creditor's claim outranks State VAT/sales-tax and similar statutory first charges, unless the special statute itself contains an express, later non-obstante priority. The practical lesson is blunt: register early, because s.26E priority runs from registration.

Part VIII · Chapter V (ss. 27–30D)

Offences & the RBI penalty code

The enforcement regime needs a deterrent layer. Originally that meant small daily fines for registry defaults and a general contravention offence. The 2016 amendment bolted on a modern administrative-penalty code wielded directly by the RBI.

Part IX · Chapter VI (ss. 31–42)

Miscellaneous: the clauses that decide the close cases

The miscellaneous chapter is deceptively important. The jurisdiction bar, the overriding clause and the limitation provision are litigated in almost every contested enforcement.

SectionProvisionWhy it matters
31Provisions not to apply in certain casesThe subject-matter exclusions discussed in Part III (agricultural land, pledge, lien, sub-₹1-lakh debt, the 20% rule).
31APower to exempt classes of banks/FIsGovernment may switch off provisions for a class, subject to parliamentary laying.
32Protection of action in good faithNo suit/prosecution against the RBI, CERSAI, a secured creditor or its officers for anything done in good faith.

⚖ Case law — the writ vs s.17 question

Borrowers persistently try to bypass s.17 by filing writ petitions. In United Bank of India v. Satyawati Tondon (2010) and again in Authorized Officer, SBI v. Allwyn Alloys and Phoenix ARC v. Vishwa Bharati Vidya Mandir (2022), the Supreme Court held that the High Court should not entertain a writ where the efficacious statutory remedy under s.17 exists, save in cases of clear lack of jurisdiction or violation of natural justice. The civil court bar in s.34 and the alternate-remedy doctrine together keep SARFAESI disputes inside the tribunal channel.

⚖ Case law — limitation runs, and acknowledgements reset it

In Kotak Mahindra Bank v. A. Balakrishnan (2022), the Supreme Court confirmed that a recovery certificate issued by a DRT gives a fresh cause of action with its own limitation, and that SARFAESI/RDB claims must respect the Limitation Act under s.36. Watch for acknowledgements of debt and part-payments that extend limitation under ss. 18–19 of the Limitation Act.

Part XI

Landmark jurisprudence every practitioner must hold

SARFAESI is a statute shaped as much by the Supreme Court as by Parliament. These are the decisions that recur in pleadings, viva questions and seminar papers. Treat the holdings below as a map; read the judgments in full before you cite them.

Mardia Chemicals v. UOI
(2004) 4 SCC 311

Upheld the Act's validity but struck down the 75% pre-deposit before approaching the DRT as oppressive. Birthed the s.13(3A) representation right and the recast s.17/18 remedy.

Transcore v. Union of India
(2008) 1 SCC 125

A secured creditor may pursue SARFAESI and a pending RDB Act application simultaneously; no need to withdraw the DRT case before invoking s.13. The two are complementary, not alternative.

Authorized Officer, IOB v. Ashok Saw Mill
(2009) 8 SCC 366

The DRT's s.17 jurisdiction extends to events after possession is taken; it can examine the entire post-13(4) action, not just the moment of possession.

United Bank of India v. Satyawati Tondon
(2010) 8 SCC 110

High Courts should be loath to entertain writs against SARFAESI action where the efficacious s.17 remedy exists. The alternate-remedy rule applies with full rigour.

Standard Chartered Bank v. V. Noble Kumar
(2013) 9 SCC 620

The s.14 magistrate's role is ministerial/executive. A s.17 application is available against possession whether taken by the creditor directly or via the magistrate.

Mathew Varghese v. M. Amritha Kumar
(2014) 5 SCC 610

A 30-day clear notice of sale to the borrower (Rule 8/9 of the Enforcement Rules) is mandatory; a sale without it is invalid. Redemption rights must be respected.

Harshad Govardhan Sondagar
(2014) 6 SCC 1

Genuine lessees holding valid leases predating the mortgage are protected; their possession cannot be summarily disturbed. Codified later in s.17(4A).

Pandurang Ganpati Chaugule v. Vishwasrao Patil Murgud Sah. Bank
(2020) 9 SCC 215

Constitution Bench: cooperative banks (including multi-state) are "banks" for SARFAESI; Parliament was competent to bring them in. Settles a long jurisdictional debate.

Hindon Forge v. State of U.P.
(2019) 2 SCC 198

A s.17 application is maintainable at the s.14 stage; the borrower need not wait until physical possession is actually taken to seek the DRT's intervention.

Bajarang Shyamsunder Agarwal v. Central Bank
(2019) 9 SCC 94

Refined the tenancy position: a lease must be valid and registered (or within s.107 TPA) and bona fide; tenancies created to defeat the mortgage are not protected.

ITC Ltd v. Blue Coast Hotels
(2018) 19 SCC 716

Strict compliance with the Enforcement Rules is required, but a borrower who delays redemption and acts mala fide cannot defeat a validly conducted sale on hyper-technical grounds.

Celir LLP v. Bafna Motors
(2024) 2 SCC 1

After the 2016 recast of s.13(8), the borrower's right of redemption ends at publication of the auction notice; a confirmed sale to a bona fide auction purchaser will not be reopened.

Kotak Mahindra Bank v. A. Balakrishnan
(2022) 9 SCC 186

On limitation and the IBC interface: a DRT recovery certificate furnishes a fresh cause of action and is a basis for initiating CIRP; SARFAESI claims must observe s.36 limitation.

▣ Practitioner's note — citation hygiene

Citations above are given in the SCC neutral style for orientation. Before relying on any of them in a Tier-1 submission, confirm the reportable citation and pinpoint the paragraph; some of these decisions have been explained or distinguished by later benches, particularly on the tenancy and redemption questions, where the law is still maturing.

Part XII

The interface: SARFAESI, the RDB Act & the IBC

A secured creditor in 2025 chooses among three overlapping remedies. They are not mutually exclusive, but they interact in ways that decide outcomes. Understanding the hierarchy is core practitioner knowledge.

FeatureSARFAESI 2002RDB Act 1993IBC 2016
NatureSelf-help enforcement of securityAdjudicatory recovery of debtCollective insolvency resolution
ForumCreditor itself → DRT (s.17) → DRATDRT → DRATNCLT → NCLAT → SC
Court intervention to startNone (only CMM/DM help for possession)Required (application + decree)Required (admission of application)
Who can invokeSecured creditors onlyBanks/FIs (secured or not)Financial & operational creditors, corporate debtor
Effect on the assetRealise the specific collateralMoney decree, then executionMoratorium; resolution or liquidation of the whole entity

The three pressure points where the IBC overrides SARFAESI

  • The moratorium (s.14 IBC). On admission of a corporate debtor into CIRP, an absolute moratorium freezes all SARFAESI enforcement. A s.13(4) action cannot continue, and possession already initiated stalls. The IBC's s.238 non-obstante clause prevails over SARFAESI's s.35.
  • Priority in distribution. The s.26E SARFAESI priority and the s.31B RDB priority are both made "subject to" the IBC. In liquidation, distribution follows the IBC's s.53 waterfall, not the SARFAESI/RDB priority, where insolvency proceedings are pending.
  • The recovery certificate as a financial debt. Per Kotak v. Balakrishnan, a DRT recovery certificate can found a fresh CIRP application, linking the RDB/SARFAESI track to the IBC track.
▣ Practitioner's note — sequencing the remedies

The standard playbook: invoke SARFAESI first for speed (it needs no court to start), keep an RDB application alive for the personal/shortfall claim and against guarantors (s.13(10)/(11)), and hold IBC in reserve as the pressure lever against a corporate debtor. Per Transcore, running SARFAESI and RDB together is permissible. But once CIRP is admitted, down tools on SARFAESI until the moratorium lifts or the debtor exits resolution.

Part XIII

The enforcement workflow: section 13 from notice to sale

Doctrine becomes muscle memory once you can see the sequence. This is the live timeline a secured creditor's authorised officer walks through, with the statutory hook and the standard pitfall at each node.

NPA
Step 0 · Classify the account as NPA condition precedent

Per RBI norms (or the governing authority's). No valid NPA classification means no valid s.13(2) notice. Verify limitation under s.36 here too.

13(2)
Step 1 · Serve the 60-day demand notice s.13(2)+(3)

State the amount due and the secured assets to be enforced. Demand discharge within 60 days. Effect proper service; defective service is the most common ground for setting a notice aside.

13(3A)
Step 2 · Consider any representation; reply in 15 days s.13(3A)

If the borrower objects, consider it and, if rejecting, give reasons within 15 days. This is mandatory engagement, not a formality.

13(4)
Step 3 · On continued default, take a measure s.13(4)

Take possession (symbolic, then physical), take over management, appoint a manager, or call in the borrower's debtors. The borrower's 45-day clock to file under s.17 starts now.

s.14
Step 4 · If resisted, apply to the CMM/DM s.14

File the affidavit with the nine declarations. The magistrate must order within 30 days (up to 60 for recorded reasons) and may delegate. Ministerial act only.

RULES
Step 5 · Valuation & sale notice Enforcement Rules 8–9

Obtain valuation, fix the reserve price, and issue a 30-day clear sale notice to the borrower (Mathew Varghese). Publish the auction notice — this date now closes the s.13(8) redemption window.

SALE
Step 6 · Auction, confirmation & sale certificate s.13(6)

Conduct the auction; if no bid meets the reserve, the creditor may itself acquire at reserve price (s.13(5A)). Issue the sale certificate; the transfer vests clear title in the purchaser.

13(10)
Step 7 · Apply the proceeds; recover the shortfall s.13(7),(10),(11)

Apply proceeds to costs, then dues, then return the residue. For any shortfall, apply to the DRT (s.13(10)) and proceed against guarantors and pledged assets (s.13(11)).

The authorised officer's pre-flight checklist

  1. Is the security interest registered with CERSAI? Without it, enforcement is barred (s.26D).
  2. Is the asset outside the s.31 exclusions (not agricultural, not a pledge, debt above ₹1 lakh, dues above the 20% threshold)?
  3. Is the claim within limitation (s.36)? Note any acknowledgements that reset the clock.
  4. Is the account a valid NPA, and is there documentary proof of the default?
  5. In joint financing, is there 60% consent in value (s.13(9))?
  6. Has every representation been answered within 15 days (s.13(3A))?
  7. Has a 30-day clear sale notice been given and the auction notice published correctly?
  8. Is there any pending CIRP that would trigger the IBC moratorium?

Glossary of working abbreviations

ARC

Asset Reconstruction Company — RBI-registered entity that buys and works out bad loans (s.2(1)(ba)).

CERSAI

Central Registry of Securitisation Asset Reconstruction and Security Interest of India — the Central Registry under Chapter IV.

CMM / DM

Chief Metropolitan Magistrate / District Magistrate — assist in taking possession under s.14.

DRT / DRAT

Debts Recovery Tribunal / Appellate Tribunal — the s.17/s.18 adjudicatory forums (created under the RDB Act 1993).

IBC

Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016 — the collective insolvency regime that, on a moratorium, overrides SARFAESI.

NOF

Net Owned Fund — the minimum capital an ARC must hold (statutory floor ₹2 crore; RBI-set ₹100 crore).

NPA

Non-Performing Asset — sub-standard, doubtful or loss account (s.2(1)(o)); the trigger for s.13(2).

QB

Qualified Buyer — the only class permitted to subscribe to security receipts (s.2(1)(u)).

RDB Act

Recovery of Debts and Bankruptcy Act, 1993 (formerly RDDBFI Act) — the adjudicatory recovery statute and parent of the DRTs.

SR

Security Receipt — the tradeable instrument representing an undivided interest in a securitised pool (s.2(1)(zg)).
