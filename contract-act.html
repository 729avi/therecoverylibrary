Part I

Philosophy & evolution: why a promise binds

Contract law answers one deceptively simple question: when will the State lend its coercive power to enforce a private promise? Master that question and everything in the Act, from the offer in section 2 to the agent's authority in section 238, becomes an answer to it.

The intellectual spine of the Act is freedom of contract, the idea that adults of full capacity should be free to bargain on their own terms and that the law will hold them to those terms. Two Latin maxims carry the load. Pacta sunt servanda means agreements must be kept. Consensus ad idem means the parties must meet on the same thing in the same sense. The Act takes these inherited ideas and gives them a statutory grammar: a proposal, an acceptance, consideration, capacity, free consent and a lawful object, which together convert a bare understanding into an enforceable contract.

For a restructuring lawyer this is not abstract. Every facility agreement, deed of hypothecation, guarantee, inter-creditor agreement, novation, settlement and resolution plan is a contract whose enforceability turns on these same elements. When a borrower argues that a guarantee was signed under undue influence, that a one-time settlement lacked consideration, or that a force majeure clause excused performance, the lawyer is litigating the Indian Contract Act, 1872, whatever the dispute is nominally about.

Commercial certainty as the organising value

The deeper purpose of the Act is commercial certainty. Markets function because parties can predict that promises will be enforced and can price the risk that they will not. The Act protects that predictability in three ways. It fixes the moment a contract is formed, so neither side can resile at will. It limits the excuses for non-performance, so that ordinary commercial hardship does not dissolve a bargain. And it confines the remedy for breach to compensation for loss actually suffered, so that liability is foreseeable rather than punitive. A practitioner who keeps certainty at the centre will read every ambiguous provision the way the courts do, in the direction that upholds bargains and disciplines opportunism.

PRE-1872
A patchwork of personal laws and English principles

Before codification, contractual disputes in British India were resolved by a mixture of Hindu and Muslim personal law, local usage, and the principles of English common law and equity applied through the doctrine of justice, equity and good conscience. The result was uncertainty, especially in the growing world of mercantile and banking transactions.

1872
Codification by the Third Indian Law Commission

The Act received assent on 25 April 1872 and came into force on 1 September 1872. It was designed not to be exhaustive but to state the general principles of contract clearly enough to give the commercial community a stable framework. Section 1 expressly saved trade usages and customs not inconsistent with the Act.

1930
Sale of goods carved out

The original sections 76 to 123 on sale of goods were repealed and re-enacted as the Sale of Goods Act, 1930, recognising that the sale of movables had grown into a specialised body of law deserving its own statute.

1932
Partnership carved out

The original sections 239 to 266 on partnership were repealed and re-enacted as the Indian Partnership Act, 1932. What remained is the durable core that practitioners still work with: general principles (ss. 1 to 75) and the special contracts of indemnity, guarantee, bailment, pledge and agency (ss. 124 to 238).

1996 to date
Targeted modern amendments

Section 28 was amended in 1997 and again in 2013 to police clauses that curtailed limitation or extinguished rights. The Information Technology Act, 2000 supplied the missing rules for electronic offer and acceptance. The Specific Relief (Amendment) Act, 2018 reshaped the remedy of specific performance that sits alongside the Act. The 1872 text has proved remarkably durable, amended at the edges rather than rebuilt.

The interface with English common law

The Act was drafted by English lawyers steeped in the common law, and many of its sections codify English doctrine. But it is a statute, not a restatement, and where its language differs from English law the statute prevails. The leading illustration is frustration. English law developed the theories of implied term and a radical change in obligation; Indian courts have repeatedly held that in India the doctrine lives entirely inside the positive language of section 56 and is not to be controlled by shifting English theory. The practitioner's discipline is therefore precise: read the section first, treat English authority as persuasive illustration of the underlying commercial logic, and never assume that an English rule survives unaltered in India.

Practitioner's note — the Act is not a complete code

Section 1 preserves usages and customs of trade, and the courts continue to fill gaps with principles of equity and common law where the Act is silent. So a contract problem is never solved by the Act alone. Read it together with the Sale of Goods Act, the Transfer of Property Act for security over immovables, the Specific Relief Act for non-monetary remedies, the Limitation Act for timing, the Arbitration and Conciliation Act for the forum, and, once a company is in distress, the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, which overrides inconsistent contractual rights.

Memory aid — the anatomy of an enforceable contract

Run every agreement through six gates, in order: Offer, Acceptance, Consideration, Capacity, Consent (free), Legality of object. If any gate fails the agreement is void, voidable or unenforceable, and the consequence differs at each gate. The chapters that follow are simply these six gates examined under a microscope, followed by what happens when a valid contract is performed, discharged or broken.

Chapter 1 · Sections 2 to 11

Formation: how a binding contract comes into being

Formation is the threshold every dispute returns to. Before a court asks whether a promise was broken, it asks whether there was a contract at all. The answer is built from defined terms in section 2, a communication regime in sections 3 to 9, and capacity in sections 10 and 11.

s. 2 The defined vocabulary

Section 2 is the dictionary that the rest of the Act speaks in. Each definition is a building block, and the architecture only makes sense once the blocks are in place.

Proposal / Offer
s.2(a)

When one person signifies to another a willingness to do or abstain from doing anything, with a view to obtaining that other's assent. The signification, not the inner intention, is what counts.

Promise
s.2(b)

A proposal, once accepted, becomes a promise. The acceptance is what converts a unilateral willingness into a mutual commitment.

Promisor & Promisee
s.2(c)

The person making the proposal is the promisor; the person accepting it is the promisee. In a bilateral contract each party is both, in respect of the promise they make and the promise they receive.

Consideration
s.2(d)

When, at the desire of the promisor, the promisee or any other person has done, abstains from doing, or promises to do or abstain, something. India recognises past, executed and executory consideration, and crucially consideration may move from a third party.

Agreement
s.2(e)

Every promise and every set of promises forming the consideration for each other. Agreement equals offer plus acceptance; it is the genus of which contract is a species.

Reciprocal promises
s.2(f)

Promises that form the consideration, or part of the consideration, for each other. The order and dependence of reciprocal promises is governed later by sections 51 to 54.

Void agreement
s.2(g)

An agreement not enforceable by law. It is a nullity from the start; it creates no rights and no obligations, and nothing can revive it.

Contract
s.2(h)

An agreement enforceable by law. The whole Act is built to identify which agreements clear this bar. Contract = agreement + enforceability.

Voidable contract
s.2(i)

An agreement enforceable at the option of one party but not the other. It is valid and binding until the party with the option elects to avoid it. The classic source is a defect in free consent.

Void contract
s.2(j)

A contract that ceases to be enforceable by law. Unlike a void agreement, it was once valid; supervening impossibility under section 56 is the typical cause.

Frequently misunderstood — void agreement vs void contract vs voidable

These three are not interchangeable, and confusing them costs cases. A void agreement was never a contract (a minor's agreement, an agreement in restraint of trade). A void contract began as a valid contract and later ceased to be enforceable (a contract frustrated under section 56). A voidable contract is fully valid and enforceable unless and until the innocent party avoids it (a guarantee procured by coercion). The practical stakes differ: restitution under section 65 follows a contract that becomes void or is avoided, but is approached differently for an agreement void from the outset.

ss. 3 to 9 The communication regime

Formation is not a state of mind but a sequence of communications. Sections 3 to 9 supply the rules for when those communications take legal effect.

Offer versus invitation to offer

This distinction decides who controls the moment of contract. An offer is capable of acceptance and binds the offeror on acceptance. An invitation to offer merely invites others to make offers, which the inviter remains free to accept or decline. Goods displayed in a shop, advertisements, catalogues, prospectuses, a request for tenders and an auctioneer's call for bids are ordinarily invitations to offer, not offers. The commercial significance is control: a bank that circulates indicative terms is inviting offers, and is not bound until it accepts a borrower's application.

Is this communication an offer or an invitation to offer?
Offer (binds on acceptance)

The maker has shown a willingness to be bound the moment the other side says yes, with terms settled and nothing left to negotiate. A general reward promise, a firm quotation accepted without change, a tender invitation that promises to accept the lowest conforming bid.

Invitation to offer (no binding yet)

The maker is testing the market and reserves the right to accept or refuse. Display of goods, advertisements, an information memorandum, a prospectus, a request for proposals, indicative term sheets marked "subject to contract".

The recurring formation puzzles

Five patterns recur in commercial practice, and each has a settled rule.

  • Cross offers. Two identical offers cross in the post without knowledge of each other. There is no contract, because acceptance requires knowledge of the offer; mere coincidence of terms is not consensus ad idem.
  • Counter-offer. A reply that varies the terms rejects the original offer and substitutes a new one. The original offer is destroyed and cannot later be accepted (Hyde v Wrench). In negotiation this means a redlined acceptance kills the prior offer.
  • Standing offer / tender. A tender to supply goods as and when required is a standing offer; each order placed is a separate acceptance forming a distinct contract. The supplier may revoke the standing offer for future orders, but is bound for orders already placed.
  • General offer. An offer to the world at large, accepted by anyone who performs the stated condition with knowledge of the offer (Carlill v Carbolic Smoke Ball). Reward notices and performance-linked promises fall here.
  • Knowledge requirement. A person who performs the condition of an offer in ignorance of it cannot claim the reward, because there is no acceptance of an offer one does not know exists (Lalman Shukla v Gauri Dutt).

The postal rule and instantaneous communication

Section 4 builds the postal rule into the statute: as against the proposer, acceptance is complete when posted. This was sensible for non-instantaneous post. But where communication is instantaneous, the rule shifts. In Bhagwandas Goverdhandas Kedia v Girdharilal Parshottamdas the Supreme Court held that for a contract made by telephone the contract is complete only when and where the acceptance is actually heard by the proposer, following the English position in Entores v Miles Far East. The reason is that instantaneous media let the acceptor know at once if the message did not get through, so the risk of non-receipt should rest on the acceptor, not the proposer.

Drafting note — electronic offer and acceptance

Email, click-wrap and electronic execution now dominate transaction practice. The Indian Contract Act is supplemented by sections 11 to 13 of the Information Technology Act, 2000, which provide rules for attribution, acknowledgement and the time and place of dispatch and receipt of electronic records. In Trimex International v Vedanta the Supreme Court held that a concluded contract can arise from an exchange of emails even without a formal signed agreement, once the essential terms are agreed and unconditionally accepted. Practitioners should therefore mark pre-contractual emails "subject to contract" where they do not intend to be bound, and should specify in the contract the agreed time and place of receipt of electronic notices.

ss. 10 & 11 What agreements are contracts; capacity

Section 10 gathers the elements: an agreement is a contract if made by free consent of parties competent to contract, for a lawful consideration and with a lawful object, and not expressly declared void. Section 11 then defines competence. A person is competent if of the age of majority, of sound mind, and not disqualified from contracting by any law to which they are subject.

Landmark — a minor's agreement is void ab initio

In Mohori Bibee v Dharmodas Ghose (1903) the Privy Council held that an agreement by a minor is not merely voidable but absolutely void. A minor who mortgaged property and took a loan could not be compelled to repay, and the moneylender could not recover on the contract or in restitution under the then law. The decision settled that the contractual incapacity of a minor under section 11 is a complete bar. The minor's necessaries are recoverable not on contract but under section 68, which makes the minor's property (not the minor personally) liable for necessaries supplied. The Specific Relief and equity principles permitting restitution where the minor misrepresented age developed later, but the core rule stands: you cannot lend to a minor and expect contractual recovery.

Capacity in the corporate and restructuring context

For a restructuring practitioner, capacity rarely turns on minority and almost always turns on corporate and authority questions. A company's capacity to contract is governed by its constitution and the Companies Act; the historic doctrine of ultra vires has been softened by the modern objects clause and by section 4 of the Companies Act, 2013. Two practical capacity checks recur. First, is the entity authorised to enter the transaction, with a valid board or shareholder resolution where required. Second, is the signatory authorised to bind the entity, which is an agency question governed by Chapter 13 and the doctrine of indoor management. A guarantee or security document signed without authority can be repudiated, which is why lenders insist on board resolutions, certified constitutional documents and officer certificates as conditions precedent.

Memory aid — the formation checklist for a credit document

Before treating a facility, security or guarantee document as binding, confirm: a clear offer (sanction terms), an absolute and unqualified acceptance (no surviving "subject to" conditions), consideration (the advance, or forbearance), capacity (entity power plus signatory authority), free consent (no coercion or undue influence over a guarantor), and a lawful object. A defect at any gate is the first thing a borrower's counsel will probe when enforcement begins.

Chapter 2 · Sections 13 to 22

Free consent: the vitiating factors and their consequences

A contract requires not just consent but free consent. The five vitiating factors, coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation and mistake, are the most heavily litigated part of the Act in restructuring, because a guarantor or borrower who cannot dispute the debt will dispute the consent.

ss. 13 & 14 Consent and free consent

Section 13 defines consent: two or more persons consent when they agree upon the same thing in the same sense, the principle of consensus ad idem. Section 14 then defines free consent negatively: consent is free when it is not caused by coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation or mistake. The consequence differs by cause. Where consent is caused by coercion, undue influence, fraud or misrepresentation, the contract is voidable at the option of the party whose consent was so caused (sections 19 and 19A). Where consent is caused by certain mistakes, the agreement may be void (section 20).

Voidable — ss. 19, 19A

Caused by coercion, undue influence, fraud or misrepresentation. The contract is valid until the wronged party elects to avoid it. The wronged party may instead affirm and insist on performance, and may claim restoration of any benefit.

The right to avoid is lost by affirmation, by lapse of time, or where third-party rights have intervened in good faith and for value.

Void — ss. 20, 21

Caused by a mutual mistake of fact essential to the agreement (s.20), the agreement is void from the start. A mistake as to a matter of fact not essential, or a mistake of Indian law (s.21), does not make the contract voidable.

A unilateral mistake (s.22) generally does not avoid a contract, subject to narrow exceptions on identity and the nature of the document signed.

s. 15 Coercion

Coercion is the committing or threatening to commit any act forbidden by the Indian Penal Code, or the unlawful detaining or threatening to detain any property, to the prejudice of any person, with the intention of causing that person to enter into the agreement. The pressure need not be directed at the contracting party and need not relate to the contract's subject matter.

  • In Ranganayakamma v Alwar Setti a widow was coerced into adopting a boy by a threat to obstruct the removal of her husband's corpse for cremation. Her consent was held not free.
  • In Chikham Ammiraju v Chikham Seshamma a threat to commit suicide was held to amount to coercion within section 15, even though suicide itself was beyond the reach of prosecution, because the threat was of an act forbidden by the Penal Code.
Practitioner's note — coercion in lending rarely succeeds

Borrowers and guarantors frequently allege that a settlement or a fresh security was extracted under coercion because the bank threatened recovery action, refused further credit, or invoked a guarantee. A lawful threat is not coercion. A threat to do what one is legally entitled to do, such as classifying an account as an NPA, initiating SARFAESI, filing a recovery application or refusing further advances, is not an act forbidden by the Penal Code and does not vitiate consent. The plea generally fails unless the pressure was unlawful in itself.

s. 16 Undue influence

Undue influence is the more dangerous plea for a creditor, because it does not require any unlawful act. A contract is induced by undue influence where the relations between the parties are such that one is in a position to dominate the will of the other, and that person uses the position to obtain an unfair advantage. Section 16(2) presumes a dominating position where one party holds real or apparent authority over the other, or stands in a fiduciary relation, or contracts with a person whose capacity is temporarily or permanently affected by age, illness, or mental or bodily distress.

The critical mechanism is the burden of proof. Where one party is in a position to dominate and the transaction appears, on its face or on the evidence, to be unconscionable, section 16(3) shifts the burden onto the dominant party to prove that the contract was not induced by undue influence.

Landmark — presumed influence and unconscionable bargains

In Allcard v Skinner the English court set the template for presumed undue influence in relationships of spiritual and personal ascendancy. Indian courts apply the same logic through section 16. In Subhas Chandra Das Mushib v Ganga Prosad Das Mushib the Supreme Court clarified that mere existence of a relationship of confidence is not enough; the party challenging must show both domination and that the transaction was unfair, after which the burden shifts. In Central Inland Water Transport Corporation v Brojo Nath Ganguly the Court struck down an oppressive service clause as unconscionable and contrary to public policy under section 23, holding that an unequal bargaining position in a standard-form contract can render a clause void. These principles feed directly into challenges to harsh terms in standard-form facility and guarantee documents.

Pitfall — the guarantor's undue influence plea

A personal guarantor, often a promoter's spouse, parent or relative, may argue that the guarantee was procured by undue influence by the promoter or the bank. The plea has teeth where the surety received no benefit, did not understand the document, and was in a position of dependence. Lenders defend by showing independent advice, clear explanation, and benefit to the surety. Document the surety's understanding and, for vulnerable sureties, insist on independent legal advice as a condition precedent, because this is the single most effective defence to a later undue influence challenge.

s. 17 Fraud

Fraud means any of the following acts committed by a party, or with their connivance, or by their agent, with intent to deceive or to induce the other party to enter the contract: a suggestion of a fact that is not true, by one who does not believe it to be true; the active concealment of a fact by one having knowledge or belief of the fact; a promise made without any intention of performing it; any other act fitted to deceive; and any act or omission specially declared fraudulent by law. The defining feature is knowledge of falsity or absence of belief in truth, coupled with intent to deceive.

The classic statement of the mental element is Derry v Peek: fraud is established when a false representation is made knowingly, or without belief in its truth, or recklessly careless whether it be true or false. Mere negligence is not fraud. The explanation to section 17 adds that mere silence is not fraud unless there is a duty to speak or unless silence is, in itself, equivalent to speech, for example where partial disclosure makes what is said misleading.

s. 18 Misrepresentation

Misrepresentation is the innocent cousin of fraud. It covers a positive assertion of something not true that the maker believes to be true, a breach of duty that gains an advantage by misleading another, and causing a party to make a mistake about the substance of the subject matter. The vital distinction from fraud is the absence of intent to deceive and of knowledge of falsity. A representation of mere opinion, honestly held, is not misrepresentation; in Bisset v Wilkinson a seller's honest estimate of the sheep-carrying capacity of land was opinion, not a representation of fact.

ss. 20 to 22 Mistake

Mistake is the only vitiating factor that can make an agreement void rather than merely voidable, and only in narrow circumstances.

  • Section 20, mutual mistake of fact. Where both parties are under a mistake as to a matter of fact essential to the agreement, the agreement is void. The mistake must be shared and must go to the root of the contract, for example the subject matter has, unknown to both, ceased to exist.
  • Section 21, mistake of law. A contract is not voidable because it was caused by a mistake as to any law in force in India; ignorance of Indian law is no excuse. A mistake as to a foreign law is treated as a mistake of fact.
  • Section 22, unilateral mistake. A contract is not voidable merely because one party was under a mistake as to a matter of fact. The narrow exceptions developed by the courts concern mistake as to the identity of the contracting party, and mistake as to the very nature of the document signed (the plea historically called non est factum).
Drafting note — entire-agreement and representation clauses

Transaction documents routinely contain an entire-agreement clause and a non-reliance clause stating that the buyer relied only on the express warranties and not on any extraneous representation. These clauses narrow the scope for a later misrepresentation claim by defining what was, and was not, relied upon. They cannot, however, exclude liability for fraud, because Indian courts will not allow a party to contract out of its own dishonesty. In a share purchase or business transfer, the negotiated boundary between warranty (sounding in contractual damages), representation (sounding in avoidance and, if fraudulent, deceit) and indemnity (a contractual debt) is where most of the risk allocation lives.

Memory aid — consequence map of the vitiating factors

Coercion, undue influence, fraud and misrepresentation all make the contract voidable. Only fraud carries a freestanding right to damages. Mutual mistake of essential fact makes the agreement void. Mistake of Indian law changes nothing. Unilateral mistake changes nothing, save the narrow identity and document exceptions. When a guarantor pleads "my consent was not free", the first question is always which factor, because the remedy and the burden of proof flow from the answer.

Chapter 3 · Sections 23 to 30

Legality & void agreements: the limits of freedom of contract

Freedom of contract is not absolute. Sections 23 to 30 mark the boundaries the State will not let private bargains cross: unlawful objects, agreements without consideration, and a list of agreements expressly declared void, including the two that dominate commercial drafting, restraint of trade and ouster of jurisdiction.

s. 23 What consideration and objects are lawful

Section 23 is the gateway. The consideration or object of an agreement is lawful unless it is forbidden by law; or is of such a nature that, if permitted, it would defeat the provisions of any law; or is fraudulent; or involves or implies injury to the person or property of another; or the court regards it as immoral or opposed to public policy. In each of these cases the agreement is void. The width of the section lies in the open category of public policy, which courts apply cautiously, conscious that it is, in the old phrase, an unruly horse.

Section 23, the five heads of illegality The consideration or object is unlawful if it is forbidden by law, would defeat the provisions of any law, is fraudulent, involves or implies injury to the person or property of another, or the court regards it as immoral, or opposed to public policy. In each case the agreement is void.

s. 24 Agreements void where consideration is unlawful in part

If any part of a single consideration for one or more objects, or any one of several considerations for a single object, is unlawful, the agreement is void. The section forces a practitioner to test severability: where the lawful and unlawful parts are genuinely independent, the lawful part may survive, but where the unlawful element infects the whole consideration, the entire agreement falls.

s. 25 Agreement without consideration is void, with exceptions

The general rule is that an agreement made without consideration is void. But section 25 carves out three commercially important exceptions, and adds a fourth in its explanation.

  • Natural love and affection. A written and registered agreement, made on account of natural love and affection between parties standing in a near relation to each other, is enforceable without consideration.
  • Past voluntary service. A promise to compensate, wholly or in part, a person who has already voluntarily done something for the promisor, or done something the promisor was legally compellable to do, is enforceable.
  • Time-barred debt. A written promise, signed by the debtor or an authorised agent, to pay a debt of which recovery is barred by the law of limitation, is enforceable. This is heavily used in restructuring, because it revives an otherwise unenforceable debt.
  • Explanation, completed gift. The rule does not affect the validity of any gift actually made.
Practitioner's note — reviving a time-barred debt and acknowledgements

A written promise to pay a time-barred debt under section 25(3) is a powerful tool, but it must be a clear promise, signed by the debtor, not a mere acknowledgement. Distinguish two different mechanisms. A fresh promise under section 25(3) creates a new enforceable obligation even for a debt already time-barred. An acknowledgement of liability before limitation expires, under section 18 of the Limitation Act, merely extends the existing limitation period. In a one-time settlement or restructuring, capture the borrower's commitment as an express written promise to pay, signed by an authorised signatory, so that it is enforceable whether or not the original debt was within time.

s. 26 Agreement in restraint of marriage

Every agreement in restraint of the marriage of any person, other than a minor, is void. It has little direct commercial application but completes the structure of expressly void agreements.

s. 27 Agreement in restraint of trade

Every agreement by which anyone is restrained from exercising a lawful profession, trade or business of any kind is, to that extent, void. The single statutory exception is the sale of goodwill: a seller of the goodwill of a business may agree with the buyer to refrain from carrying on a similar business within specified local limits, so long as the limits are reasonable. India is stricter than England here: section 27 voids restraints regardless of reasonableness, save the goodwill exception, whereas English law tests reasonableness across the board.

Landmark — restraints during versus after employment

In Niranjan Shankar Golikari v Century Spinning and Manufacturing Co. the Supreme Court upheld a negative covenant operating during the term of employment, because a restraint that binds an employee only while employed is not a restraint of trade within section 27; it is a term of the engagement. By contrast, a covenant restraining an employee after the employment ends is generally void under section 27, however reasonable. In Percept D'Mark v Zaheer Khan and Superintendence Company of India v Krishan Murgai the Court reaffirmed that post-termination non-compete clauses are unenforceable in India. The drafting lesson is to protect legitimate interests through confidentiality and during-term exclusivity, not through post-exit non-competes, which the courts will not enforce.

s. 28 Agreements in restraint of legal proceedings

Section 28 voids, to that extent, any agreement that absolutely restrains a party from enforcing its rights through the ordinary tribunals, or that limits the time within which rights may be enforced. The section was amended in 1997 to make clear that clauses extinguishing rights or discharging liability on the expiry of a specified period, shorter than limitation, are void, and again in 2013 to extend the protection to guarantees given to banks and financial institutions. There are two carve-outs preserved by the section: a clause referring disputes to arbitration is valid, and a clause fixing the place of suit where more than one court has jurisdiction (an exclusive jurisdiction clause) is valid, because it regulates rather than ousts the remedy.

Void under s.28

A clause that says no suit may be brought at all. A clause that says the right itself is extinguished, or the surety's liability discharged, if no claim is made within, say, six months, where that is shorter than limitation.

A clause purporting to bar a guarantor's claim against a bank after a curtailed period (now expressly void post-2013).

Valid despite s.28

An arbitration clause, because it channels the remedy to a chosen tribunal rather than ousting it. An exclusive jurisdiction clause naming one of several competent courts.

A clause that requires a claim to be made within a period (notice of claim), as distinct from one that extinguishes the right.

s. 29 Agreements void for uncertainty

Agreements whose meaning is not certain, or capable of being made certain, are void. Commercial courts strive to uphold bargains and will resolve apparent uncertainty by reference to trade usage, the conduct of the parties, and machinery within the contract for fixing the uncertain term. But an agreement to agree on an essential term in the future, with no mechanism to settle it, is void for uncertainty. The drafting discipline is to ensure that price, quantity, duration and the identity of the parties are either fixed or determinable by an objective standard.

s. 30 Wagering agreements

Agreements by way of wager are void, and no suit lies to recover anything won on a wager. A wager is a promise to pay money or money's worth on the determination of an uncertain event, where neither party has any interest in the event other than the stake, and each stands to win or lose by the result. Two features distinguish a wager from a real commercial contract: mutual chances of gain or loss, and the absence of any interest beyond the stake.

Landmark — void but not illegal, and the collateral question

In Gherulal Parakh v Mahadeodas Maiya the Supreme Court held that although a wagering agreement is void under section 30, it is not illegal under section 23, and so collateral transactions connected to a wager are not necessarily tainted. The distinction matters because a void agreement, unlike an illegal one, does not infect collateral contracts. A partner who advances money for the purpose of a wagering business could therefore recover from co-partners, the wager itself being merely unenforceable rather than unlawful. The practical takeaway is to separate the two ideas: voidness affects only the agreement itself, while illegality under section 23 can poison everything built on it.

The modern judicial approach to public policy

Public policy under section 23 has acquired its sharpest modern significance in the law of arbitration, where the ground of "patent illegality" and conflict with public policy is used to test awards. In ONGC v Saw Pipes the Court read public policy widely to include patent illegality, which produced a wave of challenges; later decisions in Renusagar, Shri Lal Mahal, and Ssangyong Engineering v NHAI, together with the 2015 amendments to the Arbitration Act, narrowed the ground again, confining patent illegality largely to domestic awards and excluding a mere error of law or re-appreciation of evidence. The lesson for a commercial lawyer is that public policy is deployed not to rewrite bargains but to refuse enforcement to the small class of agreements and awards that offend the fundamental policy of Indian law, the interests of justice, or basic notions of morality.

Memory aid — the expressly void agreements

Six agreements are declared void by name in this chapter: without consideration (s.25), in restraint of marriage (s.26), in restraint of trade (s.27), in restraint of legal proceedings (s.28), uncertain (s.29), and by way of wager (s.30). Plus the open-textured unlawful objects of s.23. A quick mnemonic for the named six is "Many Traders Lose Under Wild Conditions", mapping to Marriage, Trade, Legal proceedings, Uncertainty, Wager, Consideration.

Chapter 4 · Sections 31 to 36

Contingent contracts: promises that depend on an event

A contingent contract is one to do or not to do something if some collateral event happens or does not happen. The structure underlies project finance conditions precedent, insurance, performance bonds and escrow releases, and the chapter's value to a practitioner is its clean separation of the contingent contract from the void wager.

s. 31 The definition

A contingent contract is a contract to do or not to do something if some event, collateral to the contract, does or does not happen. Two features define it. The performance obligation is conditional on an uncertain future event, and that event is collateral to the contract, meaning it is not itself part of the consideration but a condition on which the promise turns. A contract of insurance is the paradigm: the insurer promises to pay if the insured event occurs.

Contingent contract distinguished from wager

The two look similar because both turn on an uncertain event, but the legal consequence is opposite: a contingent contract is valid and enforceable, a wager is void.

Contingent contract (valid)

The event is collateral to a genuine underlying transaction. At least one party has a real interest in the subject matter beyond the stake. The promise is to do or pay something in performance of a bargain.

Example: insurance on one's own factory, a performance bond on a construction contract, payment on a ship's safe arrival of goods one owns.

Wager (void)

The uncertain event is the whole substance of the agreement. Neither party has any interest other than the stake. Each side wins or loses purely on the outcome.

Example: a bet on whether a ship will arrive, by persons with no interest in the ship or its cargo.

Contingent contract distinguished from insurance

Insurance is a species of contingent contract, but it is enforceable precisely because the insured has an insurable interest, a real economic stake in the subject matter, which removes it from the wager category. A life or fire policy taken without insurable interest collapses into a wager and is void. The presence of insurable interest is therefore the line that separates a valid contingent contract of indemnity from a void bet.

Conditions precedent and conditions subsequent in commercial documents

The contingent-contract machinery is the legal foundation of the conditions that govern modern financing and construction.

  • Conditions precedent. In a facility agreement, the lender's obligation to advance is contingent on the satisfaction of conditions precedent, such as perfected security, corporate authorisations, legal opinions and the absence of default. Until they are satisfied, no obligation to lend arises, mirroring section 32.
  • Conditions subsequent. A contract may be fully operative but liable to be discharged on the occurrence of a later event, for example a clause that lets a lender accelerate or cancel on a rating downgrade or a covenant breach. The obligation exists but is defeasible.
  • Escrow releases. Funds held in escrow are released on the happening of a defined event, a classic contingent obligation governed by sections 32 and 35 where a time limit is set.
Drafting note — conditions precedent in project finance

In an infrastructure or project-finance transaction the conditions precedent are the practitioner's principal risk-control tool. Each draw is contingent on a checklist: land acquisition and right of way, environmental and regulatory clearances, the engineering, procurement and construction contract in place, the offtake or concession agreement executed, sponsor equity infused, and security created and registered. Draft each condition as an objective, verifiable fact, not a matter of opinion, so that the contingency can be tested cleanly. Specify a long-stop date under section 35 logic, after which the lender may walk away if conditions remain unmet, and state clearly who certifies satisfaction and whether conditions can be waived and by whom.

IBC angle — contingent claims in insolvency

The contingent nature of a claim matters in insolvency. A guarantee, an indemnity and an undrawn commitment are contingent obligations. Under the IBC, a creditor with a contingent or unmatured claim can still file a claim with the resolution professional, who values it for the purpose of voting and distribution, often at a notional or discounted figure pending crystallisation. Performance bonds and bank guarantees, being autonomous contracts, are generally treated separately from the moratorium. Understanding which obligations are contingent, and when the contingency falls in, is essential to advising a creditor on how its exposure will be recognised in a corporate insolvency resolution process.

Chapter 5 · Sections 37 to 67

Performance: obligation, tender, novation and the order of play

Once a valid contract exists, this chapter governs how it is carried out, who must perform, in what order reciprocal promises fall due, how payments are appropriated, and how a contract is varied, novated or discharged by agreement. For a restructuring lawyer the indispensable sections are appropriation (ss. 59 to 61), novation and rescission (s. 62), remission and waiver (s. 63), and time as essence (s. 55).

ss. 37 to 41 The obligation to perform, and by whom

Section 37 states the basic duty: the parties to a contract must either perform, or offer to perform, their respective promises, unless performance is dispensed with or excused under the Act or any other law. The obligation survives the death of a party and binds the representatives, unless a contrary intention appears or the contract is personal in nature.

  • Section 38, tender. Where a promisor offers to perform and the offer is not accepted, the promisor is not responsible for non-performance and does not lose rights under the contract. A valid tender must be unconditional, made at a proper time and place, and give the other party a reasonable opportunity to ascertain that the thing offered is what the promisor is bound to deliver. A valid tender of money keeps the debtor's rights alive though it does not discharge the debt.
  • Section 40, who may perform. If it appears that the parties intended performance by the promisor personally, the promise must be performed by the promisor. Otherwise the promisor or a competent person may perform. Personal-skill contracts cannot be delegated; ordinary commercial obligations can.
  • Section 41, accepting performance from a third person. When a promisee accepts performance from a third person, the promisee cannot afterwards enforce it against the promisor. This is significant where a guarantor or a group company performs the debtor's obligation.

ss. 42 to 45 Joint promisors and joint promisees

Where two or more persons make a joint promise, the rules on liability and contribution are commercially vital, because lenders constantly deal with co-borrowers and co-guarantors.

Where performance is to be at a particular place, the promisor must apply to the promisee to appoint a reasonable place and perform there (s.49). ss. 51 to 54 Reciprocal promises and the order of performance

Reciprocal promises are the engine of a bilateral contract, and the order in which they must be performed often decides who is in breach. Section 52 says the order is that which the contract fixes; where the contract is silent, they are performed in the order the nature of the transaction requires. Section 51 deals with simultaneous reciprocal promises: a promisor need not perform unless the promisee is ready and willing to perform the reciprocal promise. Section 54 is the practical workhorse: where the performance of one promise cannot be claimed until another has first been performed, and the party who should perform first fails to do so, that party cannot claim performance of the reciprocal promise and must compensate the other for any loss.

Practitioner's note — readiness and willingness

In any suit on a contract with reciprocal promises, and especially in specific performance, the plaintiff must plead and prove continuous readiness and willingness to perform its own part. A buyer suing a defaulting seller, or a lender enforcing a borrower's obligation, must show it stood ready to do what the contract required of it. This is a recurring evidentiary battleground; the party that first defaulted on a condition it was bound to perform first cannot insist on the other side's performance, by force of section 54.

ss. 59 to 61 Appropriation of payments

When a debtor owes several distinct debts to a creditor and makes a payment insufficient to clear all, the law of appropriation decides which debt it pays. This is constantly litigated in banking, where a borrower with multiple facilities makes part payments.

  • Section 59, debtor's right. The debtor may, at the time of payment, expressly or by implication direct that the payment be applied to a particular debt. The creditor must apply it accordingly.
  • Section 60, creditor's right. If the debtor gives no direction, the creditor may apply the payment to any lawful debt actually due, including a debt the recovery of which is barred by limitation.
  • Section 61, default rule. If neither party appropriates, the payment is applied to debts in order of time, the earliest first. If debts are of equal date, the payment is applied proportionately.
Landmark — the rule in Clayton's Case

For a running account, such as an overdraft or cash-credit account, the rule in Clayton's Case (Devaynes v Noble) supplies the default: in an account current, the first item on the debit side is discharged by the first item on the credit side, payments being appropriated to the earliest debits in order of date. The first sum paid in is the first drawn out. This "first in, first out" presumption matters where a guarantor's liability is fixed at a point in time, because subsequent credits to the running account are treated as paying off the earlier (guaranteed) debits, potentially exhausting the guarantor's exposure unless the security or guarantee is ring-fenced. Banks frequently contract out of Clayton's Case to preserve the appropriation they want.

s. 62 Novation, rescission and alteration

Section 62 is the heart of restructuring practice. If the parties to a contract agree to substitute a new contract for it, or to rescind or alter it, the original contract need not be performed. The three mechanisms are distinct.

In Union of India v Kishorilal Gupta & Bros. the Supreme Court explained that novation under section 62 extinguishes the original contract and substitutes a new one, and that the new agreement must itself be valid and enforceable; if it fails, the parties are not necessarily remitted to the old contract. Novation must be by consent of all parties. A unilateral alteration of a written contract by one party without the other's consent does not novate; it may, under section 87 of the Negotiable Instruments Act and the general principle in Lala Pyare Lal v Hari Kishen, even discharge the other party where the alteration is material. The drafting point is that any restructuring must be documented as an agreed novation or alteration signed by all affected parties, including guarantors, or the change may not bind those whose consent was not taken.

s. 63 Promisee may dispense with or remit performance

Every promisee may dispense with or remit, wholly or in part, the performance of the promise made to him, or may extend the time for performance, or may accept any satisfaction he thinks fit. Section 63 is a striking departure from English law, which requires consideration or a deed for a binding release. In India, a creditor's agreement to accept less than the full debt, or to give time, is binding without fresh consideration. This is the statutory basis for one-time settlements, waivers and haircuts in restructuring.

Landmark — accord and satisfaction under s.63

In Kapur Chand Godha v Mir Nawab Himayatalikhan Azamjah the Supreme Court held that where a creditor accepts a smaller sum in full and final settlement of a larger claim, and the debtor pays it, the creditor cannot afterwards sue for the balance; the accord and satisfaction binds under section 63, no further consideration being needed. The cautionary corollary, from cases such as Day v McLea in the common law and applied in India, is that a payment "under protest" or accepted while expressly reserving rights does not amount to satisfaction. For a clean settlement, the documentation must record that the lesser sum is accepted in full and final discharge, without reservation, and signed by an authorised officer.

ss. 64 & 65 Consequences of rescission and restitution

When a person at whose option a contract is voidable rescinds it, the other party need not perform, and the rescinding party must restore any benefit received under the contract (s.64). Section 65 supplies the wider restitutionary principle: when an agreement is discovered to be void, or a contract becomes void, any person who has received an advantage under it must restore it, or compensate the person from whom it was received. Restitution under section 65 is the safety net that prevents unjust enrichment when a contract falls away, and it is regularly invoked when a frustrated or avoided transaction has to be unwound.

s. 55 Time as the essence of the contract

Whether time is of the essence determines whether late performance is a breach that lets the innocent party rescind, or merely a breach sounding in damages. Section 55 distinguishes three situations. Where time is essential and the promisor fails to perform on time, the contract becomes voidable at the option of the promisee. Where time is not essential, failure to perform on time does not make the contract voidable, but the promisee is entitled to compensation for the delay. And where time is essential but the promisee accepts late performance, the promisee cannot claim compensation for the delay unless he gave notice of intention to do so at the time of acceptance.

Landmark — time and immovable property

In commercial contracts for the supply of goods, time is ordinarily of the essence, as the Court recognised in Hind Construction v State of Maharashtra and Citadel Fine Pharmaceuticals v Ramaniyam Real Estates. By contrast, in contracts for the sale of immovable property, the settled presumption, restated in Chand Rani v Kamal Rani and Govind Prasad Chaturvedi v Hari Dutt Shastri, is that time is not of the essence unless the parties have clearly made it so by express stipulation, by the nature of the property or the surrounding circumstances. Even where a contract recites that time is of the essence, the conduct of the parties, such as repeated extensions, can waive the stipulation. The practitioner must therefore read both the clause and the conduct, and must serve a clear notice making time of the essence before treating delay as repudiatory.

Entire contracts and substantial performance

An entire contract is one where complete performance by one party is a condition precedent to the other's liability to pay. The classic illustration is Cutter v Powell, where a sailor who died before completing a voyage earned nothing, the wage being payable only on completion. The harshness of the entire-contract rule is tempered by the doctrine of substantial performance: where a party has substantially performed, with only minor defects or omissions, it may recover the contract price less the cost of remedying the defects, as in Hoenig v Isaacs. The distinction is central to construction disputes, where the question is whether a contractor who has not perfectly completed is entitled to payment subject to a deduction, or to nothing at all.

Memory aid — the restructuring toolkit in s.62 and s.63

Two sections do almost all the heavy lifting when a stressed loan is reworked. Section 62 gives you the structural moves: novate (replace the contract or a party), rescind (cancel it), alter (change a term). Section 63 gives you the concessionary moves without needing consideration: remit (accept less), extend time, accept any satisfaction. Whenever you restructure, ask which section you are operating under, document it as an agreement signed by every affected party including sureties, and record settlements as full-and-final and unconditional.

Chapter 6 · Section 56 and the modes of discharge

Discharge: frustration, force majeure and the limits of excuse

A contract may end in many ways: by performance, by agreement, by impossibility, by operation of law, by lapse of time, or by breach. The contested mode, and the one that generated a flood of litigation after 2020, is discharge by supervening impossibility under section 56, the doctrine of frustration.

s. 56 Agreement to do an impossible act; supervening impossibility

Section 56 has two limbs. The first is initial impossibility: an agreement to do an act impossible in itself is void. The second is the doctrine of frustration: a contract to do an act which, after the contract is made, becomes impossible, or by reason of some event the promisor could not prevent becomes unlawful, becomes void when the act becomes impossible or unlawful. A third part imposes a compensation obligation: where one person promised something he knew, or with reasonable diligence might have known, to be impossible, and the other did not know, the promisor must compensate the other for any loss sustained through the non-performance.

Section 56, supervening impossibility A contract to do an act which, after the contract is made, becomes impossible, or, by reason of some event which the promisor could not prevent, unlawful, becomes void when the act becomes impossible or unlawful.

The Indian doctrine of frustration is statutory, not English theory

The single most important Indian decision is Satyabrata Ghose v Mugneeram Bangur & Co. The Supreme Court held that in India the doctrine of frustration is not governed by the English theories of an implied term or a radical change in the obligation, but lives entirely within the positive language of section 56. The word "impossible" in the section does not mean only physical or literal impossibility; it includes impracticability and uselessness from the point of view of the object the parties had in mind. But a mere rise in cost, or a change that makes performance more onerous or less profitable, does not frustrate a contract. The test is whether the supervening event so fundamentally alters the basis of the contract that performance of the contract as agreed has become impossible.

Landmark line — the boundaries of frustration

Taylor v Caldwell (destruction of the subject matter) and Krell v Henry (the coronation cases, frustration of purpose) are the English origins. In India, Satyabrata Ghose domesticated the doctrine into section 56. Alopi Parshad & Sons v Union of India confirmed that commercial hardship, even a steep increase in cost during wartime, does not frustrate a contract; a contract is not frustrated merely because it has become more expensive to perform. Naihati Jute Mills v Khyaliram Jagannath held that frustration cannot be invoked where performance, though difficult, remains possible and the party could have guarded against the event. The cumulative message is that Indian courts apply frustration narrowly, reserving it for events that strike at the root of the contract, not for ordinary commercial misfortune.

Force majeure clauses and the section 32 / section 56 divide

The relationship between an express force majeure clause and the statutory doctrine of frustration was settled by the Supreme Court in Energy Watchdog v Central Electricity Regulatory Commission. The Court drew a clean line. Where the contract contains an express force majeure clause covering the supervening event, the matter is governed by that clause and falls under section 32 as a contingent contract; the contract is not frustrated but is dealt with on its own terms. Section 56 operates only as a residual doctrine, where the event is not provided for in the contract. On the facts, a rise in the price of imported coal did not trigger the force majeure clause and did not frustrate the contracts, because it merely made performance more expensive, not impossible.

Express force majeure clause → s.32

If the event is within the clause, the clause governs the consequences: suspension of obligations, extension of time, or termination, as the clause provides. The court enforces the parties' agreed allocation of risk. Read the clause for whether it lists the event, requires notice, and provides for mitigation.

No clause, or event outside it → s.56

If the contract is silent, or the clause does not cover the event, the residual doctrine of frustration applies. The contract becomes void only if the event makes performance impossible or radically different, not merely costlier. Restitution of benefits then follows under section 65.

The COVID-19 jurisprudence

The pandemic produced a rapid body of case law that, in substance, reaffirmed the pre-existing principles rather than relaxing them.

  • In Standard Retail v G.S. Global Corp the Bombay High Court refused to restrain invocation of letters of credit, holding that the lockdown did not frustrate a contract for the supply of steel where the force majeure clause favoured the seller and the contracts were capable of performance; distribution difficulties were not frustration.
  • In Halliburton Offshore Services v Vedanta the Delhi High Court granted limited interim protection against invocation of bank guarantees during the early lockdown, treating it as a special equity on the particular facts, while emphasising that the autonomy of bank guarantees and the narrow fraud and irretrievable-injustice exceptions remained intact.
  • In Ramanand v Dr. Girish Soni the Delhi High Court held that a tenant could not invoke section 56 to suspend rent, because a lease is a completed transfer creating an interest in property, and the doctrine of frustration does not apply to an executed lease; temporary non-use does not discharge the obligation to pay rent, though equitable relief on quantum might be considered in a fit case.
Settled point — frustration and leases

A recurring question is whether a lease can be frustrated. The Supreme Court in Raja Dhruv Dev Chand v Harmohinder Singh and the line following it, including T. Lakshmipathi v P. Nithyananda Reddy, holds that section 56 does not apply to a completed conveyance such as a lease, because a lease is not merely a contract but a transfer of an interest in property; the doctrine of frustration governs executory contracts, not executed demises. A lessee facing a supervening event must therefore rely on an express force majeure or cesser-of-rent clause in the lease, not on section 56. This is a frequent trap for tenants and a useful shield for landlords.

Drafting note — building a force majeure clause that works

Because section 56 is narrow and a court will hold parties to an express allocation of risk under section 32, the force majeure clause is where the real protection is engineered. A robust clause should: define the triggering events with both a list and a sweep-up; require that the event be beyond the affected party's reasonable control and not caused by its default; impose a notice obligation with a time limit; require the affected party to mitigate and to resume performance when able; specify the consequences (suspension, extension, price adjustment, and ultimately a right to terminate if the event persists beyond a long-stop period); and state expressly whether payment obligations are suspended, since courts are reluctant to excuse the payment of money on force majeure grounds. Without these elements the clause may leave the party worse off than the residual doctrine.

IBC angle — insolvency as discharge by operation of law, and the override of contractual termination

Insolvency interacts with discharge in two ways. First, an approved resolution plan novates and extinguishes the corporate debtor's old obligations by force of section 31 of the IBC, a statutory discharge that binds even dissenting creditors and is broader than anything section 62 of the Contract Act can achieve by consent. Second, the IBC restricts a counterparty's ordinary contractual right to terminate. The moratorium under section 14 of the IBC, and the protection of essential and critical supplies, prevent a supplier from terminating or suspending merely because the debtor has entered insolvency; the Supreme Court in Gujarat Urja Vikas Nigam v Amit Gupta restrained termination of a power purchase agreement that was triggered solely by the insolvency, where the contract was central to the debtor's survival. A practitioner advising a counterparty must therefore check whether a contractual termination right is neutralised by the Code before exercising it.

Chapter 7 · Section 39 and the law of breach

Breach & repudiation: actual, anticipatory and the doctrine of election

Breach is the trigger for every remedy. The Act says relatively little about it directly, but section 39 supplies the crucial rule on repudiation and election, and the common law it codifies governs anticipatory breach, the right to terminate, and the consequences of getting the election wrong.

Actual breach and anticipatory breach

An actual breach occurs when a party fails to perform when performance falls due, or performs defectively. An anticipatory breach occurs before performance is due, when one party renounces its obligations or disables itself from performing, signalling in advance that it will not perform. The distinction matters because anticipatory breach gives the innocent party an immediate right of action, without waiting for the date of performance to pass.

s. 39 Effect of refusal to perform, and the right of election

Section 39 provides that when a party to a contract has refused to perform, or disabled itself from performing, its promise in its entirety, the promisee may put an end to the contract, unless it has signified, by words or conduct, its acquiescence in the continuance of the contract. The section codifies two ideas: the innocent party has a right to terminate on a repudiation, and it has an election, it may accept the repudiation and end the contract, or it may affirm and keep the contract alive.

Landmark — the right to sue at once on anticipatory breach

In Hochster v De La Tour the English court established that on an anticipatory repudiation the innocent party may sue immediately, without waiting for the time fixed for performance. In Frost v Knight the principle was extended and refined: the innocent party may either accept the repudiation and sue at once, or keep the contract open, in which case it remains binding on both sides and is subject to the contingency that supervening events may discharge it before the date of performance. Indian courts apply these principles through section 39. The choice the innocent party makes, to accept or to affirm, has lasting consequences and cannot usually be reversed.

The doctrine of election, and its risks

Election is where many breach disputes are won and lost. If the innocent party accepts the repudiation, the contract is at an end, both parties are discharged from future performance, and damages are assessed as at the date of acceptance. If the innocent party affirms, the contract continues in force for the benefit of both parties, and the affirming party keeps its own obligations and bears the risk that a later event may discharge the contract or that the affirming party may itself fall into breach.

The other side has repudiated. What are your options under s.39?
Accept the repudiation

Treat the contract as ended. You are discharged from further performance and may sue for damages assessed at the date of acceptance. You must take reasonable steps to mitigate from that point. The election is generally irrevocable.

Affirm the contract

Keep it alive and insist on performance. The contract binds both sides; you must remain ready and willing to perform your part. You risk a supervening frustrating event, or your own later default, discharging the other party's liability.

Caution — affirming keeps the contract alive for both sides

The danger of affirmation is illustrated by Avery v Bowden: where the innocent party kept the contract open after a repudiation, and a supervening event (the outbreak of war making performance illegal) then discharged the contract, the innocent party lost the right to sue, because by the time performance fell due the contract had been frustrated rather than breached. The practical lesson is that affirmation is not a costless way to preserve options; an innocent party who keeps a contract alive must continue to perform and exposes itself to events that may extinguish the very claim it was preserving.

Continuing breach and the right to terminate

A continuing breach is one that recurs from day to day, such as a persistent failure to maintain a standard required by the contract; each day's failure may give a fresh cause of action and affect the running of limitation. Not every breach justifies termination. Only a breach that goes to the root of the contract, a breach of a condition or a fundamental term, or a repudiatory breach, entitles the innocent party to terminate. A breach of a minor or subsidiary term (a warranty) sounds only in damages and does not justify ending the contract. The practitioner's task on a defaulting counterparty is to characterise the breach correctly before purporting to terminate, because a wrongful termination is itself a repudiation that the other side may accept, turning the terminating party into the contract-breaker.

Rights after breach

On an accepted breach the innocent party's principal remedy is compensation in damages under sections 73 and 75, examined in the next chapter. Where damages are an inadequate remedy, specific performance or injunction may be available under the Specific Relief Act, 1963, as reshaped by the 2018 amendment which made specific performance a more general remedy rather than a discretionary exception. A party rightfully rescinding for breach is entitled under section 75 to compensation for any damage sustained through the non-fulfilment of the contract. And the innocent party is throughout under a duty to mitigate, taking reasonable steps to reduce its loss.

Drafting note — events of default and termination machinery

In a facility agreement the law of breach is operationalised through the events of default and the termination and acceleration clauses. Good drafting separates true events of default (which permit acceleration) from mere review or covenant events, builds in cure periods and materiality thresholds, includes cross-default and cross-acceleration triggers calibrated to the borrower's group, and states clearly that the lender's rights are cumulative and that no failure to exercise a right operates as a waiver. The waiver point connects directly to section 39 and the doctrine of election: a lender that continues to accept payments and deal with a defaulting borrower may be held to have affirmed and waived the default, so a "no waiver" clause and an express reservation of rights are essential to preserve the right to terminate.

Memory aid — the breach decision sequence

Faced with a counterparty's default, work through five questions in order. One, is the breach actual or anticipatory. Two, is it repudiatory (root of the contract) or merely a breach of warranty. Three, do you accept or affirm under section 39. Four, if you accept, when is the date for assessing damages and from when must you mitigate. Five, are damages adequate, or do you need specific performance or injunction. Getting question three wrong, by wrongfully terminating or by inadvertently affirming, is the most common and most expensive error.

Chapter 8 · Sections 73 to 75

Damages the measure of compensation

This is the chapter the practice turns on. Almost every commercial dispute that is not about whether a contract exists is about what the breach is worth. Sections 73 to 75 codify the common law of compensatory damages: section 73 fixes the measure and the limits of recoverable loss, section 74 governs pre-agreed sums and the penalty doctrine, and section 75 preserves compensation for a party that rightfully rescinds. Indian damages law is compensatory, not punitive. The object is to put the injured party, so far as money can, in the position it would have occupied had the contract been performed, never to punish the defaulter or to give the claimant a windfall.

Section 73, the compensatory principle and the remoteness rule When a contract has been broken, the party who suffers is entitled to receive compensation for any loss or damage caused by the breach which naturally arose in the usual course of things from the breach, or which the parties knew, when they made the contract, to be likely to result from its breach. But such compensation is not to be given for any remote and indirect loss or damage sustained by reason of the breach.

The architecture of section 73

Section 73 does three things at once. It states the entitlement to compensation, it fixes the test of remoteness that limits that entitlement, and through its explanation it imports the duty to mitigate by directing that the means available to the injured party for remedying the inconvenience caused by the breach must be taken into account. A practitioner pricing a claim works through these three filters in sequence: first establish the loss caused in fact by the breach, then ask how much of that loss is too remote to recover, then ask how much of the recoverable loss the claimant could and should have avoided.

Remoteness: the two limbs of Hadley v Baxendale

The remoteness rule in section 73 is the statutory enactment of the rule in Hadley v Baxendale. The case concerned a mill whose broken crankshaft was delayed in carriage; the mill claimed lost profits for the period the mill stood idle. The court refused the lost profits and laid down the two-limb test that governs to this day. Loss is recoverable if it falls within either limb.

First limb — general damages

Loss arising naturally, in the usual course of things from the breach. This is loss that any reasonable person would foresee as the ordinary consequence of the breach, and it needs no special knowledge to be recoverable. It is imputed to the defaulter whether or not it actually contemplated it.

Second limb — special damages

Loss that does not arise in the ordinary course, but which the parties actually knew, at the time of contracting, was likely to result from a breach. Such loss is recoverable only if the special circumstances were communicated to and known by the defaulter when the contract was made, not when it was broken.

The decisive feature is the time at which knowledge is tested. The relevant contemplation is at the date of the contract, not the date of the breach. A loss that would be extraordinary becomes recoverable only if the special circumstances generating it were brought home to the other side before it bound itself. This is why well advised parties record, at the contracting stage, the consequences a delay or default will cause, and why the other side resists doing so.

Refinement — the degree of foreseeability required

In Victoria Laundry (Windsor) Ltd v Newman Industries Ltd the late delivery of a boiler made the laundry liable for the ordinary loss of profits the buyer would obviously suffer, but not for an exceptionally lucrative dyeing contract of which the seller had no notice. In Koufos v C Czarnikow Ltd (The Heron II) the House of Lords clarified the standard of foreseeability in contract: the loss must be not unlikely or of a kind the defaulter should have realised was a serious possibility, a higher threshold than the mere reasonable foreseeability that governs tort. Indian courts apply the same gradation under section 73.

Indian authority on remoteness

In Madras Railway Co v Govinda Rau a tailor delivered a sewing machine and cloth for carriage to a destination to take up a lucrative festival engagement; the goods were delayed and he sued for the special profits lost. The court allowed only ordinary damages, refusing the special loss because the carrier had no notice of the special purpose. The case is the classic Indian illustration of the second limb: special loss is irrecoverable unless the special circumstances were communicated. The principle recurs constantly in infrastructure and supply disputes, where a contractor's downstream losses are recoverable only to the extent the counterparty knew of the downstream commitments.

The duty to mitigate

The explanation to section 73 requires the court to take into account the means the injured party had of remedying the inconvenience caused by the breach. This is the duty to mitigate. The injured party cannot recover for loss it could have avoided by taking reasonable steps, it cannot recover for loss it actually avoided, and it can recover the reasonable costs of mitigation even where the attempt failed. The duty is not a duty to act reasonably for the defaulter's benefit at the claimant's own risk; it requires only reasonable steps, judged from the claimant's position at the time, not with hindsight.

Landmark — mitigation is built into the measure

In Murlidhar Chiranjilal v Harishchandra Dwarkadas the Supreme Court held that the two foundations of damages assessment are that the party complaining of breach must be placed in the same position as if the contract had been performed, and that it must take all reasonable steps to mitigate, being debarred from claiming any loss it could have avoided. In M Lachia Setty & Sons Ltd v Coffee Board the Court confirmed that where a buyer defaults, the seller's resale in a falling market, if conducted reasonably, fixes the measure, and the buyer cannot complain of a price obtained in a bona fide resale. The English source, British Westinghouse v Underground Electric Railways, supplies the rule that benefits actually obtained through reasonable mitigating action are credited against the loss.

Frequently misunderstood — mitigation is not a precondition to suit

Mitigation does not bar the claim; it reduces the recoverable quantum. The burden of proving a failure to mitigate lies on the defaulter, not the claimant, and it is a burden of showing that reasonable steps were available and not taken. A claimant does not have to show it mitigated; the defendant has to show it did not. A second misunderstanding is that mitigation requires accepting the defaulter's own offer of substitute performance; ordinarily the innocent party need not accept further dealings with a party that has repudiated, though refusing a plainly reasonable cure may be held unreasonable.

Measure of damages: the three interests

Behind the section 73 measure lie three distinct interests a claimant may seek to protect, and identifying which one is in play disciplines the quantum analysis.

Types of damages

  • General (ordinary) damages. Loss flowing naturally from the breach under the first limb. Presumed and need not be specially pleaded with particularity.
  • Special damages. Loss recoverable only under the second limb, requiring notice of special circumstances and specific pleading.
  • Nominal damages. A small sum awarded where a breach is proved but no actual loss is shown, vindicating the right without compensating a loss.
  • Consequential damages. Indirect losses flowing from the breach, recoverable only so far as they pass the remoteness test; commonly excluded or capped by contract.
  • Loss of profits. Recoverable where proved with reasonable certainty and not too remote; the central head in commercial supply and works disputes.
  • Loss of opportunity. The lost chance of a benefit, recoverable where the chance was real and substantial, valued by reference to its probability.
  • Exemplary or punitive damages. Generally not available for breach of contract in India, because the measure is compensatory; this marks the sharpest divide between contract and tort.

s. 74 Liquidated damages and the penalty doctrine

Section 74 is the most commercially significant provision in the chapter, because almost every substantial contract fixes a sum payable on breach, whether as liquidated damages, a forfeiture of earnest money, or a stipulated rate of interest on default. The section departs deliberately from the English distinction between enforceable liquidated damages and unenforceable penalties, and substitutes a single statutory rule.

Section 74, compensation for a stipulated sum When a contract is broken, if a sum is named in the contract as the amount to be paid in case of breach, or if it contains any other stipulation by way of penalty, the party complaining is entitled to receive from the party in breach reasonable compensation not exceeding the amount so named, whether or not actual damage or loss is proved to have been caused.

The statutory scheme has three features that a practitioner must hold together. First, the named sum operates as a ceiling, not an entitlement: the claimant cannot recover more than the stipulated figure, but is not automatically entitled to it. Second, what the claimant recovers is reasonable compensation, which the court assesses. Third, the section dispenses with strict proof of actual loss in cases where loss is difficult to prove, but it does not dispense with the requirement that loss or injury must in fact have flowed from the breach.

1963
Fateh Chand v Balkishan Dass. The Supreme Court held that section 74 abolishes the English distinction between penalty and liquidated damages and entitles the innocent party only to reasonable compensation not exceeding the named sum, whether the sum is a penalty or a genuine pre-estimate. Forfeiture of a sum already paid is subject to the same control.
1969
Maula Bux v Union of India. Where loss is incapable of precise proof, as in many supply defaults, the named sum may be taken as a reasonable measure; but where loss can be proved, the claimant must prove it and recovers only what it proves. Earnest money genuinely deposited as security may be forfeited if reasonable.
2003
ONGC v Saw Pipes Ltd. Where the contract expressly stipulates a sum as a genuine pre-estimate of damage, and the loss is difficult to prove, the court may award the stipulated amount without insisting on proof of actual loss, provided the sum is a reasonable pre-estimate and not a penalty. This relaxed the proof requirement in commercial contracts.
2015
Kailash Nath Associates v DDA. The definitive restatement. Section 74 awards reasonable compensation for damage or loss caused by the breach; if no legal injury results, no compensation is due and even a stipulated sum cannot be recovered. The named sum is the outer limit of a genuine pre-estimate; damage or loss is a sine qua non. Forfeiture of earnest money is permissible only where it is a reasonable amount and loss is shown or presumed.
Reconciling Saw Pipes and Kailash Nath

The two leading modern authorities are read together, not in opposition. Saw Pipes holds that where loss is genuinely difficult to quantify and the sum is a reasonable pre-estimate, the court need not insist on proof of the precise figure and may award the stipulated amount. Kailash Nath insists that some loss or injury, in fact caused by the breach, must exist; section 74 does not permit recovery of a stipulated sum where the breach caused no loss at all. The settled position is therefore: damage or loss caused by the breach is essential; once that threshold is crossed, the difficulty of exact proof does not defeat a reasonable pre-estimate up to the named ceiling.

Application — construction and government contracts

In Construction and Design Services v DDA the Supreme Court applied Kailash Nath to a delay damages clause, holding that even without precise proof some compensation may be presumed where the nature of the breach makes loss likely, but the burden of displacing the presumption can shift and the award must remain reasonable. The practical effect for infrastructure work is that a liquidated damages or delay clause is enforceable up to its cap as reasonable compensation, but the employer cannot mechanically deduct the full sum if the contractor shows that no loss, or a much smaller loss, actually resulted.

Earnest money and forfeiture

Earnest money sits at the intersection of sections 73, 74 and the law of deposits. A genuine earnest deposit, given as security for due performance and part of the price, may be forfeited on the buyer's default without proof of loss, provided the amount is reasonable. A sum that is not true earnest but is in substance a penalty is controlled by section 74 and may be forfeited only to the extent of reasonable compensation.

Genuine earnest — forfeitable

Shree Hanuman Cotton Mills v Tata Air Craft Ltd sets out the indicia of earnest money: it is part of the purchase price, paid as a guarantee of performance, and liable to forfeiture on default. A reasonable earnest may be forfeited as such.

Earnest in immovable property sales

Satish Batra v Sudhir Rawal confirmed that earnest money in a contract for sale of immovable property may be forfeited on the buyer's default if it is reasonable, but a forfeiture of an amount that is in truth a penalty is subject to section 74 and the reasonable compensation control.

s. 75 Compensation for a party rightfully rescinding

Section 75 completes the scheme: a person who rightfully rescinds a contract is entitled to compensation for any damage sustained through the non fulfilment of the contract. The provision ensures that lawful termination for the other side's breach does not strip the innocent party of its damages claim; rescission ends future performance but preserves the accrued right to be compensated for the breach that justified it. The link to chapter 7 is direct: a party that validly accepts a repudiation under section 39 looks to section 75 for its compensation, measured by the section 73 principles.

Application — construction and infrastructure measure

In McDermott International Inc v Burn Standard Co Ltd the Supreme Court addressed the measure of damages in a large works contract and endorsed established methods of computing loss, including formula based approaches to delay and disruption such as Hudson, Emden and Eichleay, while cautioning that any formula is only an aid and the claimant must still prove loss caused by the breach. For an infrastructure practitioner the case is the reference point for proving prolongation and disruption claims, where the measure is the contractor's additional time related cost, not an abstract percentage.

Share purchase agreements and commercial contracts

In an SPA, the damages analysis turns on the warranty and indemnity architecture. Breach of warranty sounds in damages measured by the difference between the value of the shares as warranted and their true value, subject to remoteness and mitigation. Because that measure is hard to prove and is exposed to the mitigation and remoteness filters, sophisticated SPAs convert key risks into indemnities, which provide a rupee for rupee recovery on a defined trigger and sidestep the section 73 limitations, examined in the next chapter. Liability caps, baskets, de minimis thresholds and survival periods are the contractual counterparts to the common law controls of remoteness and mitigation.

Memory aid — pricing any breach claim

Run the claim through four gates in order. Cause: what loss did the breach actually cause. Remoteness: is it first limb (natural) or second limb (special, needing notice at contract date). Chapter 9 · Sections 124 to 125

Indemnity shifting a defined risk

An indemnity is a promise to make good a loss. It is the workhorse of transactional risk allocation: in an SPA, a facility agreement or a settlement, the indemnity is how parties move a specified risk from the party who would otherwise bear it onto the party better placed to control or absorb it. The two statutory sections are narrow, but the common law has built a far wider commercial instrument on top of them, and the practitioner must know where the Act ends and the contract begins.

Section 124, contract of indemnity A contract by which one party promises to save the other from loss caused to him by the conduct of the promisor himself, or by the conduct of any other person, is called a contract of indemnity.

The statutory definition and its narrowness

On its literal terms section 124 covers only loss caused by human conduct, that of the indemnifier or of some other person. It does not, on its face, extend to loss caused by accident, by an act of God, or by the operation of law. This is narrower than the English understanding of indemnity, which embraces a promise to save another harmless from loss whatever its cause. Indian courts have not confined commercial indemnities to the literal scope of section 124; they enforce wider indemnities as valid contracts under the general law, treating section 124 as describing one species rather than exhausting the genus. For drafting purposes the lesson is that the scope of an indemnity is whatever the words make it, and the parties routinely contract for indemnities far broader than section 124 describes.

Section 125, rights of the indemnity-holder when sued An indemnity-holder, acting within his authority, is entitled to recover from the indemnifier all damages he may be compelled to pay in any suit in respect of any matter to which the indemnity applies, all costs he may be compelled to pay in such suit, and all sums paid under the terms of any compromise of such suit, provided the compromise was not contrary to the indemnifier's orders and was prudent.

Commencement of liability: must the indemnity-holder pay first?

The most litigated question on indemnity is one of timing: can the indemnity-holder compel the indemnifier to provide the money before the indemnity-holder has actually paid out, or must it first suffer the loss and then claim reimbursement? Section 125 speaks of sums the indemnity-holder is compelled to pay, which on a strict reading suggests payment must precede recovery. The courts have rejected that narrow reading.

Landmark — indemnity before payment

In Gajanan Moreshwar Parelkar v Moreshwar Madan Mantri the Bombay High Court held that an indemnity-holder who has incurred an absolute liability is entitled to call upon the indemnifier to put him in funds to meet that liability, even before he has actually paid. The court reasoned that if indemnity could be enforced only after payment, it might be worthless to an indemnity-holder who could not pay in the first place. The decision, drawing on the English equity in Richardson, In re, ex parte the Governors of St Thomas's Hospital, establishes that liability under an indemnity arises when the indemnified loss becomes absolute, not only when it is discharged. This is the cornerstone of modern indemnity practice.

The earlier authority of Adamson v Jarvis supplies the underlying principle that a person who acts on another's request and incurs liability as a result is entitled to be indemnified by the person who requested the act. Together these cases give the indemnity-holder a right that crystallises on the incurring of an absolute liability and is enforceable, in a proper case, by an order that the indemnifier provide funds to meet it.

Indemnity distinguished from guarantee

The distinction is fundamental and frequently litigated, because it decides whether the surety-protective rules of sections 126 to 147 apply.

Drafting note — why deals prefer indemnities

Transactional lawyers favour indemnities over damages claims for three reasons. First, an indemnity gives a debt claim for a defined sum on a defined trigger, avoiding the section 73 obstacles of remoteness and the requirement to prove loss as the natural or contemplated consequence of a breach. Second, the duty to mitigate does not automatically cut down an indemnity unless the contract so provides, so the indemnified party's recovery is more certain. Third, an indemnity can be drafted to respond to events that are not breaches at all, such as a tax liability or a third-party claim arising from pre-completion matters. The trade-off is that the indemnifier resists open-ended exposure, which is why indemnities are negotiated with caps, baskets, de minimis floors, time limits and conduct-of-claims provisions.

Indemnities in restructuring and security

Indemnities pervade restructuring documents. A resolution applicant under an IBC plan will seek indemnities from the resolution professional process for undisclosed liabilities, and the plan itself often extinguishes pre-CIRP claims so that the clean entity is not undermined by stale indemnity demands. Lenders take indemnities from borrowers and sponsors for costs of enforcement, increased costs, tax gross-ups and currency losses. In security documents, the indemnity for enforcement costs is what allows the secured creditor to add its realisation expenses to the secured debt. Each of these is a primary obligation that survives independently of any guarantee analysis.

Memory aid — indemnity versus guarantee in one line

An indemnity is I will bear your loss, a primary promise standing on its own. A guarantee is I will pay if he does not, a secondary promise that lives and dies with the principal debt. If there is no third person whose default is being answered for, you are looking at an indemnity, and the surety-protection rules do not apply.

Chapter 10 · Sections 126 to 147

Guarantee the surety's bargain

For an insolvency and restructuring lawyer this is the most important chapter in the Act. Personal and corporate guarantees are the connective tissue of secured lending: when the principal debtor enters insolvency, the creditor's recovery often depends on the guarantee, and the guarantor's liability, its discharge and its survival of a resolution plan are now central to IBC practice. The law gives the surety a suite of protections, and a great deal of restructuring litigation is the creditor and the guarantor fighting over whether those protections have been triggered.

Section 126, the tripartite definition A contract of guarantee is a contract to perform the promise, or discharge the liability, of a third person in case of his default. The person who gives the guarantee is the surety; the person in respect of whose default the guarantee is given is the principal debtor; and the person to whom the guarantee is given is the creditor. A guarantee may be either oral or written.

The tripartite structure and the three contracts

A guarantee binds three parties through three related contracts: the principal contract of debt between creditor and principal debtor, the contract of guarantee between creditor and surety, and an implied contract between the principal debtor and the surety under which the principal must indemnify the surety for sums properly paid. Understanding which contract is in play resolves most guarantee disputes. The surety's liability is secondary: it answers for the default of another and does not, by guaranteeing, become the primary obligor.

Section 128, the co-extensive principle The liability of the surety is co-extensive with that of the principal debtor, unless it is otherwise provided by the contract.

Co-extensiveness is the master key. The surety's liability is the same in extent as the principal debtor's, no greater and no less, subject to any contractual limit. It follows that whatever reduces the principal's liability reduces the surety's, and whatever the principal could raise as a defence on the merits of the debt is generally open to the surety. But co-extensiveness also means the surety's liability is immediate on default; the creditor need not first proceed against the principal or exhaust securities before calling the surety.

Landmark — the creditor need not exhaust the principal first

In Bank of Bihar v Damodar Prasad the Supreme Court held that the creditor is not bound to exhaust its remedies against the principal debtor before suing the surety, and a direction requiring the creditor to do so was set aside. The surety's liability is immediate upon the principal's default unless the contract postpones it. In State Bank of India v Indexport Registered the Court confirmed that a decree-holder with a decree against both the principal debtor and the surety may execute it against the surety first, and is not obliged to proceed against the principal or against the mortgaged property before enforcing against the guarantor. These two cases are the bedrock of bank guarantee enforcement.

Specific and continuing guarantees

Section 129 distinguishes a guarantee that extends to a single transaction from a continuing guarantee, which extends to a series of transactions. The distinction governs the extent of the surety's exposure and the manner of revocation. A continuing guarantee covers a running account or a revolving facility up to its limit, and is the standard form in working-capital lending.

Sections 133 to 139 are the heart of guarantee litigation. They protect the surety by discharging it where the creditor or the principal does something that alters the bargain the surety guaranteed or prejudices the surety's position. Every restructuring that touches a guaranteed facility must be tested against these sections, because an unconsidered variation can release the very guarantee the lender is relying on.

Critical restructuring trap — variance and the giving of time

The single most dangerous moment for a guarantee in a workout is the variation of the facility terms. A rescheduling, an interest reset, an extension of the repayment date or a standstill can each be a variance under section 133 or a giving of time under section 135, discharging the guarantor unless it consents. The defensive practice is to obtain the guarantor's written consent and confirmation of continuing liability at every amendment, and to include in the guarantee an advance consent clause by which the surety agrees that variations, time and indulgences granted to the principal shall not discharge it. Section 128's closing words, unless it is otherwise provided by the contract, are what make such clauses effective; without them the protective sections operate automatically.

Saving for the surety's consent and reservation of rights

Two qualifications soften the protective sections. First, all of sections 133 to 135 yield to the surety's consent, which is why the consent and confirmation mechanism works. Second, where the creditor, while giving time to or composing with the principal, expressly reserves its rights against the surety, the surety is not discharged, because the reservation preserves the surety's recourse. A practitioner negotiating a standstill or a one-time settlement with a principal debtor must decide deliberately whether to bind in the guarantor by consent or to reserve rights against it, and must paper that choice.

Rights of the surety

  • Subrogation, section 140. On the principal's default and the surety's payment of the guaranteed debt, the surety is invested with all the rights the creditor had against the principal debtor. The surety steps into the creditor's shoes, including the benefit of the creditor's securities and remedies.
  • Benefit of securities, section 141. The surety is entitled to the benefit of every security the creditor holds against the principal at the time the guarantee is entered into, whether or not the surety knew of it; and if the creditor loses or parts with such a security without the surety's consent, the surety is discharged to the extent of its value. This dovetails with section 139.
  • Right against the principal. The implied contract of indemnity entitles the surety to recover from the principal debtor whatever sums it has rightfully paid under the guarantee.
Section 142, guarantee obtained by misrepresentation Any guarantee obtained by means of misrepresentation made by the creditor, or with his knowledge and assent, concerning a material part of the transaction, is invalid. Section 143 adds that a guarantee obtained by the creditor's keeping silence as to a material circumstance is likewise invalid.

Bank guarantees and their autonomy

A bank guarantee, particularly an unconditional or on-demand guarantee, occupies a special position. It is treated as an autonomous contract between the bank and the beneficiary, independent of the underlying contract between the beneficiary and the bank's customer. The settled rule is that courts will not interfere with the enforcement of an unconditional bank guarantee or letter of credit, and will not allow the underlying disputes to be imported into the guarantee, because the commercial utility of these instruments depends on their being honoured as good as cash.

The autonomy principle

In UP Cooperative Federation Ltd v Singh Consultants and Engineers and Hindustan Construction Co v State of Bihar the Supreme Court held that an unconditional bank guarantee must be honoured according to its terms, irrespective of disputes under the underlying contract, and that injunctions restraining encashment should be refused as a rule.

The two narrow exceptions

Enforcement may be restrained only on proof of (i) egregious fraud of which the bank has notice, going to the root of the transaction, or (ii) irretrievable injustice or special equities of an exceptional kind. Dwarikesh Sugar Industries v Prem Heavy Engineering, Himadri Chemicals v Coal Tar Refining, U P State Sugar Corp v Sumac International and Standard Chartered Bank v Heavy Engineering Corp (2019) define these exceptions strictly.

Performance versus financial guarantees

A financial guarantee secures repayment of a debt; a performance guarantee secures the due performance of an obligation, such as completion of works to specification. The distinction matters in valuation and in IBC claims. A letter of comfort sits below both: depending on its wording it may be a binding guarantee, a mere statement of present intention, or something in between, and the courts construe each by its language. The drafting lesson is that comfort letters relied on as credit support must be tested for whether they create an enforceable obligation at all.

Guarantees under the IBC: the decisive modern battleground

The interaction of guarantees with the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code is where this chapter earns its place at the centre of restructuring practice. Four propositions, settled by the Supreme Court, must be at a practitioner's fingertips.

2018
State Bank of India v V Ramakrishnan. The moratorium under section 14 of the IBC, which protects the corporate debtor during its insolvency, does not extend to the personal guarantor of the corporate debtor. A creditor may proceed against the personal guarantor even while the principal debtor is shielded by the moratorium.
2019
Committee of Creditors of Essar Steel v Satish Kumar Gupta. Confirmed that the approval of a resolution plan that pays the creditor part of its claim does not by itself extinguish the creditor's right to proceed against a guarantor for the balance, subject to the terms of the plan and the guarantee.
2021
Lalit Kumar Jain v Union of India. Upheld the notification bringing personal guarantors to corporate debtors within the IBC, and held that the approval of a resolution plan for the corporate debtor does not ipso facto discharge the personal guarantor of its liabilities. The guarantor's liability survives the plan unless the guarantee or the plan expressly releases it.
principle
Maharashtra State Electricity Board v Official Liquidator. A statutory or involuntary discharge of the principal debtor, such as on the winding up of the company, does not automatically discharge the surety; section 134 addresses a voluntary release by the creditor, not a discharge by operation of law. The guarantor remains liable even though the principal can no longer be pursued.
Synthesis — why the guarantee survives the plan

The combined effect of Lalit Kumar Jain, Essar Steel and Maharashtra SEB is that a resolution plan binds the corporate debtor and extinguishes claims against it, but does not by its own force release the guarantors. The reduction of the principal debt to the plan figure is an involuntary statutory outcome, not a voluntary creditor release under section 134, so the guarantor is not discharged. This is why lenders insist on retaining their guarantee claims through a CIRP, why resolution applicants try to negotiate guarantor releases into the plan, and why personal guarantors of distressed groups now face parallel insolvency proceedings of their own. The practitioner advising any party to a stressed credit must map, at the outset, every guarantee in the structure and ask whether the contemplated resolution preserves or releases it.

SARFAESI and guarantee enforcement

Under the SARFAESI Act a secured creditor may enforce against the security interest created by a guarantor who has charged property as collateral, and may proceed against a guarantor as a borrower within the extended statutory definition. The guarantor's co-extensive liability under section 128 means the creditor can pursue the guaranteed debt against the guarantor's secured assets without first exhausting the principal's, consistent with Bank of Bihar and Indexport. The recovery toolkit against a defaulting guarantor therefore runs in parallel: a debt or summary suit, IBC proceedings against a personal guarantor, and SARFAESI enforcement against any property the guarantor has mortgaged.

Memory aid — the surety's seven escape routes

A guarantor seeking discharge looks to: variance (s.133), release of the principal (s.134), composition or time given (s.135), loss or release of a security (ss.139 and 141), misrepresentation or concealment by the creditor (ss.142 to 143), and the absence of consideration or of the co-extensive principal debt. The creditor's answers are the surety's consent, an express reservation of rights, and the otherwise provided by the contract saving in section 128. Master the seven routes and their three answers and you can run either side of any guarantee dispute.

Chapter 11 · Sections 148 to 171

Bailment possession without ownership

Bailment is the law of goods delivered for a purpose on terms that they be returned or dealt with as directed. It matters to a banking and insolvency lawyer for two reasons: it is the genus of which pledge, the principal possessory security, is a species, and the banker's general lien under section 171 is one of the most useful self-help rights a lender holds. Get the bailment fundamentals right and the pledge and lien analysis follows.

Section 148, definition of bailment A bailment is the delivery of goods by one person to another for some purpose, upon a contract that they shall, when the purpose is accomplished, be returned or otherwise disposed of according to the directions of the person delivering them. The person delivering is the bailor; the person to whom they are delivered is the bailee.

Essential ingredients

Bailment has three elements: delivery of possession of goods, for a purpose, on a contract or undertaking to return or deal with the goods as directed. The defining feature is the transfer of possession without transfer of ownership. The bailor retains the general property in the goods; the bailee acquires only a special property, namely lawful possession for the purpose of the bailment. Delivery may be actual, by handing over the goods, or constructive, by doing something that puts the goods in the bailee's possession, such as handing over the keys to a warehouse.

Duties of the bailee

  • Reasonable care, sections 151 to 152. The bailee must take as much care of the goods as a person of ordinary prudence would take of his own goods of the same description. If it does so, it is not liable for loss not due to its negligence; the standard is uniform regardless of whether the bailment is gratuitous or for reward.
  • Not to make unauthorised use, sections 153 to 154. Use of the goods inconsistent with the terms of the bailment makes the bailee liable for any resulting loss, and entitles the bailor to terminate.
  • Not to mix, sections 155 to 157. Rules govern the consequences where the bailee mixes the bailor's goods with its own or with others'.
  • To return on accomplishment of the purpose, section 160. The bailee must return or deliver the goods according to the bailor's directions once the purpose is achieved or the time has expired; failing which it is liable under section 161 for any loss thereafter.

Lien: the bailee's security

A lien is the right to retain possession of goods until a claim is satisfied. The Act recognises two species, and the difference between them is one of the most practically important distinctions in commercial law.

Particular lien — s. 170

Where the bailee has, by its labour or skill, improved or added value to the goods, it may retain those specific goods until it is paid for that work. It is confined to the goods worked on and secures only the charge for that work. A repairer may hold the machine it repaired until its repair bill is paid, but not against an unrelated debt.

General lien — s. 171

A general lien is the right to retain any goods of the debtor in the holder's possession as security for the general balance of account, not merely for a charge connected with those goods. Section 171 confers it by law on bankers, factors, wharfingers, attorneys of a High Court and policy brokers; others have it only by express contract.

The banker's general lien

The banker's general lien under section 171 is a powerful and frequently invoked self-help right. It entitles a bank to retain securities and goods of the customer that come into its possession in the ordinary course of banking business, as security for the general balance the customer owes, unless there is a contract, express or implied, inconsistent with the lien. The lien does not extend to property entrusted to the bank for a specific purpose inconsistent with a lien, such as securities deposited for safe custody only, and it does not attach to money or property the bank holds in a capacity other than as banker. In a distressed credit the general lien lets the bank set off and retain instruments such as fixed deposits and securities held for the customer against the defaulted exposure, subject to the terms on which they were deposited.

Frequently misunderstood — lien is a right to retain, not to sell

A lien at common law and under sections 170 and 171 is, by itself, only a right to retain possession; it is not, without more, a right to sell the goods to realise the debt. The power of sale arises where it is conferred by statute or by contract. This is precisely where a pledge differs from a bare lien, because the pledgee has a statutory power of sale under section 176. A lender relying on retained goods as effective security must therefore ensure it holds them as pledgee, or has a contractual power of sale, rather than relying on a lien that leaves it holding goods it cannot turn into money.

Memory aid — particular versus general lien

Particular lien is particular in two senses: it attaches to the particular goods worked on and secures only the particular charge for that work. General lien is general in both: it attaches to any goods in possession and secures the general balance of account. Bankers, factors, wharfingers, attorneys and policy brokers get the general lien by law; everyone else needs a contract.

Chapter 12 · Sections 172 to 181

Pledge possessory security with a power of sale

Pledge is the bailment of goods as security for a debt, and it is the oldest and cleanest form of secured lending. Its commercial value lies in the pledgee's statutory power of sale on default, which a bare lien lacks. In modern practice the pledge of dematerialised securities, shares held in a depository, is among the most common forms of collateral for promoter and acquisition finance, and its enforcement in insolvency has produced significant recent jurisprudence.

Section 172, pledge defined The bailment of goods as security for payment of a debt or performance of a promise is called a pledge. The bailor is in this case called the pawnor and the bailee the pawnee.

Pledge as a species of bailment

A pledge is a bailment for a specific purpose, namely to secure a debt or the performance of a promise. The general property in the goods remains with the pawnor; the pawnee acquires a special property, a right to retain the goods as security and, crucially, a power to sell them on default. Because possession is essential, the pledge depends on delivery, actual or constructive, of the goods to the pawnee. Where possession cannot pass, the appropriate security is a hypothecation or charge, not a pledge.

Rights of the pawnee

Landmark — sale requires notice, and the debt survives a shortfall

Section 176 makes the notice of sale mandatory and not capable of being waived by contract; a sale without reasonable notice is wrongful, and the pawnor may recover damages or redeem. In Lallan Prasad v Rahmat Ali the Supreme Court held that a pawnee who sues on the debt must be in a position to return the pledged goods, because the pawnor's right to redeem subsists until the goods are lawfully sold; a pawnee who has parted with the goods without a valid sale cannot also recover the full debt. If after a lawful sale the proceeds fall short, the pawnor remains liable for the balance; if they exceed the debt, the pawnee holds the surplus for the pawnor.

Redemption, and pledge by one without full title

  • Right to redeem, section 177. The pawnor may redeem the goods at any time before the pawnee makes an actual sale, even after the stipulated time for payment has passed, on payment of the debt and any expenses arising from the default. The right to redeem is extinguished only by a lawful sale.
  • Pledge by a mercantile agent, section 178. A pledge made by a mercantile agent in possession of goods with the owner's consent is valid as against the owner, provided the pawnee acts in good faith and without notice of any want of authority. This protects lenders who take goods in pledge from agents.
  • Pledge by a person with limited interest, section 179. Where a person pledges goods in which it has only a limited interest, the pledge is valid to the extent of that interest.
High Court and Supreme Court application

In Morvi Mercantile Bank v Union of India the Supreme Court recognised that a pledge of documents of title to goods, such as railway receipts, can constitute a valid pledge of the goods themselves, which is essential to trade finance secured on goods in transit. In Bank of Bihar v State of Bihar the Court held that where pledged goods are seized by the State from the pawnee, the pawnee's special property entitles it to recover their value, the State taking the goods subject to the pledge. These cases secure the pawnee's position against third parties and the State.

Demat and share pledges, and their enforcement in insolvency

The pledge of dematerialised securities is governed by the Depositories Act, 1996 and its regulations, which overlay the Contract Act framework. A demat pledge is created by recording the pledge in the depository system; on default the pledgee invokes the pledge and the securities are transferred to the pledgee's account, after which they may be sold. The interaction of this regime with the section 176 power of sale and with the IBC moratorium has been the subject of important recent authority.

Landmark — invocation of a demat pledge and the pledgee's status

In PTC India Financial Services Ltd v Venkateswarlu Kari (2022) the Supreme Court examined the pledge of dematerialised shares under the Depositories Act read with the Contract Act. The Court held that on invocation of the pledge the pledgee becomes the holder of the securities for the limited purpose of sale, but does not become the full beneficial owner; the pawnor's right of redemption under section 177 continues until an actual sale to a third party, consistent with the scheme of section 176. The decision aligns the demat pledge with the classical pledge: invocation is not the same as a sale, and the pawnor's equity of redemption survives until the collateral is genuinely realised.

Pledge enforcement and the IBC moratorium

When the pawnor is a corporate debtor in CIRP, the section 14 moratorium bars enforcement of security interests against the corporate debtor's assets, and a pledge of the corporate debtor's own securities cannot be enforced during the moratorium. But a pledge of securities given by a third party, such as a promoter pledging its shares to secure the company's debt, stands differently, because those pledged assets may not be assets of the corporate debtor and the third-party pledgor is not protected by the corporate debtor's moratorium, subject to the facts and to V Ramakrishnan reasoning on third-party security. The practitioner must identify, for every pledge, whose asset is charged and whether that person is itself under moratorium, before advising on enforceability.

Memory aid — why pledge beats lien

Three letters separate a pledge from a bare lien: a pledge carries a power of sale under section 176, a lien does not. Both depend on possession, both secure a debt, but only the pledge lets the holder turn the collateral into money on default, after reasonable notice. When taking possessory security, take it as a pledge, document the notice mechanism, and remember that the pawnor can redeem right up to the moment of an actual sale.

Chapter 13 · Sections 182 to 238

Agency acting through another

Agency is the law by which one person, the agent, brings the principal into legal relations with third parties. It is the largest chapter in the Act and the one most often taken for granted, because every company acts only through agents: its directors, its officers, its authorised signatories. For an insolvency practitioner the chapter is doubly important, because the resolution professional and the liquidator exercise statutory powers that displace the company's ordinary agents, and because the authority of those who signed the company's contracts is constantly in issue when those contracts are challenged.

Sections 182 and 183 to 184, the parties An agent is a person employed to do any act for another, or to represent another in dealings with third persons; the person for whom such act is done, or who is so represented, is the principal. Any person who is of the age of majority and of sound mind may employ an agent; and as between principal and third persons, any person may become an agent.

Creation of agency and the kinds of authority

Agency may be created by express appointment, by implication from conduct and circumstances, by ratification of an unauthorised act, or by operation of law including agency of necessity. The agent's power to bind the principal flows from its authority, which the Act and the case law divide into distinct kinds. Distinguishing them is the key to every dispute about whether the principal is bound.

Kind of authoritySourceEffect

Ostensible authority and holding out

Ostensible or apparent authority is the most commercially significant doctrine in the chapter, because it protects third parties who deal in good faith with someone the principal has held out as authorised. The principal is bound not because it actually authorised the act, but because it represented, by its own words or conduct, that the agent had authority, and the third party relied on that representation.

Landmark — the conditions of ostensible authority

In Freeman and Lockyer v Buckhurst Park Properties (Mangal) Ltd the English Court of Appeal set out the four conditions for binding a company by the ostensible authority of a person acting as its agent: a representation that the agent had authority, made by someone with actual authority to manage the business; reliance on that representation by the third party; that the third party was induced to enter the contract; and that the company had the capacity to enter it. In Watteau v Fenwick the principal was held bound by acts within the usual authority of the agent's position even though the principal had secretly restricted that authority and the third party did not know of the principal at all. Indian courts apply these principles through sections 187, 188 and 237.

Section 188, extent of an agent's authority An agent having authority to do an act has authority to do every lawful thing which is necessary in order to do such act, and an agent having authority to carry on a business has authority to do every lawful thing necessary for the purpose, or usually done in the course, of conducting such business.

Ratification

Where a person does an act on behalf of another without authority, or exceeds its authority, the principal may ratify the act, and ratification relates back to the time of the act, as if it had been authorised from the outset (sections 196 to 200). Ratification requires that the principal had capacity and was in existence at the time of the act, that the act was done on the principal's behalf, and that the principal ratifies with knowledge of the material facts, ratifying the whole act and not merely the favourable part.

Landmark — ratification must be by a disclosed principal in existence

In Keighley, Maxsted and Co v Durant the House of Lords held that an undisclosed principal cannot ratify a contract made by an agent who did not profess to act on the principal's behalf; ratification is available only where the agent purported to act for the principal. The corollary, important in corporate practice, is that a company cannot ratify a contract made on its behalf before it was incorporated, because it was not in existence and not capable of being the principal at the time; such pre-incorporation contracts require adoption or novation, not ratification.

Delegation, sub-agents and substituted agents

The maxim is delegatus non potest delegare: an agent cannot ordinarily delegate its authority. Sections 190 to 195 work out the exceptions. A sub-agent (section 191) is appointed by and acts under the control of the original agent; where properly appointed, the principal is bound by and responsible for the sub-agent's acts as against third parties, but the principal and sub-agent are not in direct privity. A substituted agent (sections 194 to 195) is named by the agent, at the principal's request or with authority, to act directly for the principal, and stands in direct relation with the principal. The distinction governs liability and the chain of responsibility.

Personal liability of the agent, and the undisclosed principal

  • General rule, section 230. In the absence of a contrary contract, an agent cannot personally enforce, nor is it personally bound by, contracts it makes on behalf of its principal. The contract is the principal's.
  • Presumptions of personal liability, section 230. Personal liability is presumed where the agent contracts for a principal resident abroad, where it does not disclose the name of its principal, or where the principal, though disclosed, cannot be sued.
  • Undisclosed principal, sections 231 to 234. Where an agent contracts without disclosing that it acts as agent, the principal may, on discovery, require performance, subject to the rights the third party would have had against the agent; and the third party, on discovering the principal, may sue either the agent or the principal. The doctrine protects the third party's bargain while letting the real principal take the benefit.
Section 237, liability of principal for the agent's apparent authority When an agent has, without authority, done acts or incurred obligations to third persons on behalf of the principal, the principal is bound by such acts if he has by his words or conduct induced such third persons to believe that such acts were within the agent's authority.

Termination of agency

Agency terminates by the principal revoking the authority, by the agent renouncing the business, by completion of the business, by the death or unsoundness of mind of either party, and by the principal being adjudicated insolvent (section 201). Two qualifications are critical in commercial practice. First, an authority cannot be revoked after the agent has partly exercised it so as to bind the principal as to acts already done. Second, and most important for finance lawyers, is agency coupled with interest.

Section 202 — agency coupled with an interest is irrevocable

Where the agent has itself an interest in the property which forms the subject matter of the agency, the agency cannot, in the absence of an express contract, be terminated to the prejudice of that interest. This is the statutory foundation for the irrevocable powers of attorney that pervade secured lending: a power of attorney granted to a lender to deal with charged assets, where the lender has an interest in those assets as security, is not revoked by the borrower's later attempt to cancel it, nor by the borrower's death or insolvency, so far as the security interest requires. The practitioner structuring enforcement rights relies on section 202 to make the lender's powers survive the borrower's distress.

Companies, directors and the indoor management rule

A company is a person that can act only through agents. Its directors, collectively and within the board's powers, and its officers and authorised signatories, are its agents, and the company is bound by their acts within their actual or ostensible authority. Partners are agents of the firm for the purpose of its business. The agency analysis is overlaid, for companies, by the rule in Royal British Bank v Turquand, the indoor management rule, under which a third party dealing in good faith with a company is entitled to assume that the company's internal procedures and authorisations have been complied with, and is not affected by irregularities in the company's internal management that it could not discover. The rule protects outsiders, but it does not protect a person who knew of the irregularity or whom the circumstances should have put on inquiry.

Agency and insolvency: the displacement of the company's agents

The onset of insolvency transforms the company's agency relationships. On the commencement of CIRP the powers of the board of directors are suspended and vest in the resolution professional, who manages the affairs of the corporate debtor; the directors' authority as agents of the company is displaced by the statutory office. In liquidation the liquidator takes custody and control of the assets and acts for the estate. Contracts purportedly entered into by the former management after the moratorium, without the RP's authority, do not bind the corporate debtor. Equally, the resolution professional and liquidator must respect agency coupled with interest under section 202, so a lender's irrevocable enforcement power of attorney is not automatically swept away, though its exercise is subject to the moratorium. The practitioner must therefore ask, for every contract and every power in a distressed company, who had authority to bind the company at the relevant time, and whether that authority survived the insolvency trigger.

Memory aid — is the principal bound?

Run four questions. One, did the agent have actual authority, express or implied. Two, if not, did the principal hold out the agent as authorised, giving ostensible authority that the third party relied on. Three, if neither, has the principal ratified with full knowledge. Four, for a company, does the indoor management rule let the outsider assume internal compliance. If any answer is yes, the principal is bound; if all are no, the agent alone is liable.

Part XII

The Contract Act and the IBC where private bargains meet the Code

The Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code does not repeal the law of contract; it suspends, overrides and reshapes it for the duration of an insolvency. A restructuring lawyer must hold both bodies of law in view at once, because the rights the Contract Act creates are precisely the rights the Code redistributes. This part draws together the contract doctrines that recur in insolvency and states how the Code modifies each.

The overriding effect of section 238

Section 238 of the IBC provides that the Code overrides anything inconsistent contained in any other law or in any instrument having effect by virtue of any such law. The practical consequence is that contractual rights yield to the Code where the two conflict. A contractual right to terminate, to accelerate, to enforce security or to set off does not disappear, but its exercise is subject to the moratorium and to the collective process. The starting question in any insolvency dispute about a contract is therefore not only what the contract says, but whether the Code has displaced or suspended what it says.

The resolution plan as statutory novation

The single most important point of contact is the effect of an approved resolution plan. On approval under section 31 of the Code the plan binds the corporate debtor, its creditors, members, employees, guarantors and other stakeholders. Claims that are not part of the plan are extinguished; the successful resolution applicant takes over the corporate debtor on a clean slate, free of past claims that were capable of being submitted in the process. This clean-slate principle, settled in the Essar Steel line and reinforced since, operates as a statutory rewriting of the corporate debtor's contractual liabilities, achieved without the consent that section 62 of the Contract Act would otherwise require for a novation. The contract lawyer's instinct that obligations cannot be altered without agreement is, here, overridden by the Code.

What survives, and what does not

Three distinctions decide most disputes. First, claims against the corporate debtor are extinguished by the plan, but claims against guarantors generally survive, because the guarantor is a separate obligor whose liability is not the corporate debtor's. Second, the security interest over the corporate debtor's assets is dealt with by the plan or the liquidation waterfall, but third-party security, given by a promoter or group company, may stand outside the corporate debtor's process. Third, contracts essential to keeping the corporate debtor a going concern are protected from termination during the moratorium, but the counterparty's accrued money claims are admitted as claims in the process rather than enforced by suit. The practitioner maps every obligation in the structure against these three lines before advising on outcome.

Memory aid — the two-layer question

In any insolvency problem with a contract at its centre, ask two questions in order. Layer one, the Contract Act: what rights and obligations does the contract create, and are they valid and enforceable on ordinary principles. Layer two, the Code: does the IBC, through the moratorium, the clean-slate plan, the waterfall or section 238, suspend, extinguish or reorder those rights. A right that is good at layer one can still be defeated at layer two. Answer both, in that sequence, and the analysis holds.

Memory aids collected

  • Pricing any breach claim: Cause, Remoteness, Mitigation, Cap. Clear all four gates and the number is defensible.
  • Indemnity versus guarantee: indemnity is I will bear your loss (primary, standalone); guarantee is I will pay if he does not (secondary, co-extensive).
  • The surety's seven escape routes: variance (133), release of principal (134), composition or time (135), loss of security (139, 141), misrepresentation or concealment (142 to 143), and want of consideration or principal debt. Creditor's answers: consent, reservation of rights, the s.128 saving.
  • Why pledge beats lien: the pledge carries a power of sale under s.176; the lien is only a right to retain.
  • Is the principal bound: actual authority, then ostensible (holding out), then ratification, then indoor management. Any yes binds the principal.
  • The two-layer insolvency question: layer one, what does the Contract Act create; layer two, does the IBC suspend, extinguish or reorder it. A right good at layer one can fail at layer two.
