The shape of the reform, in one page

The Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016 turned ten in 2026. A decade of cases exposed gaps the drafters did not foresee and produced judicial answers that sometimes pulled in opposite directions. The 2026 Amendment is the legislature's reply: it settles several long running controversies, hard wires timelines, shifts the balance of power inside liquidation towards creditors, and opens three new doors, a creditor driven resolution route, a framework for groups of companies, and an enabling power for cross-border cases.

It is useful to hold four organising ideas in mind before reading any single clause. They recur throughout the Act and explain why otherwise scattered amendments hang together.

1 · CertaintyMandatory admission, fixed outer limits on tribunal orders, and statutory codification of judge-made rules.

3 · Value captureAvoidance look-backs lengthened, guarantor assets pulled into the estate, security realisation disciplined, government dues put back in their place.

The first practitioner question

What is actually in force, and what is not

Section 1(2) of the Amendment Act allows the Central Government to bring different provisions into force on different dates. This matters enormously in day to day practice, because some of the headline reforms are not yet operative. By the commencement notification, the large majority of the seventy-two clauses took effect on 26 May 2026. A discrete set of the most structural reforms was deliberately held back, pending the regulations and notifications needed to make them work.

Do not advise a client to plan a transaction around the creditor-initiated route, group coordination, or cross-border recognition yet. Cluster A

Definitions and the institutional frame

Definitions look dull until you realise that, in insolvency, a single defined term can decide who gets paid and who does not. The 2026 Act rewrites a handful of definitions that had been quietly doing heavy lifting in litigation, and it folds the various insolvency professionals into one umbrella expression so that the Board can regulate them coherently.

Amendment s. 2IBC s. 3 · definitions

A1Registered valuer, the meaning of "security interest", and "service provider"

Three changes are made to the master definition section. Two are clarificatory, one is structural, and the middle one is among the most consequential single sentences in the entire Act.

New clause (27A): "registered valuer"

Before

The Code used valuation throughout, for the liquidation value, the fair value, and the test that protects dissenting and operational creditors, but it never tied "registered valuer" to a recognised standard. The qualification of valuers lived only in the regulations.

After

"Registered valuer" now carries the same meaning as under Chapter XVII of the Companies Act, 2013. The competence and registration of the person who fixes the numbers is anchored in primary law.

Why

Valuation disputes are the soft underbelly of every contested resolution. Wide swings between two registered valuers feed objections under section 30 and appeals under section 61. Linking the term to the Companies Act standard raises the floor on who may value and gives the Board a firmer footing to discipline poor work.

New Explanation to clause (31): what "security interest" is, and is not

Before

"Security interest" was defined broadly. Critically, it could be read to include a charge "created by operation of law". On that reading a statutory first charge for unpaid tax made the State a secured creditor. The Supreme Court adopted exactly this reading.

After

The new Explanation says a security interest exists only where it arises from a right or claim created by the act of two or more parties, by agreement or arrangement. It expressly does not include a security interest created merely by operation of law.

Why

This sentence is Parliament's answer to the most disruptive line of insolvency authority of the last decade. By excluding charges that arise by statute alone, the Act removes the doctrinal foundation on which government tax departments had climbed up the priority order. It must be read together with the matching Explanation inserted into the section 53 waterfall (see Cluster H), which channels government dues back to their designated rung.

State Tax Officer v. Rainbow Papers Ltd.2022 SCC OnLine SC 1162; review dismissed in Sanjay Kumar Agarwal v. State Tax Officer, 2023

The Court held that a first charge under section 48 of the Gujarat Value Added Tax Act made the State a "secured creditor" under the Code, because the definition of security interest reached charges created by operation of law and did not exclude the government. A resolution plan that ignored statutory dues was held liable to be set aside.

Relevance: The new Explanation legislatively dislodges the premise of Rainbow Papers. A charge that exists only because a statute says so is no longer a "security interest" for the Code.

Paschimanchal Vidyut Vitran Nigam Ltd. v. Raman Ispat Pvt. Ltd.2023 SCC OnLine SC 842

A coordinate Bench distinguished Rainbow Papers and held that government dues are dealt with separately, and at a lower rung, by the waterfall, so the State could not enforce outside liquidation as though it were an ordinary secured creditor.

Relevance: The Amendment adopts the Raman Ispat position and ends the coordinate-Bench tug of war by statute, which is the cleaner route to certainty than waiting for a larger Bench.

New clause (31A): "service provider"

Before

The Code regulated insolvency professionals, insolvency professional agencies and information utilities as three separate species, each named at length wherever the Board's powers appeared.

After

"Service provider" is a single umbrella term covering the insolvency professional, the agency, the information utility, the registered valuer, and any other category the Central Government notifies, all registered with the Board.

Why

This is plumbing for the regulator. It lets a single set of provisions on complaints, inspection, show cause and discipline (sections 217 to 220, rewritten in Cluster O) apply across the whole professional ecosystem, and it future-proofs the regime so that newly notified categories of provider fall under the Board without further primary amendment.

Amendment s. 3IBC s. 5 · definitions (Part II)

A2Avoidance transaction, fraudulent trading, the initiation date, and asset sales as resolution

Five edits sharpen the corporate definitions. They are individually modest but together they tidy up concepts that the rest of the Act now leans on.

  • New clause (2A), "avoidance transaction". A single label is given to the family of vulnerable transactions in sections 43, 45, 49 and 50 (preferential, undervalued, transactions defrauding creditors, and extortionate credit). The existing clause is renumbered (2B). This lets the Act refer to the whole family in one breath, which it does repeatedly in the liquidation and creditor-application provisions.
  • New clause (9A), "fraudulent or wrongful trading". A defined pointer to section 66, again so that later provisions can treat avoidance and fraudulent trading as a pair.
  • Proviso to clause (11), "initiation date". Where several applications against the same debtor are pending on the commencement date, the initiation date is the date of the first application. This matters because the Act now ties the avoidance look-back to the initiation date rather than the later commencement date (Cluster I), so fixing which application counts is essential.
  • Explanation to clause (26), "resolution plan". The plan may now expressly contemplate the sale of one or more assets of the debtor through one or more plans by one or more applicants, on specified conditions. This blesses asset-wise and multi-applicant resolutions, which the market had been doing under regulation but which sat uneasily with the older definition.
  • Clause (28), "voting share". Refined to the debt owed to the members of the committee who are eligible to vote, removing ambiguity about whose debt forms the denominator.
Practitioner note

The asset-sale Explanation is quietly important for distressed mergers and acquisitions. A bidder who wants only a clean division or a single plant, rather than the whole company, now has clearer statutory cover. Expect resolution professionals to structure invitations that permit composite as well as asset-specific bids, and expect the section 30 compliance check to be applied plan by plan where the estate is carved up.

Cluster B

Admission, the gate, and the new timelines

The single most litigated question in the Code's first decade was deceptively simple: once a creditor proves a debt and a default, must the tribunal admit, or may it decline? The 2026 Act answers it, and in doing so reverses a Supreme Court decision by statute.

Amendment s. 4IBC s. 7 · financial creditor's application

B1Mandatory admission of a financial creditor's application

In plain terms. A bank shows the tribunal two things: money was owed, and it was not paid. The old text said the tribunal "may" admit. A reasonable reader, and eventually the Supreme Court, asked whether "may" gave the tribunal a discretion to refuse even when the bank had proved its case. The new text removes the doubt: if the default is made out and the file is complete, the tribunal "shall" admit, and nothing else may be weighed.

Before

Section 7(4) required the tribunal to ascertain default within fourteen days. Section 7(5)(a) said it "may admit" once satisfied of default, completeness and no disciplinary cloud over the proposed professional.

The word "may" was read literally to confer discretion, so a tribunal could keep an admission in abeyance even where debt and default were proved.

After

The tribunal "shall" admit within fourteen days where default has occurred and the application is complete, with no pending disciplinary proceeding against the proposed professional.

Explanation I states that once those requirements are met, no other ground may be considered to reject. Explanation II makes an information utility record of default sufficient proof. Delay beyond fourteen days must be reasoned in writing.

Why

The Code was built on a clean trigger: default, not commercial insolvency. The Bankruptcy Law Reforms Committee deliberately moved away from the older "inability to pay" enquiry, which invited the debtor to litigate its own solvency and bled the timeline dry. A discretion to weigh the debtor's overall financial health reopened exactly that door. Parliament has now shut it.

Innoventive Industries Ltd. v. ICICI Bank(2018) 1 SCC 407

The Court held that the moment the tribunal is satisfied a default has occurred, the application must be admitted unless incomplete. The enquiry is binary: debt and default.

Relevance: The new section 7(5) restores and codifies the Innoventive twin test as the whole of the admission enquiry.

Vidarbha Industries Power Ltd. v. Axis Bank Ltd.(2022) 8 SCC 352; review dismissed

A two-judge Bench read "may" in section 7(5)(a) as conferring genuine discretion, so the tribunal could decline or defer admission after weighing surrounding circumstances, there, a large award receivable by the debtor that would have cleared the debt.

Relevance: This is the decision the Amendment overrides. By substituting "shall" and adding Explanation I, the legislature removes the discretionary reading that Vidarbha had introduced.

M. Suresh Kumar Reddy v. Canara Bank(2023)

A later Bench confined Vidarbha to its peculiar facts and reaffirmed that once default is established there is hardly any discretion to refuse admission.

Relevance: The Amendment converts this judicial course-correction into the settled statutory rule, ending bench-to-bench variation.

Practitioner note

For a lender, admission risk on a clean, information-utility-recorded default is now close to nil, which strengthens the bargaining position long before the file reaches the tribunal. For a debtor, the defensive playbook narrows sharply: the time to settle, dispute the existence of default, or cure is before filing, not at the admission hearing. Solvency, a pending receivable, or hardship are no longer arguments against admission. Note also that the operational creditor route under section 9 always used "shall", so the Amendment harmonises the two gates.

Still unsettled

The mandatory rule does not abolish the threshold of a genuine pre-existing dispute on operational debt, nor does it license abuse: the new penalty for frivolous or vexatious proceedings (Cluster N) is the counterweight, and the tribunal retains the duty to satisfy itself that a real default, and not a manufactured one, is before it.

Amendment s. 5IBC s. 9Amendment s. 6IBC s. 10

B2Operational and debtor applications: speed, and the debtor loses the power to choose its own professional

Before

On a section 9 operational creditor application, certain information was "as prescribed" by rules. There was no express duty to record reasons for delay.

On a section 10 application by the debtor itself, the debtor proposed the resolution professional and the tribunal screened for disciplinary proceedings against that nominee.

After

Section 9 information moves to the "as specified" regulation-making power, and a delay beyond fourteen days must be reasoned in writing, mirroring section 7.

Section 10 drops the debtor's nomination of the professional. Under the matching change to section 16 (next card), the tribunal refers the choice of interim professional to the Board.

Why

A debtor who files its own insolvency and hand-picks the professional invites a tame, debtor-aligned process. Removing self-nomination and routing the appointment through the Board protects the confidence of the creditors who will sit on the committee, and addresses a recognised avenue for misuse of the voluntary route.

Amendment s. 10IBC s. 16 · appointment of interim professional

B3Who becomes the interim resolution professional

Before

The professional proposed in the application was generally appointed as interim professional, including for debtor-filed cases.

After

For a financial creditor's application the proposed professional is appointed, subject only to no pending discipline. For a debtor's application under section 10 the tribunal refers the matter to the Board for a recommendation. Consequential wording threads the new sub-section (3A) into sub-section (4).

Practitioner note

A financial creditor keeps effective control of the first professional, which preserves creditor confidence at the outset. A debtor loses it. Advisers running a section 10 filing should set client expectations early: the professional will be a Board nominee, not a familiar face.

Amendment s. 43IBC s. 61Amendment s. 44new s. 64AAmendment s. 45IBC s. 65

B4Tightening the clock and deterring tactical filings

A cluster of changes hard-wires speed and discipline across the gate and the appeal.

  • Section 61, appeals. The appellate tribunal must dispose of an appeal within three months of receipt. Combined with the fixed thirty-day windows the Act places on tribunal orders at admission, plan approval, liquidation and dissolution, the whole timeline now has outer limits at every node.
  • New section 64A, frivolous Part II proceedings. A person who launches a frivolous or vexatious proceeding before the tribunal may be penalised between one lakh and two crore rupees. A mirror provision, section 183A, does the same for Part III (Cluster N).
  • Section 65. The existing bar on fraudulent or malicious initiation is extended to the new creditor-initiated process.
Why

Mandatory admission removes the tribunal's discretion to filter out weak cases at the door. The frivolous-proceedings penalty is the deliberate counterweight: the gate is open, but those who abuse it pay. The three-month appeal limit attacks the other end of the problem, where resolution timelines were defeated not at admission but in years of appellate delay.

Still unsettled

Disposal "within three months" is, on the Code's own history, more aspirational than self-executing, the original fourteen-day admission norm was routinely overshot. Whether the appellate tribunal is resourced to meet a hard quarterly limit, and what follows if it does not, will be tested in practice. The reasons-for-delay duty at least creates a record.

Cluster C

Withdrawal and settlement

Insolvency is collective. Once a company is admitted, the proceeding stops being a private fight between one creditor and the debtor and becomes a process for the benefit of all creditors. The hard question is when, and on whose say-so, the parties may still call the whole thing off by settling.

Amendment s. 8IBC s. 12A · substituted

C1The new withdrawal window

In plain terms. Two parties can shake hands and end the case, but only inside a fixed window, and only if a heavy majority of the creditors agree. The new section draws the window with bright lines: not before the committee of creditors is formed, and not after bidding has opened.

Before

The old section 12A allowed withdrawal of an admitted application by the applicant, with ninety per cent committee approval, "in such manner as may be specified". The detail lived in regulation 30A, which had to be patched repeatedly. Courts filled the gaps using Article 142 and inherent powers.

After

Withdrawal is now made by the resolution professional, with ninety per cent committee approval, and is barred (a) before the committee is constituted, and (b) after the first invitation for resolution plans has issued. The tribunal must pass its order within thirty days, recording reasons for any delay.

Why

The Code discourages a single creditor unwinding a collective process at will. The earlier framework left two gaps the courts had to plug case by case: withdrawal before the committee exists, and withdrawal after bidding opens. The Amendment writes the answers into primary law, channels the application through the professional rather than the applicant, and gives the tribunal a clock.

Swiss Ribbons (P) Ltd. v. Union of India(2019) 4 SCC 17

Upheld section 12A and the ninety per cent threshold, and accepted that before the committee is constituted the tribunal may permit withdrawal after hearing the parties.

Relevance: The new pre-committee bar refines, rather than contradicts, this position by routing early withdrawal through the professional within the specified procedure.

GLAS Trust Company LLC v. BYJU Raveendran2024 INSC 811; (2025) 3 SCC 625

The Court mapped the stages at which withdrawal may be sought, held that the application must travel through the professional, and clarified that once the committee is in existence the section 12A route with its ninety per cent threshold governs, displacing recourse to inherent powers or Article 142.

Relevance: The substituted section 12A codifies the GLAS Trust architecture: professional-led applications, a defined window, and committee primacy over settlement.

Practitioner note

Settlement strategy now has a deadline you can mark on a calendar. If a one-time settlement is the client's exit, it must be landed and committee-approved before the first invitation for resolution plans goes out. Miss that, and the only escape from the process is a resolution plan, not a private deal. For a settling creditor, the ninety per cent bar means the deal must satisfy the committee as a whole, not just the applicant who filed.

Cluster D

The moratorium, and a new power over guarantor assets

The moratorium is the protective shield that freezes claims against the debtor while a resolution is attempted. Two changes here are easy to miss but commercially significant: one clarifies that a guarantor cannot use a back-door claim to puncture the shield, and the other lets the estate reach into assets that secured the company's debt but sit in a guarantor's hands.

Amendment s. 9IBC s. 14 · moratorium

D1The surety cannot ricochet a claim back onto the debtor

Before

Section 14(3)(b) carved a surety under a contract of guarantee out of the moratorium, so that creditors could pursue guarantors even while the company was protected. What was less clear was the reverse: could a surety who had paid then turn around and pursue the debtor during the moratorium?

After

A new Explanation confirms that the moratorium also applies where the surety seeks to initiate or continue any action against the corporate debtor pursuant to a contract of guarantee. The numbering also adds the new sub-section (2A) (which protects guarantor-asset transfers) to the list of carve-outs in sub-section (1).

Why

A guarantor who pays the creditor steps into the creditor's shoes by subrogation under the Indian Contract Act, 1872, and could in theory chase the debtor for indemnity. Allowing that during a resolution would let a fresh claim ricochet onto the very estate the process is trying to preserve. The Explanation stops the back-door claim and protects the value being assembled for all creditors. It sits alongside the clean-slate codification in section 31 (Cluster F), which extinguishes a paying guarantor's indemnity right once a plan is approved.

Amendment s. 17new s. 28A

D2Transferring a guarantor's asset as part of the resolution

In plain terms. Suppose a bank held security not over the company's own factory but over a building owned by the promoter who guaranteed the loan, and the bank has already taken possession of that building. The new section lets that building be brought into the deal and sold as part of the company's resolution, with the committee's blessing, instead of being marooned in a separate, slower recovery action.

Before

There was no mechanism to fold a guarantor's asset, already in a creditor's possession, into the corporate resolution. The asset sat outside the process, realised separately and slowly, fragmenting the value available to a resolution applicant who wanted the whole picture.

After

Where a creditor has taken possession of a personal or corporate guarantor's asset by enforcing security under any law, the creditor may, during the corporate process, permit that asset to be transferred as part of the resolution, with prior committee approval. Tiered approvals apply where the guarantor is itself in process: sixty-six per cent of the corporate guarantor's committee, or three-fourths in value of a personal guarantor's creditors. Sale proceeds are adjusted against the guarantor's debt after costs of preservation, and any surplus returns to the guarantor.

Why

Promoter and group guarantees are the norm in Indian lending. Keeping the guarantor's collateral outside the resolution fragmented the estate and depressed the price a bidder would pay. Section 28A consolidates value: a resolution applicant can now acquire a coherent asset base, and the transfer vests clean title in the buyer as if made by the asset's owner.

Practitioner note

Two conditions gate this power and must be checked first: the creditor must already have taken possession, and must have enforced its security under a law that empowers a transfer, typically the SARFAESI Act. The regulations are expected to specify which assets qualify, who may buy, and how a composite transfer is valued. Until then, treat section 28A as available in principle but dependent on careful structuring of the underlying enforcement.

Cluster E

Conduct of the resolution process

Between admission and the plan vote, the professional runs the company, collects and verifies claims, and the committee deliberates. The Amendment tightens duties here, broadens who must cooperate, and, in its single most debated move, extends the committee's reach into the liquidation that follows a failed resolution.

Amendment s. 11IBC s. 18Amendment s. 12IBC s. 19

E1Claims verification, and a wider duty to cooperate

Before

Section 18 required the interim professional to collate claims. Whether collation included verification, and the determination of claim value, was a recurring fight.

Section 19 spoke of "personnel" of the debtor and its promoter cooperating with the professional, language that some used to escape the duty by saying they were neither.

After

Section 18 now states expressly that the professional, while collating claims, shall verify them and, if required, determine the value of verified claims, in the specified manner.

Section 19 changes "Personnel" to "Persons" and extends the cooperation duty to anyone associated with the debtor's management or engaged under a contract for service. An Explanation confirms references to the interim professional include the professional.

Why

Two practical headaches are removed. First, the verification-versus-collation argument that delayed the constitution of the committee and the admission of claims. Second, the cooperation gap, where consultants, former officers and contractors who held the debtor's records sheltered behind the narrow word "personnel". The wider "persons" formula closes that escape.

Amendment s. 13IBC s. 21 · new sub-section (11)

E2The committee of creditors now supervises liquidation

In plain terms. Until now, the committee of creditors was a creature of the resolution stage. Once a company tipped into liquidation, the committee dissolved and the liquidator ran the show under the tribunal's eye. The Amendment keeps the committee alive into liquidation and gives it a supervisory role over the liquidator. This is the structural pivot of the whole Act.

Before

The committee's statutory life effectively ended at the resolution stage. In liquidation the liquidator exercised the powers, subject to the tribunal's directions, and creditors participated mainly through a consultation committee under the regulations, with no statutory supervisory authority.

After

New sub-section (11) provides that where liquidation is initiated, the committee constituted under section 21 continues, and supervises the conduct of the liquidation by the liquidator, with sections 21 and 24 applying as the context requires. The Board may admit other classes of creditor to attend without a vote. The provision applies to liquidations begun after commencement and to ongoing liquidations where the liquidator has not yet applied for dissolution.

Why

The drafters concluded that creditors, who bear the loss, should steer realisation in liquidation too, rather than handing the asset sale entirely to a liquidator supervised only by an over-burdened tribunal. Read with the liquidation overhaul in Cluster G, where the committee chooses and can replace the liquidator, sub-section (11) extends the Code's "creditor in control" philosophy across the cliff edge from resolution into liquidation.

Still unsettled, and a real debate

This is the Act's most contested reform. The case for it is creditor accountability. The case against runs from the Code's own Preamble, which commits the law to balancing the interests of all stakeholders, not only the financial creditors who dominate the committee. In liquidation the residual claimants change: operational creditors, workmen, statutory dues and even shareholders have more at stake than in resolution, yet the supervising committee is weighted towards financial creditors. A genuine question is whether committee supervision of liquidation, untempered, risks the very stakeholder balance the Preamble protects. The Board's power to admit non-voting classes is a partial answer; the regulations specifying the manner of supervision will decide how serious the tension proves.

Amendment s. 14IBC s. 22Amendment s. 15IBC s. 25Amendment s. 16IBC s. 26

E3Appointment of the professional, avoidance duties, and severing avoidance from the main process

  • Section 22, deemed appointment. When the committee resolves to appoint or confirm the resolution professional, that person is deemed appointed from the date of the resolution, and the decision is communicated to the interim professional, the debtor and the Board. This removes the dead time between the committee's vote and a formal order.
  • Section 25(2)(j), avoidance duty. The professional's duty is restated to require filing applications in respect of an avoidance transaction or fraudulent or wrongful trading, using the new defined terms.
  • Section 26, substituted. An avoidance or fraudulent-trading application, or one under the new section 47, does not affect the conduct of the resolution or liquidation, and, conversely, completion of the resolution or liquidation does not stop the avoidance proceedings continuing.
Why

The substituted section 26 answers a problem that had reached the High Courts: do avoidance applications survive once a resolution plan is approved and the professional functus officio? Treating recovery of clawed-back value as parasitic on the main process risked extinguishing valuable claims the moment a plan went through. The Amendment severs the two tracks, so a preference or undervalue claim lives on regardless of the headline outcome, preserving value that ultimately benefits creditors.

Tata Steel BSL Ltd. v. Venus Recruiter Pvt. Ltd.Delhi High Court

The survival of avoidance applications after approval of a resolution plan, and who may pursue them, was litigated at length, with real doubt over whether such applications abated on the professional's exit.

Relevance: The substituted section 26, read with the new section 47 and the section 36 and 54 changes (Clusters G and I), settles by statute that avoidance and fraudulent-trading proceedings continue independently and that the committee may direct how the recoveries are pursued and distributed.

Cluster F

The resolution plan

This is where money is allocated. Two reforms dominate: a recalibrated floor for the creditor who votes no, and a statutory clean slate for the buyer who takes over the company. Both respond directly to lines of Supreme Court authority.

Amendment s. 18IBC s. 30 · contents of the plan

F1The dissenting financial creditor's minimum, and a mandatory monitoring committee

In plain terms. When most creditors vote yes to a rescue deal but a minority votes no, the law guarantees the dissenter a floor so it is not crammed down to nothing. The Amendment redraws that floor and pins it to the actual plan, and it requires every approved plan to be watched by a dedicated committee during implementation.

New clause (ba): the floor for those who vote no

Before

Section 30(2)(b) bundled two guarantees together. Operational creditors got the higher of their liquidation value or what priority distribution of the plan amount would give them. A dissenting financial creditor got not less than the amount payable in a liquidation under section 53.

How to compute that liquidation figure for a secured dissenter, by share of admitted claim or by the value of its specific security, split the Supreme Court.

After

Operational creditors stay in clause (b). A new clause (ba) governs dissenting financial creditors. Their floor is now the lower of two amounts: (i) what they would get in a liquidation under section 53; or (ii) what they would get if the plan amount were distributed in accordance with the section 53 order of priority. Explanation I keeps the "fair and equitable" standard. Explanation II makes the new rule prospective, sparing plans already approved, or where liquidation was already ordered or resolved, before commencement.

Why

The old single floor of "liquidation value" produced a perverse incentive and years of litigation. A secured creditor could vote no and demand the full liquidation value of its collateral out of a plan whose total value was below liquidation value, in effect making the assenting majority subsidise the dissenter and pushing the deal towards collapse. Tying the floor to the lower of liquidation value and priority-distribution of the actual plan amount disciplines strategic dissent while preserving a genuine minimum.

Committee of Creditors of Essar Steel India Ltd. v. Satish Kumar Gupta(2020) 8 SCC 531

A three-judge Bench held that equitable, not necessarily equal, treatment is required; the committee's commercial wisdom governs distribution, subject to the statutory minimum and to fairness between similarly situated creditors.

Relevance: The retained "fair and equitable" Explanation carries Essar Steel's principle forward; clause (ba) supplies the recalibrated minimum that operates within it.

India Resurgence ARC (P) Ltd. v. Amit Metaliks Ltd.2021 SCC OnLine SC 409

A two-judge Bench held that a dissenting secured creditor cannot demand the full value of its security and is confined to the liquidation value, deferring to the committee's distribution; a higher entitlement would incentivise liquidation over resolution.

Relevance: The "pooled" view of the dissenter's floor.

DBS Bank Ltd. Singapore v. Ruchi Soya Industries Ltd.2024 SCC OnLine SC 3

Another two-judge Bench took the opposite view: because a dissenting secured creditor is constructively forced to surrender its security on plan approval, it must receive the value of that security as it would in a liquidation, reading section 30(2)(b)(ii) with sections 52 and 53. Finding the conflict with Amit Metaliks, it referred the question to a larger Bench.

Relevance: The "collateral-specific" view. The Amendment recalibrates the quantum of the floor but leaves the computation question, claim-share or security-value, to be read with the new section 53(1)(b)(ii) Explanation (Cluster H), which itself points towards valuing a secured creditor to the extent of its security.

Still unsettled

The Amendment does not, in terms, declare whether the section 53 figure for a dissenting secured creditor is computed by share of admitted claim or by the value of its specific collateral. The DBS Bank reference to a larger Bench is not mooted simply because the floor has been rewritten. The better view, taking clause (ba) together with the new section 53(1)(b)(ii) Explanation, is that the regime now leans collateral-specific, but a practitioner should treat the precise computation as live until the larger Bench speaks or the valuation manner is specified.

Substituted clause (d): a real monitoring committee

Before

The plan had to provide for "implementation and supervision", a thin requirement that left post-approval monitoring patchy and a frequent source of grievance when successful applicants under-performed.

After

The plan must constitute a committee for implementation and supervision, comprising the resolution professional or another insolvency professional, representatives of classes of creditors, and the resolution applicant, on specified terms. Sub-section (4) also requires the committee of creditors to record reasons for approving a plan.

Why

Implementation failure had become the quiet scandal of the resolution stage. A formal monitoring committee, with creditor and applicant representation, gives stakeholders eyes on delivery, in line with the Board's discussion paper on monitoring committees. Recording reasons for approval improves the auditability of the committee's commercial wisdom without inviting the tribunal to second-guess it.

Amendment s. 19IBC s. 31 · approval of the plan

F2Two-stage approval, continuity of licences, and the statutory clean slate

Three significant additions, the last of which writes a major judicial doctrine into the Code.

Two-stage approval, rectification, and a clock

  • New proviso to (1). On the professional's application, with sixty-six per cent committee approval, the tribunal may first approve the implementation of the plan and then approve the manner of distribution within thirty days. This lets a viable plan begin without distribution disputes holding the whole thing hostage.
  • Proviso to (2). Before rejecting a plan, the tribunal may notify the committee to rectify defects, rather than sending everyone back to square one.
  • New (2A). The tribunal must pass its order on the plan within thirty days, recording reasons for delay.

New (5): licences, permits and grants survive

Before

Nothing in section 31 protected the licences, permits, registrations, quotas and clearances a business depends on. A successful applicant could acquire a company only to find a sectoral regulator or authority treating the change as a trigger to suspend or cancel a vital permit.

After

A licence, permit, registration, quota, concession or similar grant associated with an approved plan shall not be suspended or terminated for the remaining period, provided the debtor or successful applicant complies with the obligations attached to it.

New (6): the clean slate, in statute

Before

The "clean slate" was judge-made. Approved plans were binding on all stakeholders, and the Supreme Court held that claims not in the plan stood extinguished, but the principle rested on case law rather than express text, and its edges were litigated repeatedly, including against tax and other authorities.

After

On approval, unless the plan provides otherwise, any claim against the debtor and its assets prior to approval is extinguished, and no proceeding may be continued or instituted on such claims, including for assessment. Explanation I preserves claims against promoters, those in management, guarantors and persons jointly liable. Explanation II extinguishes a jointly-liable payer's indemnity right against the debtor. Explanation III applies the clean slate from the commencement of the Code, except for matters that have attained finality.

Why

A buyer will not pay full value for a company that might be ambushed by a stale claim, a surprise tax demand, or a fresh assessment for a pre-approval period. Codifying extinguishment, and protecting licences, makes the slate genuinely clean and lifts the price a resolution applicant will offer, which benefits every creditor. The carve-outs keep guarantors and wrongdoers on the hook, so the clean slate rescues the business, not the people who ran it into the ground.

Ghanashyam Mishra & Sons (P) Ltd. v. Edelweiss ARC Ltd.(2021) 9 SCC 657

Held that once a plan is approved under section 31, all claims not part of the plan stand extinguished and no person may pursue a claim outside it, including governments and authorities.

Relevance: New sub-section (6) is the codification of Ghanashyam Mishra, and Explanation III gives it the same retrospective reach from the Code's commencement.

Committee of Creditors of Essar Steel v. Satish Kumar Gupta(2020) 8 SCC 531; State Bank of India v. V. Ramakrishnan, (2018) 17 SCC 394

Confirmed that approval of a plan does not discharge a guarantor's co-extensive liability.

Relevance: Explanation I to sub-section (6) preserves exactly this, ensuring the clean slate does not release guarantors or jointly liable persons.

Practitioner note

For acquirers, the diligence emphasis shifts. Pre-approval liabilities are largely washed away, so the focus moves to obligations attached to the licences being preserved, and to whether any matter has already "attained finality" and therefore escapes Explanation III. For lenders who also hold guarantees, the message is reassuring: pursue the company through the plan and the guarantor separately, and structure the plan so that it does not inadvertently waive guarantee rights.

Cluster G

The liquidation governance overhaul

Liquidation used to be the quiet endgame: a single liquidator, often the former resolution professional, selling assets under the tribunal's distant supervision. The 2026 Act rebuilds this stage. The committee stays in charge, a fresh liquidator takes over, a failed resolution can sometimes be revived, and a quick dissolution route opens for cases where realisation is pointless.

Amendment s. 20IBC s. 33 · initiation of liquidation

G1A moratorium in liquidation, and a one-time revival of the resolution process

Before

On a failed resolution the tribunal ordered liquidation. There was no moratorium tailored to the liquidation stage; protection came from section 33(5)'s bar on suits. Once liquidation was ordered there was no statutory mechanism to step back into resolution.

After

The liquidation order now declares a moratorium for the purposes of section 14(1)(a) and (c), applied to liquidation. Before ordering liquidation, the tribunal must consider a committee application (sixty-six per cent) to restore the resolution process, and may restore it, once only, for up to one hundred and twenty days. New sub-section (6) lets the liquidator continue or commence suits only with the tribunal's leave.

Why

Two mischiefs are addressed. First, the want of a clear moratorium in liquidation left the estate exposed to scattered enforcement. Second, and more importantly, a resolution could fail for reasons that later evaporate, a bidder returns, funding appears, and yet the Code offered no way back. The one-time restoration gives creditors a controlled second chance at rescue before assets are broken up, consistent with the Code's stated preference for resolution over liquidation.

Amendment s. 21IBC s. 34Amendment s. 22new s. 34A

G2A fresh liquidator, chosen and removable by the committee

In plain terms. The person who failed to resolve the company no longer automatically liquidates it. A new liquidator is brought in through the Board, and the creditors can sack and replace that liquidator if they lose confidence.

Before

Section 34 made the resolution professional the liquidator by default, unless the tribunal replaced them. The committee had no power to choose or remove the liquidator; on liquidation, all powers of the board vested in the liquidator and creditor input was largely consultative.

After

The tribunal refers the appointment to the Board, which recommends an insolvency professional, and the resolution professional is expressly barred from being appointed or replaced as liquidator of the same debtor. The Board must propose a name, with consent, within ten days. New section 34A lets the committee, by sixty-six per cent, resolve to replace the liquidator with another professional, on whose consent the tribunal makes the change.

Why

A professional whose resolution attempt failed carries the baggage of that failure into the asset sale, and creditors had no lever to insist on fresh eyes or remove an under-performing liquidator. Separating the roles and giving the committee selection and removal powers is the liquidation-stage expression of creditor control, and it aligns the liquidator's accountability with the people who bear the loss.

Practitioner note

Resolution professionals must now plan for a clean handover on a liquidation order, since they cannot carry the matter forward themselves; section 34 requires the former professional to forward all records to the new liquidator. For creditors, the replacement power in section 34A is real bargaining power, but it needs a sixty-six per cent supermajority, so it cannot be wielded by a single frustrated lender.

Amendment s. 23IBC s. 35Amendment s. 24IBC s. 36Amendment s. 25IBC ss. 38 to 42 omitted

G3The liquidator's powers, the estate, and the claims process moved to regulation

  • Section 35. The liquidator's duty to "verify claims of all the creditors" becomes a duty to maintain an updated list of claims in the specified manner; the power to "invite and settle" claims loses the "invite"; and the liquidator may continue or institute avoidance and fraudulent-trading proceedings. A new sub-section (2) states that the committee supervises the liquidation in the specified manner.
  • Section 36. The liquidation estate provisions are updated to refer to avoidance, fraudulent or wrongful trading, and section 47 proceedings, keeping the language consistent with the new defined terms.
  • Sections 38 to 42 omitted. The detailed statutory claims process in liquidation (consolidation, verification, admission or rejection, appeals and substantiation) is removed from primary law and shifted into the regulations, where it can be calibrated more nimbly. The Explanation in section 35 makes the omission prospective.
Why

The hard-coded claims machinery in sections 38 to 42 had proved rigid. Moving it to regulation lets the Board run a faster, form-driven claims process, the same instinct behind replacing "verify claims" with maintaining an updated list. The avoidance additions ensure the liquidator can pursue clawbacks now that section 26 keeps those proceedings alive independently.

Still unsettled

Until the replacement regulations issue, practitioners should watch the transition carefully: the omission of sections 38 to 42 leaves the detailed claims procedure to delegated legislation, and the saving Explanation confines the change to liquidations begun after commencement, so ongoing liquidations continue under the old machinery.

Amendment s. 33IBC s. 54 · dissolution

G4A 180-day liquidation clock, and dissolution without full liquidation

Before

Section 54 simply said that once assets were "completely liquidated" the liquidator applied for dissolution. There was no time limit, and no route to dissolve a shell with nothing worth selling other than to grind through the full liquidation.

After

The liquidator must liquidate and apply for dissolution within one hundred and eighty days, extendable once by ninety days. New sub-section (2A) lets the committee decide to dissolve the debtor (a compressed exit), on which the tribunal may order dissolution from the date of its order; any leftover asset is sold and surplus credited to the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Fund. Sub-sections (1A) and (1B) preserve pending avoidance and other proceedings, with the committee directing how they are pursued and distributed, and (2B) confirms dissolution does not stop them. The tribunal must order dissolution within thirty days.

Why

Open-ended liquidations were a notorious drain, dragging on for years over assets worth little. A hard outer limit forces closure. The committee-led compressed dissolution recognises the commercial reality that some companies have nothing to realise, and lets creditors choose a quick burial rather than an expensive, pointless sale process, while ring-fencing avoidance recoveries so that value is not lost in the rush to dissolve.

Cluster H

Secured creditors and the waterfall

Two sections carry some of the most consequential commercial reforms in the whole Amendment. Section 52 rewrites how a secured creditor stands outside the liquidation estate, and section 53 rewrites who gets paid, in what character, and where government dues sit. Read them together: they are the statutory answer to a decade of contested distribution law.

Amendment s. 31IBC s. 52 · secured creditor in liquidation

H1A deadline to stand outside, a super-majority over shared collateral, and a costlier exit

In plain terms. A secured creditor in liquidation has a choice. It can drop its security into the common pool and take its place in the queue, or it can stand outside, sell its own collateral, and keep the proceeds. The Amendment puts a clock on that choice, stops a single lender from acting alone where collateral is shared, and increases what an outside realiser must hand back to the estate.

Sub-section (2): a fourteen-day election, or deemed relinquishment

Before

A secured creditor electing to realise its own security merely had to inform the liquidator and identify the asset. There was no statutory deadline. Indecision and delay by secured creditors stalled liquidations, because the liquidator could not finalise the estate while the perimeter of relinquished and retained security remained open.

After

The election must be made within fourteen days of the liquidation commencement date. If the secured creditor does not inform the liquidator and identify the charged asset within that window, the security interest is deemed relinquished to the liquidation estate. The creditor then takes its chances inside the section 53 waterfall rather than outside it.

Why

Certainty and speed. A liquidator cannot run a clean process if the boundary between pooled and self-realised assets stays fluid for months. A short, hard deadline forces the secured creditor to commit early, and the deeming provision removes the temptation to keep options open at the estate's expense.

Proviso to sub-section (2): no solo realisation over shared collateral

Before

Where several creditors held security over the same asset, the Code did not expressly coordinate their realisation. One secured creditor could press to enforce in a way that prejudiced the others sharing the same collateral, producing disputes over priority, marshalling and the conduct of sale.

After

Where more than one secured creditor has a security interest over the same asset, no secured creditor may realise unless the realisation is agreed by secured creditors representing not less than sixty-six per cent. of the value of all claims secured by such security interests. A minority charge-holder can no longer force a solo sale of shared collateral.

Why

Collective discipline over a common asset. The super-majority threshold mirrors the voting architecture that runs through the rest of the Code and prevents value destruction from uncoordinated enforcement, while still letting a clear majority of the secured class proceed.

Substituted sub-section (8): the cost of staying outside has risen

Before

A secured creditor realising its own security had to hand back to the liquidator only the insolvency resolution process costs attributable to it. Workmen's dues and liquidation costs were not, in terms, deducted from its realisation.

After

The deduction now covers the insolvency resolution process costs and the liquidation costs, and workmen's dues referable to clause (a) and sub-clause (i) of clause (b) of section 53(1). The outside realiser must transfer these amounts to the liquidator for inclusion in the estate, on specified terms.

Why

Equity between the creditor who pools and the creditor who self-realises. Workmen's dues for the twenty-four-month period and the costs of the liquidation are charges that, in justice, the whole estate should bear, including the secured creditor who takes its asset out of the common pool. The amendment closes a gap that let outside realisers escape a fair share of these priority burdens.

Transitional

A new Explanation makes the substituted sub-section (2), the fourteen-day election and deemed relinquishment, prospective: it does not apply to a liquidation process initiated on or before the date of commencement of the 2026 Act. Practitioners running legacy liquidations must check the commencement date before assuming the deadline bites.

Amendment s. 32IBC s. 53 · distribution of assets

H2Partial relinquishment, the character of a secured creditor, and the end of Rainbow Papers

The waterfall in section 53 is the gravitational centre of liquidation. Two Explanations and a pair of Illustrations recalibrate it, and one of them reverses a Supreme Court decision that had unsettled the entire priority scheme.

Explanation to s.53(1)(b)(ii): you are secured only up to the value of your security

Before

When a secured creditor relinquished security worth less than its total debt, the Code did not spell out how it should be treated for the shortfall. Was it a secured creditor for its whole admitted claim and entitled to rank in clause (b)(ii) for all of it, or only to the extent of the value of the security it gave up? This was the precise computation question that split the Court in the resolution context.

After

A new Explanation clarifies that where the value of the security interest relinquished is less than the total debt, the creditor is a secured creditor only to the extent of the value of that security (determined as may be specified), and for the remaining value of the debt he is treated as an unsecured creditor. This is a collateral-specific, security-value approach.

Why

To end the pooled-versus-collateral-specific dispute on a principled footing. A secured creditor's preferential rank should reflect the value of the security it actually surrenders, not the full face of its debt. The balance falls to the unsecured tier, where it belongs. This aligns the liquidation waterfall with the security-value logic and feeds directly into the dissenting-creditor floor in section 30(2)(ba) discussed in Cluster F.

DBS Bank Ltd. Singapore v. Ruchi Soya Industries Ltd.2024 SCC OnLine SC 3

The Court read section 30(2)(b)(ii) with sections 52 and 53 and favoured a collateral-specific entitlement for the dissenting secured creditor, by reference to the value of its security, and referred the conflict with the pooled view to a larger Bench.

Relevance: The new section 53(1)(b)(ii) Explanation embeds the security-value approach in the waterfall itself. It does not formally answer the larger-Bench reference, but it strongly signals the direction Parliament has chosen.

India Resurgence ARC (P) Ltd. v. Amit Metaliks Ltd.2021 SCC OnLine SC 409

A two-judge Bench held a dissenting secured creditor is confined to liquidation value and cannot insist on the full value of its security, deferring to the committee's distribution.

Relevance: The competing pooled view. The waterfall Explanation now leans against treating the whole admitted claim as secured, recasting the surplus as unsecured.

Explanation to s.53(1)(e)(i): government dues, and the reversal of Rainbow Papers

Before

Section 53(1)(e)(i) placed amounts due to the Central and State Governments for the two years preceding liquidation in the fifth rank, below secured creditors and workmen. But a 2022 decision held that a statutory tax charge could make the State a "secured creditor", potentially lifting government dues far up the waterfall and destabilising the priority scheme that lenders had relied on.

After

A new Explanation declares that any amount due to the Central or State Government, whether or not a security interest is created to secure it by an act of parties or merely by operation of law, for the two years preceding the liquidation commencement date, is distributed under clause (e)(i), and any remaining government amount under clause (f). Government dues are pinned to their statutory rank and cannot climb the waterfall by force of a tax charge.

Why

To restore the certainty the waterfall is meant to provide. The 2022 decision had let a charge "created merely by operation of law" convert the State into a secured creditor, upsetting the settled expectation of lenders and the architecture of section 53. Read with the new Explanation to the definition of "security interest" in section 3(31), which excludes interests arising merely by operation of law, the Amendment decisively relocates government dues to their intended tier.

State Tax Officer v. Rainbow Papers Ltd.2022 SCC OnLine SC 1162

The Court held that a statutory charge for tax under a State VAT statute made the State a secured creditor within the Code's definition, so its dues could not be ignored in a resolution plan. A review was later dismissed in Sanjay Kumar Agarwal v. State Tax Officer (2023).

Relevance: This is the decision the new Explanations to sections 3(31) and 53(1)(e)(i) are designed to reverse. By excluding charges arising merely by operation of law from "security interest" and fixing government dues in clause (e)(i)/(f), the Amendment removes the basis on which the State was treated as secured.

Paschimanchal Vidyut Vitran Nigam Ltd. v. Raman Ispat (P) Ltd.2023 SCC OnLine SC 842

A later Bench distinguished Rainbow Papers, confined it to its facts, and reaffirmed that government dues take the rank assigned to them by section 53 rather than displacing secured creditors.

Relevance: The Amendment adopts the Raman Ispat position and writes it into the statute, ending the tension between the two decisions. The Ministry's discussion paper of 18 January 2023 had flagged precisely this need.

Section 53(2) already disregarded contractual arrangements between equally ranked recipients that disrupt the statutory order. Two Illustrations now make its operation concrete.

Cluster I

Avoidance and fraudulent trading

Avoidance applications claw back value that left the company in the run-up to insolvency. The Amendment lengthens the look-back window by anchoring it to the initiation date, gives creditors a direct route when the professional sits on his hands, and lets the liquidator pursue fraudulent and wrongful trading.

Amendment ss. 26, 27, 30IBC ss. 43, 46, 50 · look-back windows

I1The clock now starts at the initiation date

Before

The relevant period for preferential transactions (s.43), undervalued transactions (s.46) and extortionate credit transactions (s.50) was measured backwards from the insolvency commencement date, the date of admission. Time spent waiting for an over-burdened tribunal to admit a petition narrowed the very window in which suspect transactions could be caught.

After

The window now runs "from the initiation date and ending on the insolvency commencement date" added to the existing look-back. Because the initiation date is the filing date, and the new proviso to section 5(11) fixes it at the first application where several are pending, the look-back effectively stretches to cover the admission delay.

Why

To stop admission delay from shrinking the avoidance window. Where months pass between filing and admission, transactions in that gap previously risked falling outside the look-back. Anchoring the period to the initiation date neutralises tribunal delay and preserves the clawback estate.

Anuj Jain, IRP for Jaypee Infratech Ltd. v. Axis Bank Ltd.(2020) 8 SCC 401

The Court laid down the analytical framework for preferential transactions under section 43, distinguishing them from undervalued and extortionate transactions, and stressed that each category has distinct ingredients and look-back rules to be applied with rigour.

Relevance: The framework survives; the Amendment only re-times the look-back. Applications must still be pleaded with the precision Anuj Jain demands, but with a window that now reaches back from the initiation date.

Amendment s. 28IBC s. 47 · substituted

I2A creditor's direct route where the professional fails to act

Before

Old section 47 let a creditor, member or partner approach the tribunal only for undervalued transactions that the professional had failed to report. Preferences, extortionate credit and fraudulent trading had no equivalent fallback, so if the professional did not pursue them, they often went unremedied.

After

Substituted section 47 extends the creditor's fallback to all four heads: a preferential transaction (s.43), an undervalued transaction (s.45), an extortionate credit transaction (s.50), or fraudulent or wrongful trading (s.66). A creditor (alone or jointly), member or partner may apply, and the tribunal may make orders as if the application had been filed by the professional. It must also direct disciplinary proceedings where the professional, having had the information, failed to report.

Why

Avoidance recovery had a structural weakness: it depended on a single gatekeeper. Where a professional was inert, conflicted or simply overwhelmed, value stayed lost. Opening a direct creditor route across all avoidance heads gives stakeholders a self-help remedy and disciplines professionals who neglect the estate.

Practitioner reality

This is a meaningful new lever for an aggrieved creditor. If the resolution professional or liquidator has had information about a suspect transaction and sat on it, the creditor need not wait. The same application that secures avoidance can trigger a disciplinary reference against the professional, which changes the negotiating dynamic well before any order is passed.

Amendment ss. 29, 46IBC ss. 49, 66 · related parties; liquidator standing

I3Transactions defrauding creditors, and a liquidator who can sue for fraudulent trading

Section 49: the related-party good-faith carve-out

Before

The proviso to section 49 protected an interest acquired in good faith, for value and without notice, but only where it was acquired "from a person other than the corporate debtor". A transferee taking from a related party of the corporate debtor arguably fell within the shelter.

After

The proviso now reads "from a person other than the corporate debtor or a related party of the corporate debtor". The good-faith shelter no longer protects acquisitions traced through a related party, closing an obvious avoidance-defeating channel.

Section 66: the liquidator joins the resolution professional

Before

Section 66 let only the resolution professional apply for fraudulent or wrongful trading orders, and sub-section (2) spoke of conduct during the corporate insolvency resolution process. A liquidator had no clear standing to pursue these claims once the company moved into liquidation.

After

The marginal heading is recast as "Fraudulent or wrongful trading". Sub-section (1) now empowers the resolution professional "or the liquidator", and sub-section (2) extends to applications "by a liquidator". The liquidator can now pursue delinquent management for contributions to the estate.

Why

Most value-destroying conduct only surfaces during liquidation, yet the person actually in charge then, the liquidator, lacked standing to act on it. Aligning section 66 with the liquidator's role, and with the new liquidation-stage avoidance powers in section 35, removes an artificial limit on recovery. This dovetails with the survival principle that avoidance and fraudulent-trading proceedings outlive the main process (Cluster E, section 26).

Cluster J

Pre-pack, and the new creditor-initiated route Chapter IV-A not yet in force

The Amendment lowers the consent threshold inside the existing pre-pack regime, deletes the long-dormant fast-track chapter, and builds an entirely new, out-of-court, debtor-in-possession process: the creditor-initiated insolvency resolution process, or CIIRP. The CIIRP chapter has not yet been brought into force, so what follows is the architecture practitioners must prepare for, not live law.

Amendment ss. 34 to 38IBC ss. 54A, 54C, 54F, 54L, 54N · pre-pack

J1Pre-pack recalibrated: a lower consent threshold and tidied cross-references

Before

To begin a pre-packaged insolvency resolution process, the corporate debtor needed the approval of unrelated financial creditors holding not less than sixty-six per cent. in value (section 54A(2)(e) and (3)). The base of eligible processes and the cross-references in sections 54L and 54N reflected the pre-pack and liquidation architecture as it then stood.

After

The threshold drops to fifty-one per cent. in both sub-sections (2)(e) and (3) of section 54A, making pre-pack easier to trigger. Sections 54A(2)(a) and (b) now also recognise the creditor-initiated process. Section 54C(3) is simplified to "such information as may be specified". Section 54F(5) aligns the cooperation duty with the amended section 19. Sections 54L and 54N pick up the new liquidation sub-clauses (iv) and (v) and the rectification proviso.

Why

Pre-pack for micro, small and medium enterprises had been under-used, partly because assembling a two-thirds consent block among lenders was hard for small borrowers. Lowering the bar to a simple majority in value, combined with cross-referencing the new creditor route, is intended to make the quicker, debtor-friendly process actually usable.

Amendment s. 39IBC Chapter IV of Part II · omitted

J2The fast-track corporate insolvency resolution process is gone

Before

Chapter IV of Part II housed the fast-track corporate insolvency resolution process (sections 55 to 58), a compressed timetable for notified classes of debtor. It was little used and overtaken in practice by the pre-pack regime.

After

Chapter IV is omitted in full. Consequential clean-ups follow: section 33(1)(a) loses its reference to the fast-track process, and section 65(3) is recast around the pre-pack and creditor-initiated processes.

Why

Statutory hygiene. A dead-letter chapter that practitioners never invoked added clutter and cross-reference risk. Removing it makes room for the creditor-initiated process that replaces it as the streamlined alternative to a full corporate insolvency resolution process.

58-IWithdrawal of the public announcement with 90% committee approval, subject to the same constraints as section 12A.
58JPlan approval: 66% committee vote, then submission to the tribunal, which passes an order under section 31.
58KApplies named provisions of Chapters II, III, VI and VII mutatis mutandis, with defined substitutions of CIIRP terms for CIRP terms.
Why

The full corporate insolvency resolution process is heavy: admission, automatic moratorium, displacement of management, and a long tribunal-supervised timeline. For many defaults, especially with cooperative borrowers, that is disproportionate. CIIRP offers a lighter, creditor-led, debtor-in-possession alternative that keeps the company running and reserves the tribunal for objections, conversion and final plan approval. It draws on the debtor-in-possession philosophy already seen in the pre-pack regime.

Not yet in force, and several open design choices

Chapter IV-A has not been notified into force. Almost every operative parameter, the eligible asset, income or debt levels (s.58A), the notified class of financial institution (s.58B), and the detailed manner and conditions, is left to delegated rule-making and notification. Until those rules and the commencement notification arrive, CIIRP cannot be initiated. Practitioners should treat this chapter as a forthcoming regime to design systems and templates around, not a present remedy.

Practitioner reality, once live

CIIRP will reward lenders who can quickly assemble 51% in value within the notified class and who are willing to leave a cooperative management in place under professional oversight. The absence of an automatic moratorium changes the risk picture: enforcement by others is not frozen by default, so the professional must judge whether to seek a confirmed moratorium under section 58G. The hard back-stop is conversion under section 58H, which gives the creditor a credible route into full CIRP if cooperation breaks down.

Cluster K

Voluntary liquidation

Amendment s. 41IBC s. 59 · voluntary liquidation

K1A one-year deadline, a clean exit route, and a duty to inform

Before

Voluntary liquidation under section 59 had to meet conditions "as may be specified by the Board" but carried no statutory outer time limit in the section itself, so solvent wind-ups could drift for years. There was no express route to terminate a voluntary liquidation once begun. Sub-section (4) required the company to "notify" the Registrar and the Board.

After

Section 59(2) now requires completion "within such period which shall not be more than one year, as may be specified". New sub-sections (5A) to (5C) create a termination route: a members' special resolution, plus approval of two-thirds in value of any creditors within seven days, with the liquidator intimating the Board and Registrar, ends the proceeding. Sub-section (4) changes "notify" to "inform", and sub-section (6) imports the claim-verification duty in section 18(b).

Why

Two problems. First, open-ended voluntary liquidations clogged the system and left companies in limbo; a one-year discipline forces closure. Second, a solvent company that changed its mind, or whose circumstances improved, had no clean way out and risked being forced through to dissolution. The termination mechanism lets members and creditors jointly call it off, with safeguards. Importing section 18(b) brings claim verification into line with the rest of the Code.

Practitioner reality

Advisers running solvent wind-ups must now plan to a one-year horizon and document the basis for any specified extension. The termination route is genuinely useful: where a voluntary liquidation was commenced prematurely, or a buyer emerges for the going concern, the special-resolution-plus-creditor-approval mechanism offers a defined off-ramp instead of an awkward application to undo the process.

Cluster L

Group insolvency Chapter VA not yet in force

Amendment s. 42IBC new Chapter VA · s. 59A

L1An enabling framework for groups, built to be filled by rules

In plain terms. When several companies in the same corporate group fail together, running their insolvencies in separate silos wastes value and produces inconsistent outcomes. Section 59A gives the Central Government power to make rules to coordinate group insolvencies: a common bench, coordinated committees, a common professional, and binding coordination agreements. It is an enabling provision, the detail will come through rules.

Before

The Code had no group insolvency framework. Coordination was improvised case by case through judicial innovation, with tribunals consolidating or coordinating proceedings on an ad hoc basis and no settled rules on common benches, group committees or shared professionals.

After

New section 59A empowers the Central Government to prescribe the manner and conditions for conducting Part II proceedings against two or more group corporate debtors. Rules may provide for a common bench and transfer of proceedings, coordination between committees and professionals, a common insolvency professional, a group committee, binding coordination agreements, and cost-sharing. A defined "group", "control", "significant ownership" (26% voting rights) and "insolvency proceedings" frame the power, and draft rules must be laid before Parliament.

Why

Large corporate failures are rarely confined to one entity. Without a coordination framework, group insolvencies produced duplicated processes, forum conflicts and value leakage. The Working Group on Group Insolvency (2019) recommended an enabling, rules-based approach rather than a rigid statutory code, and section 59A adopts exactly that design, leaving flexibility for the rule-maker while fixing the core definitions in primary law.

State Bank of India v. Videocon Industries Ltd.NCLT, Mumbai (2019)

The tribunal ordered substantive consolidation of multiple Videocon group entities, treating their assets and liabilities together for resolution, one of the first significant Indian experiments in group treatment under the Code.

Relevance: Section 59A supplies the statutory footing that decisions like this previously lacked, converting judicial improvisation into a rule-based framework.

Edelweiss Asset Reconstruction Co. Ltd. v. Sachet Infrastructure (P) Ltd.NCLAT

The appellate tribunal endorsed coordinated, simultaneous resolution of group companies developing a common project, to preserve value across the group.

Relevance: Illustrates the coordination need that section 59A is designed to systematise through common benches and binding coordination agreements.

Not yet in force

Chapter VA has not been notified. It confers rule-making power; the substantive group regime will exist only when the Central Government makes and notifies rules, which must first be laid before both Houses. Until then, group coordination continues to rest on case-by-case tribunal practice. Treat section 59A as the enabling shell of a regime still to be built.

Cluster M

Personal and individual insolvency

Part III governs individuals and personal guarantors. The headline reform removes the automatic interim moratorium where a personal guarantor to a corporate debtor is the subject of the application, an anti-abuse measure aimed at guarantors who filed to shelter behind a stay. Several timing and drafting changes follow.

Amendment ss. 51, 55IBC ss. 96, 124 · interim moratorium

M1No automatic interim moratorium for a personal guarantor to a corporate debtor

Before

The moment an application was filed under section 94 or 95 (insolvency resolution) or sections 122 or 123 (bankruptcy), an interim moratorium commenced automatically over the debtor's debts and proceedings. A personal guarantor to a corporate debtor could file and instantly freeze enforcement, then run the clock, a tactic used to stall recovery from guarantors.

After

New sub-section (4) in each of sections 96 and 124 disapplies the automatic interim moratorium "where the debtor is a personal guarantor to a corporate debtor". The general automatic interim moratorium survives for other individuals; it is switched off only for the personal-guarantor-to-corporate-debtor cohort, removing the strategic stay.

Why

The automatic stay had become a shield for delay. A guarantor whose principal debtor was already in insolvency could file a personal application purely to suspend enforcement, contributing nothing to resolution. Removing the automatic moratorium for that specific class restores the creditor's ability to pursue the guarantee while the application is examined, without disturbing the protection that genuine individual debtors retain.

State Bank of India v. V. Ramakrishnan(2018) 17 SCC 394

The Court held that the section 14 moratorium in the corporate process does not extend to the assets of a personal guarantor, so creditors may proceed against guarantors during the corporate debtor's moratorium.

Relevance: The new sub-sections extend the same anti-shelter logic into Part III, ensuring a guarantor cannot manufacture a personal stay to defeat enforcement.

Lalit Kumar Jain v. Union of India(2021) 9 SCC 321

The Court upheld the 2019 notification bringing personal guarantors to corporate debtors within Part III and confirmed that approval of a corporate debtor's resolution plan does not discharge the guarantor.

Relevance: Establishes the constitutional footing for the personal-guarantor regime that section 96(4)/124(4) now refines.

Dilip B. Jiwrajka v. Union of India(2024) 5 SCC 435

A three-judge Bench upheld the constitutional validity of sections 95 to 100, holding that the resolution professional's role under section 99 is recommendatory and that the adjudicating authority applies its mind at the section 100 stage; no hearing is required at the interim-moratorium stage.

Relevance: Confirms the procedural architecture into which the new sub-section (4) fits; removing the automatic moratorium for guarantors does not offend the process the Court approved.

BRS Ventures Investments Ltd. v. SREI Infrastructure Finance Ltd.(2024) 8 SCC 489

The Court reaffirmed that a creditor may proceed against both the principal borrower and the guarantor, and that satisfaction from one reduces but does not by itself extinguish the co-extensive liability of the other.

Relevance: Reinforces why a guarantor should not obtain an automatic personal stay; the guarantee remains independently enforceable.

Amendment s. 52IBC s. 99 · report timeline

M2The professional's report: ten days becomes twenty-one, and the debtor is heard

Before

The resolution professional had to examine the application and report within ten days of appointment. Sub-section (2)'s proof-of-repayment mechanism spoke of the debtor proving repayment, without expressly bringing the creditor into that exchange.

After

The reporting period is extended to twenty-one days, and sub-section (2) is widened to read "the debtor and the creditor", so both sides are drawn into the verification of whether the claimed debt remains unpaid.

Why

Ten days was unrealistic for a proper examination, particularly once the professional must engage both debtor and creditor on the question of repayment. A twenty-one-day window allows a considered, adversarially tested report, improving the quality of the recommendation the tribunal relies on at the section 100 stage.

M3Repayment-plan meeting, bankruptcy timing, fraud and government dues, and a frivolous-application penalty

Practitioner reality

Read together, the Part III amendments shift the balance back towards creditors of personal guarantors: no automatic stay, a fraud-unwinding power, government dues confined to rank, and a penalty for frivolous filings. Advisers acting for guarantors can no longer rely on the filing itself to buy time, and must instead engage on the merits of the repayment plan within the recast timetable.

Cluster N

Penalties and enforcement

The Amendment adds targeted penalties for the conduct that most often frustrates the process, frivolous filings, breach of the moratorium or an approved plan, and an operational creditor's concealment of a dispute, while omitting two older, blunter penal sections and consolidating the residual penalty provision.

Amendment ss. 44, 47, 48IBC new ss. 64A, 67A, 67B, 67C

N1New targeted penalties

What each new penal section addresses

ClauseNew sectionMischief targeted
s.44s.64AFrivolous or vexatious applications. Penalty where a person initiates a corporate process frivolously or with malicious intent. Pairs with the recast section 65 (fraudulent or malicious initiation) and the new section 61 appeal timeline to discipline abusive filings.
s.47s.67ARecast to address contravention of the moratorium or of an approved resolution plan, giving the breach of a plan, previously hard to sanction directly, a clear penal consequence. s.48ss.67B, 67CNew sections penalising, among other conduct, an operational creditor's non-disclosure of a pre-existing dispute when filing under section 9, the very concealment the twin-test in Mobilox was meant to police, now backed by a statutory penalty.
Why

The Code's earlier penal provisions were either too general to bite or aimed at the wrong conduct. The recurring practical problems, speculative filings to extract settlements, defiance of the moratorium or an approved plan, and operational creditors hiding a genuine dispute to force admission, lacked sharp, specific sanctions. These sections supply them, raising the cost of strategic abuse.

Mobilox Innovations (P) Ltd. v. Kirusa Software (P) Ltd.(2018) 1 SCC 353

The Court held that an operational creditor's application must be rejected if there is a plausible, pre-existing dispute that is not spurious or hypothetical, the "existence of a dispute" test under section 8 and 9.

Relevance: The new penalty for non-disclosure of a dispute reinforces Mobilox by punishing the concealment that the test is designed to catch, deterring operational creditors from suppressing a known dispute.

Amendment ss. 49, 50, 68IBC ss. 74, 76 omitted; s. 235A substituted

N2Two old penal sections omitted, the residual penalty consolidated

Before

Section 74 penalised contravention of the moratorium and of resolution plans in broad terms; section 76 dealt with related default. Section 235A was the residual "punishment where no specific penalty is provided" clause.

After

Sections 74 and 76 are omitted, their subject matter relocated to the sharper, targeted sections 67A to 67C. Section 235A is substituted to serve as the recalibrated residual penalty, with savings so that omission of sections 74 and 76 does not disturb liabilities already incurred.

Why

Rationalisation. Once specific, well-aimed penalties exist for moratorium and plan breaches, the older overlapping sections are redundant and risk inconsistent sentencing. Consolidating the residual penalty in a substituted section 235A, with express savings, keeps the penal scheme coherent without creating gaps for conduct that straddles the transition.

Savings

The omission of sections 74 and 76 is accompanied by savings provisions so that anything done, or any liability incurred, under those sections before commencement is preserved. Practitioners handling conduct that pre-dates 26 May 2026 should plead under the provisions in force at the relevant time.

Cluster O

The Board, discipline and information utilities

A cluster of institutional reforms strengthens the regulator's hand: a standards-of-conduct regime for committees of creditors, recognition of service providers, a mandatory information-utility filing before an operational creditor can file, and a recast disciplinary architecture with a defined appeal to the appellate tribunal.

Amendment s. 59IBC s. 196 · functions of the Board

O1Service providers, and a code of conduct for the committee of creditors

Before

The Board's functions in section 196 covered insolvency professionals, agencies and information utilities. There was no express mandate to register or regulate other "service providers", and no statutory hook for a committee-of-creditors code of conduct, leaving creditor behaviour largely unregulated despite the committee's decisive role.

After

Section 196 is widened to recognise and regulate service providers, and a new clause (sa) empowers the Board to specify standards of conduct for the committee of creditors. A fee Explanation clarifies the Board's power over the fees of professionals and providers.

Why

The committee of creditors wields enormous power over outcomes yet operated without a formal conduct standard, a recurring criticism after several contested resolutions. A statutory code-of-conduct power lets the Board hold committees to minimum standards of good faith, participation and reasoned decision-making. Recognising service providers reflects the maturing ecosystem of valuers, process advisers and platforms that the original Code did not anticipate.

Amendment s. 62IBC s. 215 · information utilities

O2An operational creditor must file with an information utility before section 9

Before

Filing financial information with an information utility was, in practice, treated as optional for operational creditors. Disputes over the existence and quantum of debt clogged the admission stage because there was no authenticated record to point to.

After

Section 215 now requires an operational creditor to file financial information with an information utility before initiating a section 9 application, and provides for deemed authentication of information in specified circumstances. The authenticated record becomes the evidential backbone of admission.

Why

To move the contest over debt off the tribunal's docket and onto an authenticated record. If an operational creditor must lodge its claim with a utility first, and the debtor has the chance to authenticate or contest it there, the section 9 hearing becomes far cleaner. This complements the new penalty for concealing a dispute (Cluster N) and the mandatory-admission discipline of section 7 (Cluster B).

Practitioner reality

Operational creditors must now build the information-utility filing into their pre-litigation checklist. Skipping it risks a defective application. Conversely, debtors gain an earlier, formal opportunity to record a dispute on the utility's record, which can be decisive when the matter reaches the tribunal.

Cluster P

Rules, the electronic portal, cross-border, and removal of difficulties

The final cluster carries the delegated-legislation engine of the whole Amendment: expanded rule-making, a statutory electronic platform, an enabling cross-border framework aligned to the UNCITRAL Model Law, and a time-limited power to remove difficulties. Three of these are not yet in force.

Amendment ss. 69, 70IBC ss. 239, 240 · rule and regulation making

P1The expanded delegated-legislation power

Sections 239 (Central Government rules) and 240 (Board regulations) are extended with a long list of new matters on which rules and regulations may be made, every new mechanism in the Amendment that is left to be "specified" or "prescribed" finds its enabling hook here. This is the plumbing that the substantive reforms depend on.

Why

Almost every reform in the 2026 Act, the fourteen-day election manner, the CIIRP thresholds, the group-insolvency rules, the monitoring-committee terms, the manner of valuing security, is deliberately left to delegated legislation so the regime can be calibrated without further primary amendment. Sections 239 and 240 supply the corresponding powers. Until the relevant rules and regulations are notified, several reforms remain dormant in practice even where the primary provision is in force.

Practitioner reality

Read the bare section together with the rules. A provision that is "in force" may still be inoperable until the Board or Government specifies the manner. Track the notifications: the operative detail of this Amendment will live largely in subordinate legislation.

Amendment s. 71IBC new ss. 240B, 240C

P2An electronic portal, and a cross-border framework not yet in force

New sectionWhat it doesStatus
s.240BEstablishes a statutory electronic platform for filing, service, record-keeping and conduct of proceedings under the Code, the digital backbone for an end-to-end electronic process.Pending

Why

India had long relied on the inadequate sections 234 and 235 (bilateral agreements and letters of request) for cross-border cases, a route the Insolvency Law Committee's report of 16 October 2018 found unworkable and recommended replacing with the UNCITRAL Model Law. Section 240C supplies the enabling power for a modern, reciprocity-based framework. Section 240B answers the parallel need for a single electronic platform after years of fragmented filing practice.

Not yet in force

Both sections await notification and supporting rules. The cross-border regime in particular is enabling only: it will become operational when the Model-Law-aligned rules are made and notified. Until then, cross-border coordination continues under the existing, limited provisions.

Relevance: Section 240C is the legislative response, conferring the power to build that framework through rules.

Amendment s. 72IBC s. 242 · removal of difficulties

P3A renewed power to remove difficulties

Before

The original removal-of-difficulties power in section 242 was tied to the commencement of the 2016 Code and had, by its terms, spent its force.

After

Section 242 is recast to give the Central Government a fresh power to remove difficulties arising in giving effect to the provisions as amended, exercisable for a defined period (within five years), with every order laid before Parliament.

Why

An amendment of this scale, with multiple new chapters and a heavy reliance on subordinate legislation, will throw up transitional wrinkles. A time-limited removal-of-difficulties power lets the Government smooth genuine implementation problems without returning to Parliament for every minor fix, subject to the discipline of a sunset and parliamentary oversight.

What to watch

Four threads will determine how this Amendment actually lands, and a practitioner should track each one.

  • The pending notifications. The creditor-initiated process (Chapter IV-A), group insolvency (Chapter VA), the electronic platform (section 240B) and the cross-border framework (section 240C) are on the statute book but not yet operative. None can be used until separately notified, and each depends on supporting rules.
  • The secured-creditor computation question. The new section 53(1)(b)(ii) Explanation leans collateral-specific, and section 30(2)(ba) recalibrates the dissenting creditor's floor, but the larger-Bench reference in DBS Bank on how to value a dissenting secured creditor has not been mooted. Treat the precise manner of valuation as live until the Court or the rules speak.
  • The government-dues reversal. The Explanations to sections 3(31) and 53(1)(e)(i) are drafted to undo Rainbow Papers and adopt Raman Ispat. Expect early litigation testing the boundary of "merely by operation of law" against specific statutory-charge regimes.
  • The rules. So much is left to be "specified" that several live provisions are inoperable in practice until the Board and Government act. The operative detail of the 2026 Act will live in subordinate legislation.
