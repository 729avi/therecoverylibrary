Part I

Genesis: the first wave of recovery reform

If SARFAESI was Parliament's response to a banking system drowning in NPAs that it could not enforce against, the RDB Act came a decade earlier in response to a different illness, namely the inability of ordinary civil courts to dispose of bank suits within a banker's lifetime. It is the foundational layer of the modern recovery architecture.

By the late 1980s the volume of pending bank-recovery suits had become unmanageable. Civil courts treated bank claims like any other commercial dispute, with no priority on the docket and no specialised expertise. The Tiwari Committee in 1981 had already recommended the establishment of special tribunals; the Narasimham Committee on the Financial System in 1991 reaffirmed the recommendation in stronger terms. The legislative response was the RDB Act 1993, which created a two-tier specialised forum, the Debts Recovery Tribunal (DRT) and the Debts Recovery Appellate Tribunal (DRAT), exclusively to adjudicate bank claims at speed.

1981
Tiwari Committee

The first formal recommendation that Government should establish specialised tribunals for adjudication of debts due to banks and financial institutions. The recommendation lay unimplemented for a decade.

1991
Narasimham Committee on the Financial System

Repeated the call. Identified the burden of bad loans on the banking system and the impossibility of recovery through ordinary civil litigation as a structural drag on the economy.

1993
The Ordinance, then the Act

The Recovery of Debts Due to Banks and Financial Institutions Ordinance was promulgated on 24 June 1993 and was replaced by Act 51 of 1993, which received assent on 27 August 1993 and is deemed to have come into force on 24 June 1993 by virtue of section 1(3).

1995
The Delhi High Court strike-down

In Delhi Bar Association v. Union of India, the Delhi High Court struck down certain provisions of the Act as unconstitutional, holding that the exclusion of civil court jurisdiction in matters involving counter-claims and complex issues offended Article 14. The decision was carried in appeal to the Supreme Court.

2000
The first major amendment

Act 1 of 2000 substantially redrafted section 19, conferring on the DRT broad procedural powers (including those previously held to be deficient by the Delhi High Court) and introducing the Recovery Officer's machinery in greater detail. This amendment was upheld and largely settled the constitutional position.

2002
Union of India v. Delhi High Court Bar Association

The Supreme Court upheld the constitutional validity of the Act as amended in 2000, confirming that Parliament was competent to create a specialised tribunal with exclusive jurisdiction over bank-recovery matters.

2002
SARFAESI joins the system

The Securitisation Act layered self-help enforcement on top of the existing DRT machinery, with SARFAESI applications also coming to the DRT under section 17 SARFAESI. The DRT thus acquired a dual jurisdiction: as the original forum for bank claims under the RDB Act, and as the appellate forum for SARFAESI grievances.

2013
Multi-state cooperative banks

Act 1 of 2013 brought multi-state cooperative banks within the definition of "bank" under section 2(d)(vi), enabling them to use the DRT route.

2016
The structural overhaul (Act 44 of 2016)

The same omnibus statute that reshaped SARFAESI also rewrote large parts of the RDB Act. It recast section 19 with new procedural rigour, introduced a 30-day written-statement clock with a 15-day extension, halved the pre-deposit for DRAT appeals from 75% to 50%, prescribed a sale-proceeds waterfall for secured assets, made the recovery certificate a deemed decree for winding-up purposes, introduced electronic filing under section 19A, and crowned the system with section 31B, granting registered secured creditors statutory priority over Government dues.

2019
The bankruptcy avatar

With effect from 1 December 2019, by reason of section 249 read with the Fifth Schedule to the IBC, the Act was renamed the Recovery of Debts and Bankruptcy Act, 1993. The DRT was simultaneously empowered as the Adjudicating Authority for individual and partnership insolvency under Part III of the IBC, beginning with personal guarantors to corporate debtors. The DRAT became the appellate forum for these orders. The RDB Act thereby outgrew its original mandate.

Note on nomenclature

Practitioners and older case law still use "RDDBFI Act" or "DRT Act." After 1 December 2019 the correct statutory style is "Recovery of Debts and Bankruptcy Act, 1993," abbreviated "RDB Act." The Act bears two faces today, an adjudicatory recovery code for banks and financial institutions, and an adjudicating-authority charter for individual insolvency under the IBC.

Part II

Object & architecture: a specialised forum, a streamlined procedure

Three ideas hold the Act together. Specialised adjudication, by tribunals staffed with persons of judicial rank. Streamlined procedure, free of the encumbrances of the Code of Civil Procedure but disciplined by natural justice. Exclusive jurisdiction, with the civil court barred from entertaining matters falling within the DRT's authority.

Pillar 1 Specialised adjudication

The Presiding Officer of a DRT is a person who is, has been, or is qualified to be a District Judge (section 5). The Chairperson of a DRAT is a person who is, has been, or is qualified to be a Judge of a High Court, or a senior member of the Indian Legal Service, or a former DRT Presiding Officer of at least three years' standing (section 10). The forum is therefore not a creature of executive convenience; it is a judicial body staffed by persons of judicial rank.

Pillar 2 Streamlined procedure

Section 22 expressly frees the DRT and DRAT from the procedural shackles of the Code of Civil Procedure, while binding them to the principles of natural justice. The Tribunals retain the substantive powers of a civil court for the limited purposes of summoning witnesses, requiring discovery, receiving affidavit evidence, issuing commissions, reviewing decisions, and dismissing or restoring applications. The result is a forum that can move at the pace of commercial dispute resolution rather than civil litigation.

Pillar 3 Exclusive jurisdiction

Section 18 bars all courts and authorities, except the Supreme Court and a High Court under Articles 226 and 227, from exercising jurisdiction in relation to matters specified in section 17. Section 34 reinforces this by giving the Act overriding effect over inconsistent laws. Together they channel every bank-recovery dispute into the DRT.

Part III

Applicability, extent & the pecuniary threshold

The Act is national in extent and threshold-bound in jurisdiction. Section 1(4) is the gate, and the figure it speaks of has been raised by notification more than once.

s. 1(2) Extent

The Act extends to the whole of India. The original exclusion of the State of Jammu and Kashmir was removed by the Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation (Adaptation of Central Laws) notifications dated 30 October 2019, so the Act now applies to the Union territories of Jammu and Kashmir and Ladakh as well.

s. 1(3) Commencement

Deemed to have come into force on 24 June 1993, the date the original Ordinance was promulgated.

s. 1(4) The pecuniary threshold

The Act applies where the amount of debt due to a bank, financial institution, or consortium is not less than ten lakh rupees, or such other amount, being not less than one lakh rupees, as the Central Government may, by notification, specify. By notification dated 6 September 2018, the Central Government raised the threshold to twenty lakh rupees. Below that figure, the DRT route is unavailable and the bank must proceed by an ordinary civil suit.

Pitfall — the threshold is jurisdictional

A claim below ₹20 lakh is not within the DRT's jurisdiction at all. The Tribunal cannot enlarge its jurisdiction by consent. A bank that mistakenly files below the threshold will see the application returned for presentation to the proper civil court, with limitation continuing to run in the meantime. Always pleaded the principal figure carefully, exclusive of penal interest, since the threshold is on "debt" as defined.

Part IV · Section 2

The defined terms: the doors of jurisdiction

Two definitions do most of the work in RDB Act practice. The breadth of "debt" decides what the DRT can entertain, and the meaning of "bank" decides who can file. Everything else is procedural detail.

Bank
s.2(d)

A banking company, a corresponding new bank, the State Bank of India, a subsidiary bank, a Regional Rural Bank, or a multi-state cooperative bank. The last category was added by Act 1 of 2013; their inclusion is now constitutionally settled by Pandurang Ganpati Chaugule (2020).

Debt
s.2(g)

Any liability inclusive of interest claimed as due from any person by a bank, FI or consortium during the course of business, whether secured or unsecured, assigned, payable under decree or arbitral award, or under a mortgage, and subsisting on and legally recoverable on the date of application. Post-2016 it extends to unpaid debt securities after a 90-day notice by the debenture trustee.

Financial institution
s.2(h)

A public financial institution under section 4A of the Companies Act 1956; an ARC registered under section 3 SARFAESI (post-2004); a Board-registered debenture trustee for secured debt securities (post-2016); or such other institution as the Central Government may specify.

Property
s.2(jb)

Inserted in 2016. Immovable property, movable property, any debt or right to receive payment of money (secured or unsecured), receivables (existing or future), and intangible assets (know-how, patent, copyright, trade mark, licence, franchise) of similar commercial nature.

Secured creditor & security interest
s.2(la), 2(lb)

Both inserted in 2016. Borrowed wholesale from section 2(zd) and section 2(zf) of SARFAESI, harmonising the two statutes on what counts as a security interest and who counts as a secured creditor.

Recovery Officer
s.2(k)

An officer appointed by the Central Government for each Tribunal under section 7(1). Distinct from the Presiding Officer. The Presiding Officer adjudicates; the Recovery Officer executes.

Presiding Officer
s.2(ja)

The single judicial member who constitutes a DRT under section 4(1). Must be qualified to be a District Judge.

Chairperson
s.2(ea)

The single judicial member who constitutes a DRAT under section 9. Must be qualified to be a Judge of a High Court, or otherwise eligible under section 10.

Tribunal & Appellate Tribunal
s.2(o), 2(a)

The DRT established under section 3 and the DRAT established under section 8 respectively.

Practitioner's note — the breadth of "debt"

The definition of debt in section 2(g) is one of the widest in Indian commercial law. It is not limited to a contractual loan. It captures liability under a guarantee, an arbitral award, a decree of any civil court, and an unpaid mortgage. The Supreme Court has read this definition expansively to bring within the DRT's jurisdiction even claims arising from foreign-exchange transactions, indemnity claims, and bill-discounting disputes. The first question in any RDB Act analysis is not whether there is a contract, but whether the claim sounds in debt as so defined.

Part V · Chapter II (ss. 3–16)

The Tribunals: DRT and DRAT, end to end

Chapter II establishes the forum and staffs it. It is the constitutional plumbing of the Act. The substantive movement comes later in Chapter IV.

s. 3 Establishment of the Tribunal

The Central Government, by notification, establishes one or more DRTs and specifies the territorial jurisdiction of each. Post-2019, sub-section (1A) clarifies that DRTs also exercise the jurisdiction of the Adjudicating Authority under Part III IBC.

s. 4 Composition

A DRT consists of one person only, the Presiding Officer, appointed by notification. Sub-section (2), substituted in 2016, allows the Central Government to authorise a Presiding Officer (or a judicial member) of another Tribunal to discharge DRT functions concurrently, addressing the chronic problem of vacancies.

s. 5 Qualifications for Presiding Officer

A person is qualified if he is, has been, or is qualified to be, a District Judge.

s. 6 / 6A Term of office

Post-2016, the term of office is five years from the date of entry, with eligibility for reappointment, subject to a retirement age of 65 years (substituted by Act 44 of 2016). Section 6A, inserted by the Finance Act 2017 and subsequently substituted by the Tribunal Reforms Act 2021, now governs the qualifications, appointment, salaries, allowances, resignation and removal of all Presiding Officers appointed after the commencement of that Act, by reference to Chapter II of the Tribunal Reforms Act 2021.

s. 7 Staff and the Recovery Officer

Each DRT is provided with one or more Recovery Officers and such other staff as the Central Government thinks fit, all under the general superintendence of the Presiding Officer. The Recovery Officer is the key executive arm.

s. 8 Establishment of the Appellate Tribunal

The Central Government establishes one or more DRATs. Since 2016, the Chairperson of one Appellate Tribunal can be authorised to discharge the functions of another (sub-section 3), again to manage vacancies. Sub-section (1A), inserted by the IBC, enables the DRAT to entertain appeals against the Adjudicating Authority's orders under Part III IBC.

s. 9 / 10 Composition and qualifications of the DRAT Chairperson

A DRAT consists of one person only, the Chairperson. He must be, have been, or be qualified to be, a Judge of a High Court, or a member of the Indian Legal Service who has held a Grade I post for at least three years, or a Presiding Officer of a DRT for at least three years.

s. 11 / 15A Term of office

Post-2016, the term is five years, with eligibility for reappointment, subject to a retirement age of 70 years. Section 15A mirrors section 6A for the Chairperson and now refers to the Tribunal Reforms Act 2021.

ss. 13–16 Service conditions, vacancies, resignation, removal

Salaries and allowances are prescribed by rules and may not be varied to the incumbent's disadvantage. Removal is only by order of the Central Government on the ground of proved misbehaviour or incapacity, after inquiry by a sitting Judge (of the High Court for a Presiding Officer; of the Supreme Court for a Chairperson), with a reasonable opportunity of being heard. Section 16 protects acts and proceedings from being called into question merely on grounds of defect in constitution.

Amendment watch — the Tribunal Reforms Act 2021

Since 4 April 2021, all appointments to the DRT and DRAT, including qualifications, term, salaries, allowances, resignation and removal, are governed by Chapter II of the Tribunal Reforms Act 2021, not by sections 6 and 11 of the RDB Act, by reason of sections 6A and 15A. Officers appointed before the commencement of Part XIV of Chapter VI of the Finance Act 2017 continue under the older regime.

Part VI · Chapter III (ss. 17–18)

Jurisdiction, powers and authority: what the DRT decides and who else cannot

s. 17 The DRT's jurisdiction

Sub-section (1) gives the DRT, from the appointed day, the jurisdiction, powers and authority to entertain and decide applications from banks and financial institutions for recovery of debts due to them. Sub-section (1A), inserted in 2019, extends the DRT's jurisdiction to applications under Part III of the IBC, and authorises circuit sittings at district headquarters. Sub-section (2) confers appellate jurisdiction on the DRAT against DRT orders, and sub-section (2A) similarly extends appellate jurisdiction over Part III IBC orders.

s. 17A Power of the DRAT Chairperson

Inserted by Act 1 of 2000 and reinforced by Act 44 of 2016. The Chairperson exercises general superintendence and control over DRTs within his jurisdiction. He may direct DRTs to furnish information on pendency under both the RDB Act and SARFAESI, convene meetings of Presiding Officers to review performance, and recommend inquiry against a Presiding Officer for misbehaviour or incapacity. Importantly, sub-section (2) confers on the Chairperson the power to transfer cases from one DRT to another within his jurisdiction, on application or suo motu.

Case law — only the Chairperson can transfer

In Eskays Construction Pvt. Ltd. v. Soma Papers and Industries Ltd. (2016) and the line of authority following it, the High Courts have held that they cannot transfer a case from one DRT to another. Section 17A vests that power exclusively in the DRAT Chairperson. A writ petition seeking transfer is liable to be dismissed on the alternate-remedy ground.

s. 18 Bar of jurisdiction

From the appointed day, no court or other authority has jurisdiction over the matters specified in section 17, except the Supreme Court and a High Court exercising jurisdiction under Articles 226 and 227. The bar is comprehensive, even excluding civil courts that may have entertained a matter before establishment of the DRT (subject to the savings in section 31). The proviso preserves, on a transitional basis, certain proceedings of multi-state cooperative banks pending before the 2013 amendment.

Case law — Allahabad Bank v. Canara Bank

In Allahabad Bank v. Canara Bank (2000) 4 SCC 406, the Supreme Court held that, in matters of priorities between banks over the same secured assets, the DRT has exclusive jurisdiction. The civil court is excluded even where the question is one of competing claims rather than recovery from the borrower. The decision confirms the wide sweep of section 18.

Part VII · Chapter IV (ss. 19–24)

Procedure: the section 19 machine

Section 19 is the operative heart of the Act. Substantially rewritten in 2016, it now reads like a complete civil-procedure code in miniature, covering jurisdiction, summons, written statement, set-off, counter-claim, attachment before judgment, the receiver, the final order, the waterfall, and the recovery certificate.

s. 19(1) Where to file

An application may be filed before the DRT within whose local limits any one of the following is situated: (a) the branch or any other office of the bank or FI maintaining the account in which the debt claimed is outstanding (added in 2016, this is now usually the most convenient ground); (aa) the place where the defendant actually and voluntarily resides, carries on business, or personally works for gain; (b) similarly, the place of any one of multiple defendants; or (c) the place where the cause of action arises wholly or in part. The provisos permit withdrawal with DRT permission to invoke SARFAESI instead, to be disposed of within 30 days.

s. 19(1A) and (1B) The multi-state cooperative bank option

A multi-state cooperative bank may opt to recover under the Multi-State Co-operative Societies Act 2002 instead of approaching the DRT. If it has already filed before the DRT, it may withdraw with permission to switch to that route. The election is the bank's, not the borrower's.

s. 19(2) Joinder

Where one bank has filed against a defendant and another bank or FI has a claim against the same defendant, the second bank may join the first at any stage before final order. The DRT becomes a forum for consolidated recovery.

s. 19(3) Form, fee, and document discipline

Every application must be in the prescribed form, accompanied by true copies of all documents relied on, and the prescribed fee. The Explanation, inserted in 2016, clarifies that "documents" includes statements of account or any entry in banker's books duly certified under the Bankers' Books Evidence Act, 1891. This codifies the practice of relying on certified bank statements as primary evidence.

s. 19(3A) Disclosure of the security pool (inserted 2016)

The applicant must state the particulars of debt secured by security interest, the estimated value of those securities, and where insufficient, the particulars of other properties owned by the defendants. If even those are insufficient, the applicant may seek an order directing the defendant to disclose particulars of other assets. This is the discovery engine of the modern RDB Act.

s. 19(4) and (4A) Summons and the asset freeze

On receipt of the application, the DRT issues summons directing the defendant: (i) to show cause within 30 days why the relief should not be granted, (ii) to disclose particulars of properties other than those identified by the applicant, and (iii) to restrain him from dealing with or disposing of disclosed assets pending hearing. Sub-section (4A), inserted in 2016, overrides section 65A of the Transfer of Property Act and forbids the defendant from transferring secured or disclosed assets by sale, lease or otherwise (except in the ordinary course of business) without prior DRT approval.

s. 19(5) Written statement and the 30+15 day clock

The defendant must file his written statement within 30 days of service, including any claim of set-off or counter-claim, accompanied by original or true-copy documents relied on. In exceptional and special circumstances recorded in writing, the Presiding Officer may extend the period by a further 15 days, but no more. Non-disclosure under an order entitles the DRT to detain the defaulting officer in civil prison for up to three months, subject to a hearing.

s. 19(5A) and (5B) Admission and partial decree (inserted 2016)

On filing of the written statement, or expiry of the period, the DRT fixes a date for admission or denial of documents and for continuation or vacation of the interim order. Where the defendant admits the debt in full or in part, the DRT orders payment of the admitted amount within 30 days; failure to comply entitles the DRT to issue a recovery certificate to the extent of the admission. This is the DRT's analogue of a judgment-on-admission.

s. 19(6)–(11) Set-off and counter-claim

The defendant may plead set-off of any ascertained sum legally recoverable by him from the applicant. He may also set up a counter-claim in respect of any cause of action accruing before he has delivered his defence, including a claim for damages. A counter-claim has the effect of a cross-suit. Where the applicant contends that the counter-claim ought not to be disposed of as a counter-claim but in an independent action, the DRT decides the issue alongside the recovery application (sub-section 11, substituted 2016).

Case law — counter-claim before the DRT

In Indian Bank v. ABS Marine Products (P) Ltd. (2006) 5 SCC 72, the Supreme Court held that a defendant in a DRT proceeding cannot transfer his counter-claim to the civil court; the DRT is the only forum that can adjudicate the counter-claim along with the bank's recovery application. The decision protects the integrity of the DRT's exclusive jurisdiction even where the counter-claim is large.

s. 19(10A) and (10B) Affidavit verification (inserted 2016)

Every application, written statement, set-off, counter-claim, reply or pleading must be supported by an affidavit verifying all facts and pleadings. Witness affidavits must be filed simultaneously. Non-compliant facts cannot be relied on as evidence. This is the procedural innovation that aligns RDB practice with the affidavit-driven model of commercial arbitration.

s. 19(13)–(18) Attachment before judgment and the receiver

At any stage, on the applicant's application disclosing particulars and estimated value of the property to be attached, or otherwise being satisfied, the DRT may direct the defendant to furnish security where he is about to dispose of property, remove it from jurisdiction, or cause damage to it (sub-section 13). Where the defendant fails to show cause or furnish security, the DRT may attach the whole or sufficient part of the property. Sub-section (15) permits conditional attachment, sub-section (16) voids any attachment made without compliance with sub-section (13), and sub-section (17) permits civil-prison detention for up to three months for disobedience. Sub-section (18) empowers the DRT to appoint a receiver, with the full range of powers to bring and defend suits, collect rents and profits, and execute documents.

s. 19(19) and (20AB) The waterfall in winding-up and otherwise

Where a recovery certificate is issued against a company in liquidation, the DRT directs distribution in the manner provided in section 326 of the Companies Act, 2013 (formerly section 529A of the 1956 Act), preserving the workmen's-dues priority. Sub-section (20AB), inserted in 2016, prescribes a waterfall for sale of secured assets in all other cases: (i) preservation, valuation, public-notice, auction and other sale-related costs paid in full, and (ii) the bank's debts. The Explanation makes the waterfall subject to the IBC where insolvency proceedings are pending.

s. 19(20) The final order

The DRT, after giving both sides an opportunity of being heard on the claim, set-off, counter-claim and interest, must pass an interim or final order within 30 days from the conclusion of hearings. The order may include payment of interest from the due date until realisation or actual payment.

s. 19(22) and (22A) The recovery certificate and the bridge to insolvency

The Presiding Officer issues a recovery certificate, signed by him, to the Recovery Officer, specifying the amount of debt for recovery. Sub-section (22A), inserted in 2016, gives the certificate a deemed-decree status for the purposes of (i) initiating winding-up under the Companies Act 2013, (ii) proceeding against an LLP under the LLP Act 2008, and (iii) initiating insolvency proceedings against any individual or partnership firm under any law in force.

Case law — recovery certificate as a fresh cause of action for CIRP

In Kotak Mahindra Bank Ltd. v. A. Balakrishnan (2022) 9 SCC 186, the Supreme Court held that a recovery certificate issued by the DRT under section 19(22) gives rise to a fresh cause of action, with its own limitation, for initiating CIRP under section 7 of the IBC. Earlier in Dena Bank v. C. Shivakumar Reddy (2021) 10 SCC 330, the Court had similarly held that a recovery certificate is a "financial debt" and the holder a "financial creditor" for IBC purposes. The two decisions converted the recovery certificate from a domestic execution document into a bridge to the insolvency regime.

s. 19(24) The 180-day target

The application must be dealt with as expeditiously as possible. Post-2016, "every effort shall be made to complete the proceedings in two hearings" and to dispose of the application finally within 180 days from the date of receipt. The targets are directory rather than mandatory in practice, but they discipline the conduct of the proceedings.

s. 19A Electronic filing

Inserted by Act 44 of 2016. The Central Government may by rules notify Tribunals before which applications, written statements and documents must be filed in electronic form, authenticated by digital signature. Orders displayed on the Tribunal's website are deemed public notice, and electronic transmission of orders is deemed service. The e-DRT framework has been rolled out progressively since 2017.

s. 20 Appeal to the DRAT

Any person aggrieved by a DRT order may appeal to the DRAT within 30 days (reduced from 45 by Act 44 of 2016). No appeal lies from a consent order. The DRAT may, on sufficient cause, condone delay. The appeal must be disposed of, as far as possible, within six months.

s. 21 The 50% pre-deposit

Where the appeal is preferred by the borrower, it shall not be entertained unless he deposits with the DRAT 50% of the amount of debt determined by the DRT under section 19 (reduced from 75% by Act 44 of 2016). The DRAT may, for recorded reasons, reduce the deposit, but not below 25%. The pre-deposit cannot be entirely waived. This deposit is the borrower's gateway to appellate review.

s. 22 Procedure and powers

The Tribunals are not bound by the CPC but are guided by natural justice. They have civil-court powers in respect of summoning, discovery, affidavits, commissions, review, dismissal for default, and restoration. Proceedings are deemed judicial proceedings under the IPC, and the Tribunals are deemed civil courts for the limited purposes of section 195 CrPC and Chapter XXVI of the CrPC. Sub-section (4), inserted in 2016, makes the Bankers' Books Evidence Act, 1891 applicable to all proceedings.

s. 22A Uniform procedure

Inserted in 2016. The Central Government may prescribe a uniform procedure for proceedings before all DRTs and DRATs, an attempt to address the inconsistency in practice across tribunals.

s. 23 Right of representation

A bank may authorise one or more legal practitioners or officers to act as Presenting Officers. The defendant may appear in person or through similar authorisation. The Act recognises a non-advocate Presenting Officer model, distinguishing it from ordinary civil litigation.

s. 24 Limitation

The provisions of the Limitation Act, 1963 apply, as far as may be, to an application made to the DRT. The applicable article is Article 137 (three years, "any other application"). Acknowledgements under sections 18 and 19 of the Limitation Act extend the period.

Part VIII · Chapter V (ss. 25–30A)

Recovery: the Recovery Officer's arsenal

A judgment without execution is paper. Chapter V puts execution in the hands of the Recovery Officer, with powers borrowed wholesale from the Income Tax Act's recovery code. The Recovery Officer is the practical recovery engine.

s. 25 Modes of recovery

On receipt of the certificate, the Recovery Officer may proceed by one or more of: (a) attachment and sale of the defendant's movable or immovable property; (aa) taking possession of property over which security interest is created and appointing a receiver to sell (added in 2016, an internalised SARFAESI-style power); (b) arrest and detention of the defendant in civil prison; (c) appointing a receiver for management of the defendant's properties; and (d) any other mode prescribed by the Central Government.

s. 26 Validity of the certificate

The defendant cannot dispute before the Recovery Officer the correctness of the amount in the certificate, and no objection on any other ground is entertained. The Presiding Officer alone can withdraw the certificate or correct clerical or arithmetical mistakes, with intimation to the Recovery Officer.

s. 27 Stay, instalment, and forfeiture of appeal right

Post-2016 substitution. The Presiding Officer may grant time to pay only if the defendant makes a down payment of not less than 25% of the amount specified in the recovery certificate and gives an unconditional undertaking to pay the balance in a reasonable time acceptable to the bank. Once the Recovery Officer stays proceedings on such order, the defendant forfeits his right to appeal against the DRT order (sub-section 1B). Default in instalment payments lifts the stay automatically and the Recovery Officer resumes recovery (sub-section 1C). This is a strong inducement for honest defendants and a sharp penalty for tactical ones.

s. 28 Other modes including garnishee

The Recovery Officer may issue garnishee notices to any person from whom money is due, or may become due, to the defendant. The Officer may attach amounts in joint accounts (presuming equal shares), apply to any court holding the defendant's money for payment over, and resort to distraint and sale of movable property in the manner of the Third Schedule to the Income Tax Act, 1961. A garnishee who fails to comply becomes himself a defendant in default for the amount notified.

s. 29 Income Tax Act application

The Second and Third Schedules to the Income Tax Act, 1961 and the Income Tax (Certificate Proceedings) Rules, 1962 apply, as far as possible, to recovery under the Act, with necessary modifications and with the defendant treated as the assessee. This imports the full machinery of attachment, proclamation, sale, and rateable distribution.

s. 30 Appeal against the Recovery Officer's order

Any person aggrieved by an order of the Recovery Officer may, within 30 days, appeal to the DRT. The DRT may confirm, modify or set aside.

s. 30A The 50% deposit for Recovery Officer appeals (inserted 2016)

Where the appeal under section 30 is preferred by the person from whom debt is due, the DRT shall not entertain the appeal unless the appellant has deposited 50% of the debt as determined by the DRT. There is no power to reduce. This is a second pre-deposit, stricter than section 21.

Pitfall — double pre-deposit risk

A borrower who loses at the Recovery Officer's stage faces two distinct deposit gates. First, an appeal against the substantive DRT order to the DRAT requires 50% under section 21 (reducible to 25%). Then, any appeal against the Recovery Officer's execution order to the DRT requires another 50% under section 30A, not reducible. Counsel for borrowers should factor both gates into the litigation strategy from the outset.

Part IX · Chapter VI (ss. 31–37)

Miscellaneous: priority, override, and transition

s. 31 Transfer of pending cases

Every suit or proceeding pending before any court before establishment of a DRT, if the cause of action would have fallen within the DRT's jurisdiction, stands transferred to the DRT. Appeals are excluded. The provisos preserve, transitionally, certain multi-state cooperative bank proceedings under the 2002 Act.

s. 31A Recovery certificate in case of decree (inserted 2000)

Where a decree was passed before the commencement of the 2000 Amendment Act and has not been executed, the decree-holder may apply to the DRT for an order of recovery. The DRT issues a certificate to the Recovery Officer, who proceeds as if it were a domestic certificate.

s. 31B Priority to secured creditors (inserted 2016)

The mirror of section 26E SARFAESI. Notwithstanding anything contained in any other law for the time being in force, the rights of secured creditors to realise secured debts by sale of assets over which security interest is created shall have priority and shall be paid in priority over all other debts and Government dues, including revenues, taxes, cesses and rates payable to the Central Government, State Government or local authority. The Explanation expressly subordinates the priority to the IBC where insolvency proceedings are pending.

⇄ Cross-reference — s.31B RDB vs s.26E SARFAESI

Section 31B RDB and section 26E SARFAESI together reverse the older common-law primacy of State and Crown dues. The two provisions operate together. Section 26E applies to enforcement by secured creditors under SARFAESI; section 31B applies to recovery through the DRT. The trigger for section 26E is registration of the security interest with CERSAI; section 31B has no such precondition but its priority is again subject to the IBC waterfall in section 53 where insolvency proceedings are pending.

s. 32 Public servants

The Chairperson, Presiding Officer, Recovery Officer and other officers and employees of the Tribunals are deemed public servants under section 21 of the Indian Penal Code.

s. 33 Protection of good-faith action

No suit, prosecution or other legal proceeding lies against the Central Government, the Presiding Officer, the Chairperson, or the Recovery Officer for anything done or intended to be done in good faith under the Act.

s. 34 Overriding effect

The provisions of the Act have effect notwithstanding anything inconsistent in any other law for the time being in force. Sub-section (2) makes the Act in addition to and not in derogation of the IFCI Act 1948, the SFC Act 1951, the UTI Act 1963, the IRBI Act 1984, the Sick Industrial Companies (Special Provisions) Act 1985 and the SIDBI Act 1989.

ss. 35–37 Removal of difficulties, rule-making, repeal

Section 35 empowers the Central Government to remove difficulties within three years of commencement. Section 36 confers rule-making power, including post-2016 powers on uniform procedure (clause da), modes of recovery (clause db) and e-filing (clauses ccc and ccd). Section 37 repealed the 1993 Ordinance and saved everything done under it.

Part X

The Adjudicating Authority avatar: the DRT under Part III of the IBC

Since 1 December 2019, the DRT wears a second hat. By virtue of section 179 of the IBC, the DRT is the Adjudicating Authority for insolvency resolution of individuals and partnership firms under Part III. The DRAT is the appellate forum. The rollout has been staggered, beginning with personal guarantors to corporate debtors.

The IBC's Fifth Schedule simultaneously did three things to the RDB Act. It renamed the Act to its present style. It inserted sub-sections (1A) and (1B) in section 8 and sub-sections (1A) and (2A) in section 17, conferring the new jurisdiction. And it added a stub section 19A specific to Part III IBC, providing that the application made to the DRT under the IBC is to be dealt with in the manner provided under that Code, not the procedure in section 19 of the RDB Act.

For practitioners, the practical effect is that the DRT now decides three categories of matter: (i) recovery applications by banks and FIs under section 19 RDB Act; (ii) SARFAESI grievances under section 17 SARFAESI; and (iii) insolvency applications under Part III IBC. Each category follows its own procedural code. A single Presiding Officer juggles three statutes.

Amendment watch — phased commencement

The Part III IBC jurisdiction has been notified in tranches. Personal guarantors to corporate debtors were notified with effect from 1 December 2019. Other individuals and partnership firms remain subject to a separate notification. Until the relevant tranche is notified, the older Presidency-Towns Insolvency Act 1909 and the Provincial Insolvency Act 1920 continue to apply. Check the latest notification before filing.

Part XII

Landmark jurisprudence the cases that decide DRT practice

Union of India v. Delhi High Court Bar Association
(2002) 4 SCC 275

The cornerstone constitutional decision. The Supreme Court upheld the validity of the RDB Act as amended in 2000, holding that Parliament could lawfully create a specialised tribunal with exclusive jurisdiction over bank-recovery matters. It set aside the earlier Delhi High Court ruling that had struck down provisions of the un-amended Act.

Allahabad Bank v. Canara Bank
(2000) 4 SCC 406

Established the wide sweep of section 18. The DRT has exclusive jurisdiction even in inter-bank priority disputes over secured assets, to the exclusion of the civil court.

Indian Bank v. ABS Marine Products
(2006) 5 SCC 72

A counter-claim by a defendant before the DRT cannot be transferred out to the civil court. The DRT alone can adjudicate the counter-claim alongside the bank's recovery application.

Industrial Investment Bank of India v. Marshal's Power and Telecom
(2007) 1 SCC 106

Read section 19(8) liberally on the scope of permissible counter-claims; a counter-claim can be set up against any of the defendant's rights as against the applicant bank.

Standard Chartered Bank v. Dharminder Bhohi
(2013) 15 SCC 341

Clarified the relative powers of the Presiding Officer and the Recovery Officer. The Recovery Officer cannot reopen the merits of the certificate; his jurisdiction is execution alone, and section 26 bars merit-stage objections at his level.

Eskays Construction v. Soma Papers
(2016) 11 SCC 357

Only the Chairperson of the DRAT can transfer a case from one DRT to another under section 17A(2). The High Court has no such transfer power.

Transcore v. Union of India
(2008) 1 SCC 125

The pivotal cross-statute decision. A pending OA before the DRT is no bar to invoking SARFAESI; the two remedies are complementary, not alternative. Section 37 SARFAESI is the textual anchor.

Dena Bank v. C. Shivakumar Reddy
(2021) 10 SCC 330

A recovery certificate is a "financial debt" and its holder a "financial creditor" under the IBC. The decision is the first step in the recovery-certificate-to-CIRP bridge.

Kotak Mahindra Bank v. A. Balakrishnan
(2022) 9 SCC 186

A recovery certificate gives rise to a fresh cause of action with its own limitation, sufficient to found a CIRP application under section 7 IBC. This is the most important recent decision linking the RDB Act to the IBC.

Bank of Rajasthan v. VCK Shares
(2022) SCC OnLine SC 1340

Reaffirmed that the DRT's jurisdiction under section 19 extends to any claim sounding in debt within the wide section 2(g) definition, including claims relating to securities transactions financed by the bank.

United Bank of India v. Satyawati Tondon
(2010) 8 SCC 110

Though primarily a SARFAESI decision, this case applies equally to the RDB Act. High Courts should be reluctant to entertain writ petitions against DRT orders where the statutory appellate remedy under section 20 is available.

Pandurang Ganpati Chaugule v. Vishwasrao Patil Murgud Sah. Bank
(2020) 9 SCC 215

Constitution Bench. Multi-state cooperative banks are "banks" for both the RDB Act and SARFAESI. Settles a long jurisdictional debate at the intersection of cooperative and banking law.

Part XIII · The decision framework

SARFAESI vs RDB vs IBC: when to invoke which remedy

This is the question that every counsel acting for a stressed lender must answer at the outset, and the answer determines the entire litigation strategy. The three statutes are not interchangeable. Each has a specific shape, a specific threshold, a specific forum, and a specific kind of outcome.

Regime 1

SARFAESI

Self-help enforcement of a security interest, without court intervention to start.

Nature
Enforcement of a specific security by the creditor itself
Who may use it
Secured creditors only: banks, FIs, ARCs, qualifying debenture trustees
Threshold
Secured debt over ₹1 lakh; dues over 20% of principal plus interest
Trigger
NPA classification plus a Section 13(2) notice
Forum
The creditor itself, with CMM or DM assistance under Section 14. Borrower's recourse is to the DRT under Section 17, with appeal to the DRAT
What you get
Possession and sale of the specific collateral. Not a personal money decree
Speed
Fastest. No court involvement to commence. Possession often within 60 to 120 days of notice if uncontested
Limits
Cannot reach unsecured assets. Excluded from agricultural land, pledges, aircraft, vessels. CERSAI registration required to enforce
Regime 2

RDB Act

Adjudicatory recovery of debt by tribunal, with a money decree and execution machinery.

Nature
Specialised adjudication, money decree, and execution against any property of the debtor
Who may use it
Banks and financial institutions (including ARCs, debenture trustees). Other creditors cannot file
Threshold
Debt of ₹20 lakh or more (current notification under section 1(4))
Trigger
Liability sounding in "debt" under section 2(g). Wide definition, includes secured, unsecured, decretal, guarantee-based
Forum
DRT having jurisdiction (branch maintaining account, defendant's residence/business, or cause of action). Appeal to DRAT with 50% pre-deposit, reducible to 25%
What you get
A recovery certificate, which is a deemed decree for winding-up, LLP and individual insolvency purposes. Executable against all the debtor's property
Speed
Target 180 days from receipt of application. In practice, slower, but faster than civil suit
Limits
Below ₹20 lakh, the DRT is closed. Above, the DRT is the only forum
Regime 3

IBC

Collective insolvency resolution or liquidation of the entire entity, with a moratorium.

Nature
Collective insolvency resolution or liquidation of the whole entity, not individual creditor enforcement
Who may use it
Financial creditor, operational creditor, or the corporate debtor itself (for CIRP). Creditors and debtors for personal insolvency
Threshold
For CIRP: default of ₹1 crore (raised from ₹1 lakh in March 2020). Lower thresholds for personal insolvency
Trigger
A default on a financial or operational debt
Forum
NCLT for corporates, DRT for individuals and partnership firms (Part III). NCLAT and DRAT respectively on appeal
What you get
A resolution plan binding on all stakeholders (Section 31 IBC), or liquidation with distribution under the Section 53 waterfall
Speed
Strict: CIRP 180 days, extendable to 270, with an outer limit of 330 days
Limits
Cannot be used for individual creditor enforcement. Once admitted, moratorium under Section 14 freezes all SARFAESI and RDB action
The practitioner's decision tree

Q1. Are you a secured creditor with a registered security interest in non-excluded collateral, and is the account NPA?

Yes: SARFAESI is the fastest route. Invoke Section 13(2) immediately. The DRT is the borrower's forum, not yours. No: proceed to Q2.

Q2. Are you a bank or financial institution with a claim sounding in debt of ₹20 lakh or more, and do you need an adjudicated money decree or to reach unsecured assets?

Yes: file an Original Application under Section 19 RDB Act. The DRT is your forum, and you obtain a recovery certificate executable across the debtor's estate. No: proceed to Q3.

Q3. Is the debtor a corporate, with a default of ₹1 crore or more, and is the business potentially viable (resolution preferable to liquidation), or do you wish to invoke a moratorium and a collective process?

Yes: the IBC is your forum. File a Section 7 application before the NCLT (financial creditor) or Section 9 (operational creditor). Admission triggers the Section 14 moratorium and the collective process. No: if the debtor is an individual or partnership firm and the default is significant, Part III IBC may be available before the DRT.

Q4. Are you below all of these thresholds, or is your claim outside "debt" under section 2(g)?

Then: the ordinary civil suit, under the Code of Civil Procedure before the appropriate civil court, remains the only route.

Coordination The three remedies in combination

The three regimes are not mutually exclusive. In a typical large-exposure recovery scenario, all three operate, sequentially or in parallel. The practitioner's task is to coordinate them.

SARFAESI plus RDB Act

Per Transcore v. Union of India (2008), a bank may pursue both remedies simultaneously. Section 37 SARFAESI provides that the Act is in addition to and not in derogation of the RDB Act. The standard playbook is to invoke SARFAESI for speed against the specific collateral, while keeping the RDB Act application alive for the personal claim, guarantor liability, and any shortfall under section 13(10) SARFAESI. Recovery in one forum is adjusted against the other; double recovery is impermissible.

RDB Act feeding into the IBC

Per Dena Bank v. C. Shivakumar Reddy (2021) and Kotak Mahindra Bank v. A. Balakrishnan (2022), a recovery certificate is a financial debt and its holder a financial creditor. The certificate furnishes a fresh cause of action with its own limitation, sufficient to support a Section 7 IBC application. Counsel should think of the recovery certificate as a passport, not a terminus.

The IBC's primacy on admission

Once CIRP is admitted, Section 14 IBC freezes all SARFAESI and RDB enforcement. Section 238 IBC overrides Section 35 SARFAESI and Section 34 RDB. The priority under Section 26E SARFAESI and Section 31B RDB is itself made subject to the IBC waterfall in Section 53 where insolvency proceedings are pending. A creditor whose enforcement is in mid-flight must down tools, lodge its claim with the resolution professional, and participate in the collective process.

⇄ Cross-reference — the choice in seven words

SARFAESI for the asset. RDB for the decree. IBC for the entity. SARFAESI realises specific collateral. The RDB Act establishes liability and produces a portable certificate. The IBC reorganises or liquidates the whole entity. Each is reaching for a different prize.

Part XIV

The tactical playbook: a sequenced approach

Doctrine becomes practice once you can see the sequence. Here is how a counsel for a secured lender typically sequences the three statutes from default to recovery.

NPA
Step 0 · Classification and CERSAI verification

Confirm the NPA classification under RBI norms. Verify that the security interest is registered with CERSAI under Section 26B SARFAESI. Without registration, SARFAESI enforcement is barred.

13(2)
Step 1 · Issue the SARFAESI demand notice SARFAESI s.13(2)

Start the SARFAESI clock. The 60-day period is the fastest path to possession of the specific collateral. Concurrently, prepare the RDB Act application.

OA
Step 2 · File the Original Application under section 19 RDB Act

Sue for the entire debt, exclusive of penal interest. Plead all security particulars under Section 19(3A) and seek attachment-before-judgment under Section 19(13). The RDB Act application protects the unsecured portion, the guarantor exposure, and the future shortfall.

14
Step 3 · Take possession; if resisted, Section 14 SARFAESI to the CMM/DM

The CMM or DM must order possession within 30 days, extendable to 60. This proceeds in parallel with the RDB Act adjudication.

SALE
Step 4 · Sale of the secured asset; apply proceeds

Conduct the sale under the Enforcement Rules. Apply proceeds to costs, then dues. Any shortfall feeds back into the RDB Act application under Section 13(10) SARFAESI.

RC
Step 5 · Recovery Certificate from the DRT RDB s.19(22)

Obtain the recovery certificate from the Presiding Officer. The Recovery Officer proceeds with attachment, sale, garnishee, and Income Tax Act machinery against all the debtor's other property.

IBC
Step 6 · Strategic IBC filing

If the borrower is a corporate and the default exceeds ₹1 crore, a Section 7 IBC application before the NCLT is a powerful pressure lever. The recovery certificate itself is a sufficient basis for the application per Kotak v. Balakrishnan. Admission triggers the moratorium, freezing all enforcement and forcing collective resolution. Use this when piecemeal recovery has stalled, or when the entity is genuinely insolvent.

Five questions before filing

  1. Is the claim within "debt" under section 2(g)? Anything sounding in liability inclusive of interest from any business activity will qualify.
  2. Is the principal claim at least ₹20 lakh? Below the threshold, the DRT is closed.
  3. Have you computed limitation correctly? Apply Article 137 of the Limitation Act, and check for acknowledgements under sections 18 and 19.
  4. Have you identified the correct DRT under Section 19(1)? The branch-account ground is usually the cleanest.
  5. Are you exhausting SARFAESI first or in parallel? Do not abandon SARFAESI just because the DRT is faster; the collateral is your best asset.
